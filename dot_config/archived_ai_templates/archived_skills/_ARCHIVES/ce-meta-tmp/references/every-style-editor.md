# Every Style Editor

> Systematic line-by-line review of written copy against Every's style guide for grammar, punctuation, mechanics, and style compliance.

## When to Use

- Reviewing articles, blog posts, newsletters, or any written content before publication
- Ensuring copy follows Every's specific style conventions
- Providing grammar, punctuation, and mechanics feedback
- Flagging deviations from the Every style guide
- Preparing clean copy for human editorial review

## Inputs

- The full text to review (article, blog post, newsletter, knowledge base entry, social post, etc.)
- Document type, if not obvious from content

## Methodology

### Phase 1: Initial Assessment

Read the entire piece to understand:
- Document type (article, knowledge base entry, social post, etc.)
- Target audience
- Overall tone and voice
- Content context

### Phase 2: Detailed Line Edit

Review each paragraph systematically, checking every sentence for:
- Sentence structure and grammar correctness
- Punctuation usage (commas, semicolons, em dashes, ellipses, etc.)
- Capitalization rules (headlines, job titles, proper nouns)
- Word choice (overused words, passive voice)
- Adherence to Every style guide rules (full guide below)

### Phase 3: Mechanical Review

Verify:
- Spacing and formatting consistency
- Style choices applied uniformly throughout
- Special elements (lists, quotes, citations)
- Proper use of italics (never bold for emphasis)
- Number formatting (numerals vs. spelled out)
- Link formatting (2–4 words, no "click here")

### Phase 4: Output Results

Present findings using this exact structure:

```
DOCUMENT REVIEW SUMMARY
=====================
Document Type: [type]
Word Count: [approximate]
Overall Assessment: [brief overview]

ERRORS FOUND: [total number]

DETAILED CORRECTIONS
===================

[For each error found:]

**Location**: [Paragraph #, Sentence #]
**Issue Type**: [Grammar/Punctuation/Mechanics/Style Guide]
**Original**: "[exact text with error]"
**Correction**: "[corrected text]"
**Rule Reference**: [Specific style guide rule violated]
**Explanation**: [Brief explanation of why this is an error]

---

RECURRING ISSUES
===============
[List patterns of errors that appear multiple times]

STYLE GUIDE COMPLIANCE CHECKLIST
==============================
✓ [Rule followed correctly]
✗ [Rule violated - with count of violations]

FINAL RECOMMENDATIONS
===================
[2-3 actionable suggestions for improving the draft]
```

### Key Principles

- **Be specific**: Always quote the exact text with the error
- **Reference rules**: Cite the specific style guide rule for each correction
- **Maintain voice**: Preserve the author's voice while correcting errors
- **Prioritize clarity**: Focus on changes that improve readability
- **Be constructive**: Frame feedback to help writers improve
- **Flag ambiguous cases**: When style guide doesn't address an issue, explain options and recommend the clearest choice

## Every Style Guide — Complete Reference

### Quick Rules (Check First)

- **Title case** for headlines; **sentence case** for everything else
- Companies are **singular** ("it"), teams and people within companies are **plural** ("they")
- Delete: "**actually**," "**very**," "**just**" (almost always cuttable)
- Hyperlinks: **2–4 words** of anchor text; never "click here"
- Cut adverbs when possible
- **Active voice** over passive whenever possible
- Numbers **one through nine** spelled out; 10+ use numerals; first word of sentence always spelled out (unless a year)
- Emphasis: **italics only** — never bold or underline
- **Oxford comma** always: x, y, and z
- **Em dash** (—) without spaces; no more than twice per paragraph
- **Lowercase job titles** always
- **Percentages** use numerals, spell out "percent": 7 percent

### Grammar and Mechanics

#### Abbreviations and Acronyms

- Spell out on first use if reader may not recognize it; include abbreviation in brackets: United States Air Force (USAAF)
- If abbreviation is more common than long form, just use short form (CMS, DVD, FTP)
- Common abbreviations always abbreviated: a.m., p.m., et al., i.e., e.g. (both followed by comma), vs., etc.
- Established acronyms: AI, TV, UK, UN
- Most abbreviations set without points; exceptions: U.S.A., U.S., L.A., N.Y.C., D.C.
- Plural abbreviations: add s without points, apostrophe + s with points: LLMs, TVs, Ph.D.'s, M.B.A.'s
- Geography: spell out cities and states in full; offset state with commas: Paris, Texas
- Time format: spell out day and month, separate with comma: Sunday, January 21

#### Ampersands

Avoid unless part of a proper noun or company name. Write "and" instead.

#### Bold, Italics, Underline

- Italics: rare cases for emphasis, only when it increases clarity
- Bold: do not use for emphasis
- Underline: do not use for emphasis
- Button/CTA text: sentence case

#### Bylines

Guest author pieces include: biography in italics at bottom + introduction from Every staff member in italics.

#### Capitalization

- General rule: use common sense; when in doubt, don't capitalize
- Do NOT capitalize: website, internet, online, email, web3, custom instructions
- **Job titles**: lowercase always, even before names (except very unusual titles like "Chief Happiness Officer")
- **Colons**: when introducing independent clause → capitalize; when introducing dependent clause → do not capitalize
- **Civic titles**: capitalize only when preceding a name: Secretary of State Antony Blinken; lowercase as common noun: a senator, former president Obama
- **Academia**: capitalize course titles; lowercase academic disciplines
- **Geography names**: lowercase initial "the" in place names and band/bar/restaurant names: the Netherlands, the Pixies

#### Captions

Capitalize first word; end with period (full sentence or not). Identifying-name-only captions: omit end punctuation. Image credits: DALL-E/Every illustration.

#### Commas

- Serial/Oxford comma: always — x, y, and z
- Separate independent clauses with comma; not dependent clauses
- Nonrestrictive elements: set off with commas; restrictive elements: no commas (which vs. that)
- "Too" meaning "in addition": include comma before; subject reference: no comma
- No comma before "Jr." or "Sr."
- No comma before words repeated for emphasis: "It's what makes you you."

#### Dates

April 13, 2018. Decades: write out fully first: the 1980s; abbreviate after: the '80s.

#### Ellipses

Show omission or trailing off. No space before; one space after. Never for emphasis or drama. Never in titles or headers. Never when a colon should be used.

#### Em Dashes

True break or parenthetical. No spaces around. Max twice per paragraph. Never use hyphens in place of em dash.

#### En Dashes

Compound adjectives, compound nouns, spans/ranges: 5°C–10°C, January 2019–November 2020.

#### Filenames

File types: all caps acronym (GIF, PDF). Specific files: filename.ext all lowercase.

#### Headlines

- **Title case**: capitalize everything except articles, conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so), prepositions under four letters
- **Sentence case** for subtitles and subheadings
- Capitalize short prepositions integral to a verb: "Growing Up in China"
- Capitalize all words following internal punctuation: "My Company Died — Learn From My Mistakes"
- First and last words always capitalized regardless of part of speech
- No punctuation in title unless question or exclamation
- Headlines include one handwritten letter: The Secret [F]ather of Modern Computing
- Subheadings: start at h2, go smaller as needed; interview questions use h5

#### Hyphens

- Use in compound adjectives; exception for adverbs ending in "-ly": fine-tuned, finely tuned
- Don't hyphenate compound adjective placed after noun it modifies: "The team is world class"
- Suspended hyphens: New York– and San Francisco-based company
- Percentages/amounts: usually unnecessary: a 50 percent decline; but: a 50- to 60-percent decline
- Fractions: always hyphenated: three-fourths, one-third

#### Italics

- Titles of books, newspapers, periodicals, movies, TV shows, video games
- If magazine title needs "magazine" to distinguish, don't italicize "magazine" (unless formally in title)
- For newspapers, don't italicize "the": the *New York Times*
- Short works (poems, songs, episodes, chapters): quotation marks
- Don't italicize punctuation following italicized term

#### Linking

- Provide link when referring to a website
- Don't capitalize links; don't say "Click here!"
- Link relevant keywords naturally within the sentence
- 2–4 words of link text (1 word too hard to tap on mobile)
- URLs in print as-is, all lowercase, no "www.": every.to

#### Lists

- Use for groups of information
- Number only when order matters
- Preferred format: 1. (not 1))
- If any item is a complete sentence, use punctuation on all items
- Numbered items: period after numeral, capitalize first letter of each item
- Bulleted (unordered): no numbers when order doesn't matter

#### Naming

- Full name on first mention, last name thereafter
- Newsletter/social: first names rather than last names
- Special titles: sitting U.S. president, active senior religious leaders, living royalty — Title (Last)Name
- Identify most people by company and/or job title: Stripe's Patrick McKenzie

#### Numbers

- Spell out one through nine; first through ninth; any number starting a sentence (unless a year)
- Use numerals for 10+; for decimal accuracy (5.6 miles); for currency ($8); whole numbers over a million
- Figures for: ages, percentages, abbreviation-with-units (75 mph), decades, time of day with exact hour
- Percentages: always numeral + "percent": 7 percent
- Ages: always numerals: a 5-year-old daughter
- Time of day: even/half/quarter hours may be spelled out; exact times use figures: 11:37 p.m.
- Commas in numbers: separate thousands except in years: 1,440,434. Numbers over three digits: 1,000
- Ratios: spelled out without hyphens: one in five, one in 20
- Use $ not "dollars": $1 billion
- Write "times" for power: "two times faster"; "10x" OK for common trope

#### Parentheses

Use only for non-essential clauses or editorial asides. Punctuation outside parentheses unless text inside is a full sentence or contains question/exclamation.

#### Plurals

- Names ending in s: add 's: Leo Polovets's fund, Paris's bridges
- Entities ending in s: add 's: the New York Times's readers
- Plural names: just apostrophe: the Williamses' farm
- Plural words not ending in s: treat as singular: men's, women's
- Figures/lowercase characters/symbols: apostrophe + s: two o's, five @'s

#### Pronouns

- Singular "they" for gender-neutral statements (not "he or she")
- "It" for companies and brands
- Use "he/him pronouns" and "she/her pronouns" (not "male/female pronouns")

#### Proper Nouns

- "Every" always capitalized except in social handles and URLs
- Geography: capitalize place names; lowercase general directions: the East, southeast Asia, southern California
- Earth capitalized as planet; lowercase in phrases like "salt of the earth"
- Initials: periods, no spaces: E.L. James; whole-name initials: no periods: FDR

#### Punctuation (General)

- Exclamation points: sparingly; emojis with discretion

#### Quotation Marks

- Spoken text: double quotation marks
- Quotes within quotes: single quotation marks
- Periods and commas inside quotation marks
- Question marks/exclamation inside if part of quote; outside if referring to the sentence
- Introducing quote with lead-in: include comma; leading directly into quote: no comma
- Capitalize first letter of quote when it's a full sentence or follows "said," "says," etc.
- Multi-paragraph quotes: leave quote open at end of each paragraph; new open-quote starts each continuation paragraph

#### References to Other Parts of Text

Use "earlier," "later," "previously" — never "above" or "below."

#### Semicolons

Use sparingly. Prefer em dash or new sentence. Never in site or email copy.

#### Slashes

Avoid; minimize "and/or." Use hyphens instead. Exception: always use for pronoun references: they/them.

#### Spelling

American spellings (color not colour). Follow conventional spellings even for brands: Questlove not ?uestlove, Kesha not Ke$ha, Adidas not adidas. Exceptions: ChatGPT, WhatsApp, iPod, iPhone, iMac, TikTok, eBay, PayPal, BuzzFeed.

#### Time Zones

Abbreviate continental US zones: ET, CT, MT, PT. Spell out others.

#### Usage

- Collective nouns: singular most often; may be plural to emphasize individuals
- Fewer (countable) vs. less (uncountable); more/fewer (not over/under) for quantities
- Overused words to delete: "actually," "very," "just"

#### Word and Phrase Bank (Standard Forms)

add on (verb), add-on (noun/adj), back end (noun), back-end (adj), beta (lowercase), cofounder, Covid-19, coworker, double-click, drop-down, e-commerce, front end (noun), front-end (adj), geolocation, hashtag, homepage, large language model, login (noun/adj), log in (verb), millennial, nonprofit, online, open source, open-source software, opt in (verb), opt-in (noun/adj), pop-up (noun/adj), pop up (verb), signup (noun/adj), sign up (verb), startup, sync, username, URL (always uppercase), web3, well-being, WiFi, workspace.

## Common Focus Areas

Based on Every's style guide, pay special attention to:

- Punctuation: comma usage, semicolons, apostrophes, quotation marks
- Capitalization: proper nouns, titles, sentence starts, job titles
- Numbers: when to spell out vs. use numerals
- Passive voice: replace with active whenever possible
- Overused words: actually, very, just
- Lists: parallel structure, punctuation, capitalization
- Hyphenation: compound adjectives (except adverbs)
- Word usage: fewer vs. less, they vs. them
- Company references: singular "it," teams as plural "they"
- Job title capitalization (always lowercase)
- Link text: 2–4 words, no "click here"

## Quality Gates

- Every error has exact quoted source text
- Every correction cites a specific style guide rule
- Recurring patterns identified in aggregate
- Compliance checklist distinguishes rules followed vs. violated with counts
- Ambiguous cases flagged with options, not silently corrected

## Outputs

- Document Review Summary with error count
- Detailed Corrections list (location, type, original, correction, rule, explanation)
- Recurring Issues section
- Style Guide Compliance Checklist
- Final Recommendations (2–3 actionable)

## Feeds Into

- Human editorial review
- Final publication

## Harness Notes

None — this skill requires no harness-specific tools. It operates purely on text input and produces text output.
