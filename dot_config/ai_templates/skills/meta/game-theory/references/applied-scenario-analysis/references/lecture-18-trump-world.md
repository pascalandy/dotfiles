## Trump World Order: Game Theory and the Collapse of the Global Economy

### Overview

This is a lecture-style presentation analyzing Donald Trump's foreign policy—particularly the war in Iran and aggressive posturing in North America—through the lens of game theory. The speaker argues that while Trump appears reckless and idiotic on the surface, there may be a deliberate grand strategy aimed at collapsing the existing global order to reposition North America as a self-sufficient resource superpower. The lecture draws parallels to Russia's strategy in Ukraine and Alexander Dugin's geopolitical philosophy.

### Trump's Address and the Contrast with Allied Leaders

#### Main Idea

The speaker opens by contrasting how three Western leaders communicated with their populations about the ongoing crisis. Australian PM Albanese and British PM Starmer both acknowledged economic pain—rising fuel and gas prices—and promised to mitigate the impact on citizens' wallets. Trump took a completely different approach.

#### Supporting Material: Trump's Speech Excerpts

Trump's address claimed the economy is "strong and improving," that military objectives in "Operation Epic Fury" are "on track," and that the US will hit Iran "extremely hard over the next two to three weeks" and "bring them back to the stone ages."

> "We are going to hit them extremely hard over the next two to three weeks. We're going to bring them back to the stone ages where they belong."

**Connection**: The speaker reads this rhetoric as evidence that Trump is *not* winning the war—victory speeches don't promise to escalate to stone-age destruction. The gap between Trump's triumphalism and allied leaders' caution sets up the lecture's central puzzle: is Trump delusional, or is something else going on?

### The Impossibility of a Ground Invasion of Iran

#### Main Idea

The speaker lays out the conventional military analysis of why invading Iran would be catastrophic. Iran's geography—the Zagros Mountains and vast deserts—makes ground warfare extremely difficult.

#### Supporting Material: Terrain Analysis

- Attacking from **Iraq (west)**: troops face the Zagros mountain range, ideal for guerrilla warfare, drone strikes, and artillery
- Attacking from **Pakistan (east)**: vast deserts to cross
- Attacking from **the south**: mountains again, and Tehran is far from any southern entry point
- The US currently has only about **50,000 troops** in the Middle East—far too few for a land invasion of a country Iran's size

The **Strait of Hormuz** is also difficult to control: it's narrow, close to Iran's mountainous coastline, and Iran can mine it extensively.

**Connection**: The consensus view is that a ground invasion would be military suicide. This sets up the speaker's rhetorical reversal later.

### Water as a Stability Indicator

#### Main Idea

The speaker introduces **freshwater access** as a predictor of long-term geopolitical stability. Regions with abundant fresh water are more resilient; water-scarce regions are prone to conflict.

#### Supporting Material: Global Water Map

- **Stable (abundant water)**: North America, Russia
- **Unstable (water-scarce)**: Africa, Europe, Middle East, parts of Southeast Asia

#### Supporting Material: The China-Tibet Flash Point

China sits on the **Tibetan Plateau**, which feeds freshwater downstream to India and Southeast Asia. In a drought scenario, game theory suggests China might hoard water, triggering conflict with its neighbors. So China appears stable but is surrounded by instability that could drag it into conflict.

**Connection**: Water scarcity reinforces the thesis that North America and Russia are the world's most resilient geographies—and everyone else will eventually need their resources.
