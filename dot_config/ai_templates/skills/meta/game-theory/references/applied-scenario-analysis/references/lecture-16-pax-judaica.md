## How This War Will End — Lecture Notes

### Overview

This lecture analyzes the ongoing US-Iran war through four dimensions of warfare (narrative, political, economic, military), argues that the US is losing due to structural problems rooted in the military-industrial complex, and presents a speculative framework in which Israel is "auditioning" to replace the American empire as the muscle behind the global system. The lecturer draws on historical patterns of mercenary-to-empire transitions and game theory to sketch a post-war Middle East divided between two regional powers: Israel and Iran.

### Setting the Stage: How US Leadership Frames the War

#### Main Idea

The lecture opens with a series of video clips from senior US officials, each revealing a different institutional perspective on the war. The lecturer uses these clips to show that the US entered this war without anticipating serious resistance and is now struggling to reconcile its expectations with reality.

#### Supporting Material: Donald Trump on the War

Trump acknowledges that Iran fought back—hitting Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, and Kuwait—and that this was unexpected. He emphasizes the US has destroyed many missile and drone factories.

**Connection**: The lecturer interprets this as an admission that the US plan was **decapitation followed by surrender**, and that the plan failed because the enemy actually fought back. The US closed the Strait of Hormuz scenario was not anticipated.

#### Supporting Material: Karoline Leavitt (White House Press Secretary)

Leavitt frames the situation as Iran failing to "accept the reality" that it has been defeated militarily. She warns that Trump "does not bluff" and is "prepared to unleash hell."

**Connection**: The lecturer reads this as the administration projecting its own confusion onto the enemy—claiming Iran doesn't know it's been defeated, rather than reconsidering whether Iran actually has been defeated.

> "President Trump does not bluff, and he is prepared to unleash hell."

#### Supporting Material: Pete Hegseth (Secretary of War / Pentagon)

Hegseth describes the air campaign as "one for the history books" and says the US "negotiates with bombs." He frames the war as an overwhelming success.

**Connection**: The lecturer highlights a disconnect—US media reports Trump is seeking an off-ramp, but the Pentagon insists on military dominance. This reveals internal tension between political and military leadership.

> "We negotiate with bombs."

#### Supporting Material: Scott Bessent (Secretary of the Treasury)

Bessent explains the strategy of unsanctioning Russian and Iranian oil to stabilize global supply after Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz. He frames this as a brilliant premeditated plan—releasing ~130 million barrels of Russian oil on the water and potentially ~140 million barrels of Iranian oil.

**Connection**: The lecturer points out the irony sharply. By unsanctioning Iranian oil, the US enabled Iran to earn approximately $14 billion quickly—more than Iran's entire annual military budget of $10 billion. The lecturer frames this as the US enriching its own enemy while claiming strategic genius.

#### Supporting Material: Larry Fink (BlackRock CEO)

In a BBC interview, Fink argues the war cannot be stopped now because doing so would mean years of oil above $100–$150/barrel and Iranian control of the Strait of Hormuz, leading to global recession.

**Connection**: The lecturer notes Fink doesn't address the counterfactual—if the war hadn't started, Iran would have had no pretext to close the Strait. Fink's logic is: the war created the problem, therefore the war must continue. The lecturer sees this as the financial establishment locking itself into escalation.

#### Supporting Material: Drone Footage Over US Base

A drone launched by an Iranian proxy in Iraq flies unchallenged over a US base, spots two American helicopters, and destroys one.

**Connection**: This demonstrates that US bases across the Middle East are vulnerable to cheap drones and cannot be adequately defended, undermining the premise of US military dominance in the region.

### The Four Dimensions of War and Two Opposing Strategies

#### Main Idea

War is fought across four dimensions: **narrative** (global opinion, historical perception), **political** (allies, domestic base), **economic**, and **military**. The US and Iran are using fundamentally opposite strategies across these dimensions.

#### The American Strategy: Military-First, World Must Conform

The US approach is to set military strategy first—**decapitation forcing surrender**—and then force the narrative, political, and economic dimensions to support it:

- **Political**: Trump ordered NATO to open the Strait of Hormuz. He ignores domestic unpopularity of the war.
- **Economic**: Unsanctioning Russian and Iranian oil to keep prices low—which benefits enemy economies.
- **Narrative**: Trump has threatened journalists with imprisonment if they don't report the war favorably.

#### The Iranian Strategy: Military Serves the Other Dimensions

Iran uses military action strategically to benefit its economy, politics, and narrative:

- **Economic**: Military controls the Strait of Hormuz selectively—letting Chinese ships (main oil buyers) pass, as well as cooperative nations like Japan, Qatar, and Oman. Iran is now charging tolls.
- **Political**: Trying to split the GCC—knowing Saudi Arabia and UAE support regime change, but Qatar and Oman are more sympathetic to Iran.
- **Narrative**: Shaping global opinion to position Iran on "the right side of history." The lecturer notes many Americans are rooting for Iran.

#### Three Strategic Consequences of These Approaches

1. **Reflection**: The US approach prevents honest self-assessment. When military strategy is supreme and everything else must conform, there's no room to ask "is this working?" Iran's approach allows the military to adapt to economic and political realities.

2. **Flexibility**: When the US military strategy isn't working, instead of adapting, they **double down**—accelerating bombing campaigns, potentially sending ground troops into Iran (which military analysts consider "countereffective or suicidal"). Iran can recalibrate.

3. **Resilience**: The US approach alienates domestic and international support, making it hard to replenish troops and equipment. Iran, by winning popular opinion and strengthening its economy through the war, can draw on more support, recruit foreign fighters, and sustain the effort longer.

### The Structure of the World: Empire, Finance, and Layers

#### Main Idea

The lecturer presents a model of global power structure, referencing a framework discussed in a previous class. This model is essential for understanding why the war's outcome matters beyond the Middle East.

#### The Layers

1. **Empire** (core): Provides the military muscle that shapes reality
2. **Finance**: City of London, Wall Street, Bank for International Settlements, asset managers—these are the "game masters" who set the rules
3. **Global Economy**: Built on the US dollar, created by the empire-finance nexus
4. **Multilateral Organizations**: UN, WTO, etc.—the "rules-based international order" that makes the system appear fair and legitimate, hiding the empire and finance behind it
5. **Culture, Education, Media**: Justifies the system, creates legal infrastructure and values

#### Above the System

Organizations that sit above nation-states to benefit from and extract rent from the system:

- Intelligence, crime, and science
- Behind these: **transnational capital**, **secret societies**, **elite families**
- Bound together by a concept the lecturer calls **the occult** (to be studied later)

#### The Status Quo Problem

Everyone who benefits from this system wants to maintain the **status quo**. But if the empire at the core is failing (losing the war in Iran), the entire edifice is threatened. The solution, according to this framework: **replace the failing empire with a new one**.

The lecturer argues this replacement is Israel—a concept he calls **Pax Judaica**.

> "On the surface, it seems like this war is between the United States and Iran. But in reality, what this war really is about is a war between the United States and Israel."

### Why the American Empire Is Falling

#### Main Idea: The Military-Industrial Complex

America historically did not have a professional standing army—the founding fathers saw it as a threat to liberty. World Wars I and II forced the creation of one. After WWII, the apparatus remained and needed justification to maintain access to unlimited government funds. This produced the Cold War, then the War on Terror.

> "The point is not to have successful wars. The point is not to win wars. The point is never-ending wars, which allows the military industrial complex to transfer American taxpayer money to a transnational elite." — Julian Assange

#### Supporting Material: Military Spending Statistics

- US accounts for **41%** of all global military spending
- China: 8.2%
- Russia: 4.1% (one-tenth of US spending)

**Connection**: Despite massive spending, the lecturer argues the US military is "probably the most corrupt institution in the world."

#### Supporting Material: Pentagon Accounting

On September 10, 2001 (one day before 9/11), Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld announced that **$2 trillion was unaccounted for** in Pentagon books.

#### Supporting Material: Defense Contractor Economics

Using Boeing as an example:

- Received $20 billion in government contracts in one year
- Spent $16 million on lobbying and $2 million on political contributions (characterized as bribes)
- Extremely profitable return on political investment
- Meanwhile, Boeing's civilian aircraft (737 Max) have had engineering failures and recalls
- Whistleblowers who tried to testify against Boeing died under suspicious circumstances

#### Supporting Material: The $103 Million Theft

A contractor stole $103 million from the Army without anyone in the Army noticing. The theft was only discovered when the IRS flagged her unusual income. The lecturer argues this suggests theft in the military is routine.

### Three Constraints on the US War Effort

#### Main Idea

The US faces three structural problems that make winning in Iran unlikely:

1. **Lack of Political Will**: Only 40% of Americans support the war. Trump has requested $200 billion in funding. A draft is possible but deeply unpopular. Without public support, funding and manpower are constrained.

2. **Lack of Manufacturing Capacity**: The US cannot quickly replenish lost planes, bombs, and equipment. If the war drags on, America literally runs out of ammunition.

3. **Unwillingness to Sustain Casualties**: The Pentagon is afraid to report deaths because of political backlash—even though casualties are inherent to war. The US sent approximately 5,000 marines to Iran, which the lecturer sees as insufficient and indicative of a lack of strategic seriousness.

### Israel's Audition to Be the New Empire

#### Main Idea

Israel is demonstrating to the global elite that it possesses what America lacks: **unity**, **capacity** (or at least resourcefulness), and **determination**. The lecturer frames this as Israel "auditioning" for the role of empire.

#### Supporting Material: The Venture Capital Analogy

A thought experiment: you're a venture capitalist choosing between two companies.

- **Company A**: Has 100 profitable companies, lots of experience and resources. But employees don't care if the new venture succeeds—they can afford to lose.
- **Company B**: Young founders who've failed before but will work 16 hours a day and go bankrupt (or worse) if it fails. Total commitment.

**Connection**: Smart investors choose Company B because total commitment produces better outcomes than complacent resources. America is Company A. Israel is Company B.

#### Supporting Material: US Military Hardware Failures

- **Patriot Missile System**: Supposed to protect GCC, Israel, and US bases from Iranian drones and missiles. "Doesn't really work." Iranian drones fly freely across the Middle East.
- **F-35 Fighter Jet**: Cost $100 million each, took 26 years to develop. Supposed to be undetectable by radar. Iran has shot down at least one using low-frequency radar. The lecturer argues the 26-year development timeline reflects corruption, not engineering complexity.
- **USS Gerald Ford**: Cost $13 billion (more than Iran's entire annual defense budget). Entered the war theater and had to withdraw after three weeks. Three competing explanations: (1) laundry room fire that spread, (2) hit by an Iranian missile, (3) Pentagon recognized its radar and guidance systems were inadequate for actual combat. The lecturer notes all three explanations are damning.

#### Supporting Material: Israel's Demonstrations of Capability

- **Gaza**: The lecturer acknowledges war crimes and global outrage but frames it as "proof of concept" for the global elite—Israel showing willingness to do what it takes. **82% of Israeli public** supports expelling all Palestinians from Gaza. **66%** believe the war is existential (invoking Amalek). This is the unity and determination America lacks.

- **Lebanon Pager Attack (2024)**: Israel implanted bombs in pagers distributed to Hezbollah members. Cost: $275 million. Took decades to implement. Limited military impact but massive psychological impact—paranoia within Hezbollah, global respect for Israeli intelligence capabilities. The lecturer emphasizes Israel is advertising this: "We'll do it cheaply."

- **ISIS**: The lecturer presents the claim that ISIS is a Mossad operation, noting ISIS attacked everywhere in the Middle East except Israel. Stories of ISIS commanders arrested and found to be Mossad agents. The argument: Israel is showing the global elite it can control the region through infiltration and proxy manipulation rather than expensive brute-force bombing.

> "We don't have to use the American strategy of just bombing everyone to death. That's expensive. That's brutal. That's not very strategic."

### Historical Pattern: Mercenaries Replacing Empires

#### Main Idea

Israel's trajectory follows a recurring historical pattern where mercenaries eventually replace the empires they serve. The dynamic: as the empire grows old and decadent, it outsources violence. The mercenaries eventually realize they're doing all the work and take over.

#### Supporting Material: Historical Precedents

- Romans started as mercenaries for the Etruscans in Italy
- Aztecs started as mercenaries for the Colhuacan in Mexico
- Greeks started as mercenaries for the Persians
- Mongols started as mercenaries for the Chinese
- Akkadians started as mercenaries for the Sumerians
- Mamluks started as mercenaries for the Ayyubid dynasty in Egypt

**Connection**: In every case, the mercenaries eventually replaced the empire. Israel was created by the British and Americans to be their "pit bull" and "aircraft carrier" in the Middle East—creating instability that allowed Anglo-American control of oil. Now Israel is asking: "We're the ones doing all the work... why shouldn't we be the empire?"

### What Pax Judaica Looks Like

#### Main Idea

The post-war order would see Israel controlling Middle Eastern resources and positioning itself as the hub of global trade and AI surveillance.

#### Components

- **Oil and Gas**: Control of ~20% of world oil. Israel positioned at the center of Middle Eastern gas fields and trade routes, shipping resources to Europe and East Asia.

- **Data Centers and AI**: Israel has the most data centers in the Middle East (vs. Saudi Arabia's 36 and UAE's 34). Unlike Saudi Arabia and UAE, Israel doesn't need to import expertise—it has the universities, IT talent, and human capital. The lecturer argues Israel will absorb the region's AI infrastructure to build a surveillance backbone for the empire.

- **India-Middle East-Europe Trade Corridor (IMEC)**: For India to reach Europe, the route passes through Israel. This corridor also connects to Africa and potentially to Ukraine/Russia (energy and food transported to Africa).

> "Israel, because of its location, can really be the epicenter of all global trade."

### Iran's Post-War Position

#### Main Idea

Paradoxically, the war has strengthened Iran economically by lifting de facto sanctions. Iran is now embedded in the global economy rather than cut off from it.

> "Trump and Israel's war has ended up delivering Iran de facto sanctions relief." — Trita Parsi

The lecturer notes Iran is now **less incentivized to end the war** because it benefits economically from its continuation.

#### Iran's Trade Networks

1. **North-South Transport Corridor**: Links Russia and Iran, giving Russia access to India/Asia and Iran access to Europe.
2. **Belt and Road Initiative**: China's route to Europe passes through Iran. Iran sits at the epicenter of this network.

### Three Rules of Geopolitics (Game Theory)

#### Main Idea

The lecturer presents three principles for understanding the post-war order:

1. **The strong respect each other and prey on the weak.** If both Iran and Israel prove strong while the GCC and US prove weak, Iran and Israel will eventually partner and turn on the weak—despite being ideological enemies.

2. **Current alliances will shift.** Right now it's Israel + GCC + US vs. Iran. But if Iran proves strong, Israel will abandon the GCC and US to work with Iran on a new Middle Eastern order.

3. **The weak do not work well together.** The GCC countries (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, Oman, Kuwait, Qatar) could theoretically combine resources to compete, but weak players don't cooperate effectively. Instead, they seek protection from the strong. As the US falls, GCC states will split—some (Qatar, Oman) toward Iran, others (Saudi Arabia, UAE) toward Israel. Either way, the GCC ceases to be a major geopolitical factor.

### Key Takeaways

- The US entered the Iran war expecting quick decapitation and surrender; it did not anticipate sustained resistance
- The US forces all dimensions of warfare to serve its military strategy; Iran does the reverse—using military action to serve economic, political, and narrative goals
- The military-industrial complex optimizes for profit extraction, not military effectiveness, producing expensive systems that underperform in actual combat
- Three constraints limit the US: lack of political will, lack of manufacturing capacity, and unwillingness to sustain casualties
- Israel is positioning itself as a replacement empire by demonstrating unity, resourcefulness, and determination
- Historical pattern: mercenaries replace the empires they serve
- The Greater Israel project's real obstacle is American military presence, not Iran
- The war has paradoxically strengthened Iran's economy through de facto sanctions relief
- Game theory suggests post-war alliances will be determined by who proves strong, not by current ideological alignments
