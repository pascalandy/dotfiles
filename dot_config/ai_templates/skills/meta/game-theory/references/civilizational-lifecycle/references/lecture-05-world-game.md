## Why Nations Rise and Fall — A Game Theory Perspective

### Overview

This lecture examines why empires and nations rise and fall throughout history, arguing that conventional metrics (population, resources, technology) fail to predict which states will dominate. Drawing on Ibn Khaldun's concept of *asabiyyah* and game theory, the lecturer proposes three alternative metrics — energy, openness, and cohesion — and traces how the "games" societies play evolve through predictable stages, from cooperation to factional struggle to conquest by marginalized outsiders.

---

### The Puzzle: Why Do the Weakest States Win?

#### Main Idea

Conventional analysis of geopolitical competition focuses on measurable advantages: population size, access to resources (farmland, rivers, trade routes), and technological sophistication. The lecturer argues these metrics consistently fail to predict which state will dominate a region.

#### Supporting Material: The Chinese Warring States Period (~250 BCE)

Multiple powerful states existed — the Chu, the Zhao, the Wei — each with strong claims to dominance based on conventional metrics. A game-theoretic analysis using population, resources, and technology would predict perhaps two likely unifiers of China. The **Qin**, however, would never appear on that list:

- Located in mountains, making it poor
- Far from rivers and trade routes
- Geographically isolated

Yet it was the Qin that conquered all of China.

**Connection**: This case introduces the central paradox of the lecture — the state with the fewest conventional advantages repeatedly wins.

The lecturer then states this as a near-universal historical pattern:

> "If you look at most of human history, this pattern repeats itself. If you look at a region, the strongest nation does not come up on top. It's usually the weakest, most marginalized area that will eventually come and conquer the entire territory and create an empire."

---

### Ibn Khaldun and the Concept of Asabiyyah

#### Main Idea

**Ibn Khaldun**, a Muslim historian, proposed the concept of **asabiyyah** — meaning cohesion or group solidarity. His theory: people on the margins are poorer, but their poverty forces unity. They focus on solidarity and working together. Meanwhile, people in wealthy centers become individualistic, decadent, and corrupt. This is why marginalized tribes repeatedly conquer wealthy civilized areas.

The lecturer frames this as "almost like an iron law of history."

---

### The Three Metrics: Energy, Openness, and Cohesion

#### Main Idea

The lecturer proposes three metrics to measure how dynamic a society is and how likely it is to triumph:

- **Cohesion**: Do people see themselves as a team? Are they willing to sacrifice for each other? Do they function as a family? This follows directly from Ibn Khaldun's asabiyyah.
- **Openness**: How willing are you to adapt? How willing are you to accept limitations and be resilient? The lecturer equates openness with humility and resilience.
- **Energy**: Willingness to work hard, focus, have a clear goal, and be motivated to achieve it.

#### How Wealth Erodes All Three Metrics

When a society becomes rich, each metric degrades:

- **Low energy**: The elite don't want to work — they prefer to exploit others. The common people, enslaved or in debt, also have low energy. They're just "getting by," not building anything.
- **Low openness**: The elite become corrupt, insolent, and arrogant. They refuse to admit they're wrong. Society stagnates.
- **Low cohesion**: Wealth inequality and corruption atomize people. Individualism replaces solidarity.

This degradation creates the opening for a new, more dynamic group to conquer.

### Historical Pattern: Marginalized Groups Conquering Empires

The lecturer walks through multiple historical cases, each following the same pattern.

#### Supporting Material: The Greek City-States and Macedonia

The Greek city-states (Athens, Sparta, Thebes, Corinth, Argos) were small — Athens at its height had about 50,000 people. Athens had overwhelming advantages:

- Greatest navy in the Aegean
- Control of trade routes
- Wealthiest city-state
- Largest population, most innovation, most strategic location

Yet Athens declined after other city-states attacked it. The unifier was **Macedonia** — poor, isolated, uncultured, "uncivilized." But they had energy, openness, and cohesion.

After unifying Greece, the Macedonians under **Alexander the Great** did the "impossible" — they crossed the Aegean and conquered the entire Persian Empire (Egypt, Anatolia, Mesopotamia, Persia) in about ten years.

#### Supporting Material: The Rise of Rome

At the time of the Macedonian Empire, Rome was an unknown backwater. The Italian peninsula was being colonized by the Greeks. The Romans were "poor, backward, tribal people." Yet over time they conquered the Italian peninsula, then the Greek mainland, then Carthage, and created the Roman Empire.

#### Supporting Material: The Aztecs

In the 15th–16th century, the Mexican region was divided into warring city-states. The Aztecs arrived from the north as a poor, starving tribe looking for food. They were warrior people, hired as mercenaries by other city-states. When they became too powerful, they were isolated onto a marsh — expected to starve.

Instead, they turned the marsh into farmland through creativity and eventually conquered everyone, ruling over millions.

#### Supporting Material: The Spanish Conquistadors

Hernán Cortés arrived with approximately 500 conquistadors in 1519 and conquered the entire Aztec Empire in a very short time. The lecturer lists conventional explanations:

- Disease (Aztecs lacked immunity to European diseases)
- The Aztecs may have believed conquistadors were gods
- Local allies who resented Aztec rule

But he argues these don't give the "full picture" and that game theory asks: what exactly happened? What were the players' motivations, incentives, and strategies?

#### Supporting Material: The Sumerians and Akkadians

**Uruk** became the first great world city because it sat at the meeting place of the Euphrates and Tigris rivers — the center of all global trade, connecting the Persian Gulf (access to India/Indus Valley Civilization), Egypt, and Anatolia.

Uruk grew wealthy, set up colonies, and those colonies came into conflict (same warring-states pattern). The unifier was not one of the powerful city-states but the **Akkadians** — tribal mercenaries from an isolated region — who conquered everyone and built the first empire. Later, the Persians from the Zagros Mountains conquered them and created the Persian Empire.

**Connection across all examples**: The pattern is consistent — wealthy, established powers are conquered by poor, marginalized groups with greater energy, openness, and cohesion.

### The Stages of the Game: How Nations Evolve

#### Main Idea

As nations rise and fall, the "game" they play changes. Their motivations, incentives, and strategies shift through distinct stages.

#### Stage 1: Cooperation Through Religion

In the beginning, a people must come together to build a land where they can prosper. The game is **cooperation**, and the mechanism is **religion** — a dynamic belief system that motivates people to be energetic and gives them reason to work hard.

The people best able to articulate this religion become the elite: **poets and priests**.

> At this point in history, it's basically a startup. Everyone's enthusiastic. Everyone's working hard. Everyone feels as though they're making a positive contribution.

#### Stage 2: Hereditary Elite and Bureaucratic Religion

Over time, the elite become **hereditary**. The game shifts to: how do I pass my privileges to my children? The religion transforms from a source of energy and creativity into a **bureaucracy** — a rigid set of rules and hierarchy that must be obeyed.

#### Stage 3: Elite Overproduction and Factionalism

**Elite overproduction**: Privileges are passed to children, but there are too many children competing for status positions. Society fractures into **factions**, typically organized around princes (symbolic figureheads, not necessarily leaders of their factions).

The initial resolution: losing factions are **exiled** and must set up new colonies. This process continues until there's no more place to colonize.

#### Stage 4: The Warring States Period (Peak Creativity)

When exile is no longer possible, you get the **Warring States period** — and paradoxically, this is the time of maximum creativity. The mechanism is **open cooperative competition**:

- Different colonies compete against each other, requiring openness and innovation
- They also trade with each other, sharing best practices and innovations

> China's been around for five thousand years, but most of the innovations happened during the warring states period. This hundred years when we had Confucius, Mengzi, Laozi, Zhuangzi. It's basically everyone.

The same applies to the Greeks during the classical period — they "created everything that becomes a foundation for Western civilization."

#### Stage 5: Equilibrium Through Intermarriage

Eventually, city-state elites realize they can work together through **intermarriage**. This creates a new **equilibrium** where the elites are now above any single state.

Warfare transforms: it's no longer about innovation or conquest but becomes a mechanism of **population control** — a way to resolve elite overproduction. Too many people striving for too many status positions, so warfare thins the ranks.

The equilibrium effectively becomes the **empire**.

#### Stage 6: Court Politics and Factional Struggle

Once equilibrium is reached, innovation is no longer necessary — just maintaining the status quo. A rigid hierarchy forms, and the game becomes **court politics**: which faction reaches the top.

### Secret Societies: The Mechanics of Factional Struggle

#### Main Idea

Within the empire/equilibrium, factions must solve three problems to triumph: **secrecy**, **trust**, and **coordination**. The historical solution across civilizations is the **secret society**.

#### Problem 1: Secrecy → Hierarchy/Compartmentalization

Secret societies use internal hierarchies where only people at the top know the full picture. New members are initiated at the bottom and slowly climb the ladder.

#### Problem 2: Trust → Transgression

Trust is built through shared transgression — breaking the law together. If caught, everyone is punished, which binds members together.

The most basic form of transgression is **ritual sacrifice** — collectively capturing and killing someone through ritual.

#### Problem 3: Coordination → Eschatology

**Eschatology** is the mythology that gives the group purpose. Typically: serving a higher god who wants to conquer the world, with members as servants in this quest to create paradise.

> Different civilizations have different eschatologies, but the system is the same.

#### The Consequence: Insularity, Corruption, Division

Every faction forms its own secret society. The game of empire becomes a struggle between secret societies. Members stop caring about the empire or its enemies — they only care about their faction winning.

This produces three qualities:
- **Insular** (opposite of open)
- **Corrupt** (opposite of energetic) — stealing from the people to fund factional struggle
- **Divided** (opposite of cohesive)

### The Mercenary Mechanism: How Outsiders Get In

#### Main Idea

The factions within the empire **invite** marginalized border groups as mercenaries to help in their political struggles. This is the critical mechanism by which outsiders gain access.

Contact happens through three channels: **trade**, **diplomacy**, and **mercenary service**.

The borderland people benefit in three ways:
- Through **trade**, they become wealthier
- Through **banditry**, they become wealthier
- Through **mercenary service**, they learn the empire's best technology, fighting skills, and weapons

Eventually, the mercenaries take over — first merging with the factions, then killing everyone and seizing control.

#### Supporting Material: The Qin and the Macedonians

The lecturer argues that the Qin conquered the warring states because different factions of established states invited the Qin to help in their political struggles. The Qin then conquered them one by one.

> They don't teach you this in history class, but according to game theory, what happened was that different factions of these different states actually invited the Qin to help them in their political struggle.

The same pattern applies to the Macedonians — they helped one city-state, conquered it, moved to the next. There was always a faction within that city-state that invited them in the first place.

### The World Game: A Classroom Demonstration

#### Supporting Material: High School Simulation

The lecturer describes a classroom game played in Canadian high schools:

- Teams of 4–5 are assigned countries (USA, Britain, India, Pakistan, Japan)
- Each team receives an envelope with resources (paper, scissors, ruler, glue)
- Goal: become the wealthiest country by turning resources into commodities (triangles, circles, squares) sold to a "World Bank"
- USA gets everything; Pakistan gets nothing
- Teams can trade with each other

**Result**: USA finishes first (overwhelming resources), but **Pakistan finishes second** — the team that started with nothing.

> Why? Because they're forced to be the most energetic, the most open, and most cohesive. Team members have to go and beg to work for free. Other team members go and lie and cheat and steal. They're forced to be resourceful and creative.

**Connection**: This directly demonstrates the lecture's thesis — having the most resources doesn't determine who wins. What matters is energy, openness, and resourcefulness. And often, these qualities emerge precisely because of poverty.

> "Poverty leads to creativity."

### The STEM Workshop: Rankings Reverse

#### Supporting Material: Teacher Training Exercise

The lecturer ran two-day STEM workshops for teachers, dividing faculty into ~10 teams competing on assignments (build the fastest toy car, best airplane, catapult, etc.). Teams were ranked at the end of day one.

**Result on day two**: The rankings reversed. Team 10 (worst on day one) shot up to first place. Team 1 (best on day one) fell to the bottom.

**Explanation**: The best team never had to reflect or improve — they became arrogant and complacent. The worst team was "used to being laughed at" and constantly adapting, making them more likely to learn and build relationships.

#### Student Question: Does Team 1 Bounce Back on Day 3?

The lecturer's answer is emphatic: **No.**

> Once you're number one, you become so arrogant that it becomes almost impossible for you to be humble again.

> When societies fall, they just die off. It's possible a new group comes and pretends to be them, but they're actually a different people.

There is no historical example of an empire falling, its people changing their mentality, and then becoming the best again. A new group may adopt the name, but they are fundamentally different people.

#### The Chinese Joke

> In China, there's a joke. In school, there are two people that everyone hates: the best student and the worst student. Why do you hate the best student? Because the best student is gonna graduate and come back as your principal one day. Why do you hate the worst student? Because he's gonna graduate and come back and give you a lecture on how to become rich.

### Applying the Theory: Future Predictions

#### Main Idea

The lecturer applies the framework to predict which nations will become great empires, identifying three candidates based on their history of defeat and humiliation.

#### Prediction 1: Germany

Lost World War I and World War II. Was never a world empire. Lost millions of people. According to the theory, Germany will one day dominate at least Europe and Asia.

#### Prediction 2: Japan

Lost World War II. Suffered two nuclear bombings and devastating firebombing. Was destroyed and humiliated. The lecturer notes that today you can see how energetic, open, and cohesive Japanese society is — a direct result of World War II forcing national reflection.

> "They want vengeance, basically."

#### Prediction 3: Israel

The people of Israel believe they were persecuted for a thousand years. This shared experience of marginalization drives energy, openness, and cohesion.

#### Who Is "Done"?

The lecturer states bluntly: the Americans are "done," and China is "done."

He adds a provocative claim: **North Korea has a better future than China** in East Asia, based purely on the theory's logic.

> "Just because you're poor does not mean you're open, energetic, and cohesive. But if you want to go from being poor to becoming rich, you have to be open, energetic, and cohesive."

#### Caveat

The lecturer repeatedly emphasizes this is "all just a theory" and he cannot predict what will actually happen.

### Student Q&A: Key Clarifications

#### "But Japan and Germany are already wealthy — won't they become corrupt too?"

The lecturer's answer: No, because **wealth without power is not the same as wealth with power.** Power means being able to impose your game on others and extract rent. Japan and Germany became wealthy after WWII, but America was the game master.

- **Plaza Accord**: America forced Japan to destroy its own economy by stopping savings and overspending. Japan got rich but never had sovereignty.
- **Germany as vassal state**: America destroyed the Nord Stream pipeline. After Russia invaded Ukraine, America forced Germany to stop doing business with Russia (cutting off its energy supply) and China (cutting off its car export market). Germany's economy has been destroyed because it's a vassal state.
- Both countries were forced to buy US treasury bonds, funneling their wealth to America.

> "It seems as though Germany and Japan became very wealthy. They were not. They were just vassal states for America."

#### "What makes a poor society become energetic and cohesive versus staying poor?"

The answer: **A leader.** This could be a general (Genghis Khan unified the divided, poor Mongols), a poet (Homer), a thinker, a prophet (Zoroaster, Jesus), or a priest. One person unifies the imagination of a people into a cohesive group.

The lecturer then makes a striking claim about international development:

> "Why do we have organizations like the United Nations? Why do we have international development aid? The goal is to identify the possibility of a leader emerging and make sure he does not emerge."

> "If you left Africa alone, Africa will be a lot better. But because you have all these schools, all these NGOs, all these organizations in Africa, what they're really doing is ensuring that Africa is in a continuous state of development."

The implication: international development keeps poor nations dependent, preventing the emergence of unifying leaders who could challenge the existing order.

### Key Takeaways

- Conventional metrics (population, resources, technology) consistently fail to predict which states will dominate
- The three metrics that matter: **energy** (willingness to work hard), **openness** (humility, adaptability), **cohesion** (group solidarity, willingness to sacrifice)
- Wealth degrades all three metrics, creating vulnerability to conquest by marginalized outsiders
- Societies evolve through predictable stages: cooperation → hereditary elite → factionalism → warring states (peak creativity) → equilibrium/empire → court politics/secret societies → conquest by outsiders
- Factions within empires invite their own destroyers as mercenaries
- Once a society falls from dominance, it never recovers — a new group may take the name, but the people are different
- Poverty can force creativity, but being poor alone is not sufficient — a leader must emerge to unify the group

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Asabiyyah (عصبية)**: Cohesion or group solidarity; concept from Ibn Khaldun explaining why marginalized groups conquer wealthy civilizations
- **Elite overproduction**: When hereditary privileges create too many elites competing for limited status positions, fragmenting society into factions
- **Open cooperative competition**: The dynamic during warring-states periods where competing polities trade and share innovations while competing, producing peak creativity
- **Eschatology**: The mythology or grand narrative a secret society uses to coordinate its members and give them purpose
- **Plaza Accord**: Agreement where America forced Japan to devalue its currency and change economic policy, effectively destroying Japan's economic momentum
- **Transgression**: Shared law-breaking used within secret societies to build mutual trust among members
