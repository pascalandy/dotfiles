## Who Succeeds and Why — A Lecture on Success, Parenting, and Social Structure

### Overview

This lecture explores the psychology and sociology of success. It begins with three well-known psychological theories of individual success (self-control, resilience, deliberate practice), then systematically dismantles them by showing they are correlates, not causes, of success. The lecture traces the real drivers of success to parenting strategies shaped by socioeconomic class, and ultimately to the hierarchical structure of society itself — including why that structure periodically collapses through revolution.

---

### Three Psychological Theories of Success

#### Theory 1: Delayed Gratification (Walter Mischel)

**Walter Mischel**, a Columbia psychologist, devised the famous **marshmallow test**. The setup: a four or five-year-old child is brought into a room. An adult has a friendly conversation with the child, then says they need to leave briefly. A marshmallow is placed in front of the child. The deal — eat it now, or wait and get two marshmallows when the adult returns.

Mischel observed children through a one-way mirror as they struggled with the temptation. He then tracked these children for fifty years.

The findings: children who resisted the temptation and waited for the second marshmallow were much more likely to:

- Do better in school
- Score higher on tests
- Have better, more stable careers
- Get promoted
- Find stable relationships
- Avoid jail, drugs, and alcohol
- Be more lean and fit
- Live longer
- Have better teeth

Children who ate the marshmallow immediately showed the opposite pattern across all these outcomes.

For Mischel, success means **delayed gratification** — the capacity for long-term planning. A simpler way to say it: **self-control**, or **emotional regulation** — the ability to keep emotions in check, to calm yourself down when angry.

> "To succeed today, I will make sacrifices necessary."

The lecturer notes this is why schools focus on teaching emotion regulation and long-term planning.

---

#### Theory 2: Growth Mindset (Carol Dweck)

**Carol Dweck**, a Stanford psychologist, wrote the book *Mindset*. Her framework:

- **Growth mindset**: When you fail, you treat it as an opportunity to learn. You think about what went wrong and try harder next time.
- **Fixed mindset**: You cannot accept failure. You believe you're incapable of improving. You give up.

The core idea here is **resilience**. Those with a growth mindset persist through failure. Those with a fixed mindset quit.

---

#### Theory 3: Deliberate Practice (K. Anders Ericsson)

**K. Anders Ericsson**, a Swedish psychologist, studied why certain people succeed as musicians or athletes. He introduced the concept of **deliberate practice**.

The distinction: many people work hard — they practice every day for long hours. But people who succeed practice *strategically*. Their plan follows a cycle:

1. What are my goals?
2. How can I achieve these goals?
3. How do I improve my plan?

They examine their own practices, identify weaknesses, create improvement plans, and follow them. If the plan works, they stick with it. If it doesn't, they change it.

The core idea is **self-reflection** or **self-assessment** — constantly evaluating yourself as a learner and adjusting your strategies.

---

#### Supporting Evidence: The Dunning-Kruger Effect

Two American psychologists, **Dunning and Kruger**, ran an experiment with five hundred first-year psychology students. Each student took an IQ test, then was asked to estimate their ranking.

No one got it right. Those in the top five percent thought they were around the top twenty percent — because it felt easy, so they assumed it was easy for everyone. The dramatic finding: those who scored in the bottom five percent thought they were average (top fifty percent).

> "Those people who are stupid lack the capacity to know they're stupid."

The **Dunning-Kruger effect** demonstrates that accurate self-assessment is the hardest part of being a student. The least competent are often the most confident. The lecturer connects this directly to real-world power dynamics.

> "Often the people in power are stupid. They don't know they're stupid. They were confident, and they do stupid things like Donald Trump."

**Connection**: The Dunning-Kruger effect validates Ericsson's claim that self-assessment is critical — and shows why it's so rare.

---

### The Problem: Correlation Does Not Equal Causation

#### Main Idea

The lecturer presents a critical pivot. Schools have tried teaching self-control, resilience, and self-assessment to struggling students. It doesn't work. A bad student who is taught these skills doesn't actually improve.

The reason: **correlation does not equal causation**.

> "Just because things are correlated does not mean they cause each other."

The example given: successful people tend to get up at four in the morning. But getting up at four doesn't make you successful. Rather, if you're already successful, you're more motivated, so you get up early.

The same logic applies to all three theories:

- If you're successful → you develop self-control, resilience, and self-assessment
- Having self-control, resilience, and self-assessment → does NOT guarantee success

> "If you're rich, guess what happens? You become successful. And therefore, you will have growth mindset, self-control, deliberate practice."

The real question becomes: what actually determines who succeeds?

### The Real Drivers: Parenting Strategies of Rich vs. Poor

#### Main Idea

Macroeconomic studies show that school performance barely matters. If your parents are rich, you'll be successful. If your parents are poor, you won't. The lecturer identifies three major differences in how rich and poor parents raise their children.

#### Difference 1: Language and Communication

- **Rich parents**: Speak to their children more, use higher vocabulary, longer sentences
- **Poor parents**: Minimal communication — "No. Yes. Go away."

#### Difference 2: Attitude — Friendly vs. Authoritarian

The lecturer gives a concrete example: a child touches a hot stove and burns their hand.

- **Rich parent**: "Listen, you made a mistake. Don't worry about it. Let me explain to you why touching a fire is bad for you. Because you'll burn yourself, and you might have to go to a doctor, and we will feel pain if you hurt yourself." — Spends time explaining why it's wrong and how not to do it again.
- **Poor parent**: "Don't you ever do that again or I'll beat the crap out of you."

**Connection**: The rich child learns the world is safe and that they are respected. The poor child learns the world is scary and adults are to be feared.

This carries directly into school:

- Rich kid sees the teacher as a friend → smiles, hugs, builds rapport → teacher reciprocates
- Poor kid is afraid of the teacher → doesn't make eye contact, appears stressed → teacher perceives a "problem child"

The authoritarian approach is effective in the short term but creates stress and makes the child feel unsafe.

#### Difference 3: Stability vs. Volatility

- **Rich parents** have money, so they can keep promises. "Next week, we'll go to Thailand for vacation." → They go to Thailand.
- **Poor parents** can't reliably keep promises because money is always an issue. "Next week, we'll go to McDonald's." → Paycheck falls short. "Sorry, we can't go anymore."

Rich parents offer **stability**. Poor parents can only offer **volatility**.

### Reinterpreting the Three Theories Through the Lens of Poverty

#### The Marshmallow Test Reinterpreted

The marshmallow test is not a test of self-control. It's a test of **trust in authority figures**.

- If you believe the adult will come back and keep their promise → you wait
- If you believe the adult is lying → you eat the marshmallow now

For children raised with stability, waiting is rational. For children raised with volatility, eating the marshmallow immediately is the rational choice — because most of the time, the second marshmallow never comes.

> "It's not that poor kids are stupid. Poor kids are rational, and they're responding to the circumstances that they live in."

#### Resilience Reinterpreted

Resilience depends on believing the world will help you. Rich kids believe that if they fall, someone will catch them. Poor kids interpret failure as a signal to stop — "you probably shouldn't be doing this."

#### Self-Assessment Reinterpreted

Self-reflection requires emotional safety. A poor child living under constant stress who looks inward sees only pain and stress — not productive insight.

### Why Better Parenting Programs Don't Work Either

#### Main Idea

The logical next step — build schools that mimic good parenting (rich vocabulary, friendly teachers, stability) — has been tried. It's more effective than teaching self-control directly, but still doesn't truly work. The children arrive too late; their worldview is already formed.

The next logical step — change how parents behave — also fails. Parents can't easily change because their parenting style is shaped by the social system they live in.

> "No matter what you do, you end up with massive differences between the poor and the rich. The rich stay rich and the poor stay poor."

---

### Society as Hierarchy: Two Different Games

#### Main Idea

Society is divided into a hierarchy of rich and poor, and these two groups live in fundamentally different worlds with different optimal strategies.

- **Poor people** survive by **obeying authority**
- **Rich people** succeed by **negotiating with authority** — debating, arguing, presenting evidence

This explains the parenting differences completely:

- Poor parents train their children to obey authority (because that's what keeps them safe)
- Rich parents train their children to negotiate with authority (because that's how you get ahead in their world)

#### The Three Authority Figures for Poor Families

Poor parents are navigating relationships with:

1. **Police** — Will bully you if you're poor. Fighting back means jail. Accept authority.
2. **Boss** — Commands you. Your child must learn to follow orders and keep quiet.
3. **Family/community** — Maintaining good relations means adopting the same values and parenting norms. If your neighbors parent one way, you copy them.

**Connection**: For poor families, authoritarian parenting is the *optimal strategy* given their circumstances. Choosing a different path (being friendly, communicative, keeping promises) would make you seem deviant to your community.

#### Supporting Material: The Lecturer's Personal Parenting Experience

The lecturer shares his own example. He and his wife have three children and parent differently from typical Chinese families:

- They give their children freedom — no packed schedule of math, swimming, piano classes
- They believe in democracy within the family — everyone communicates on decisions
- They tell stories instead of drilling math

They do this because both parents have read extensively about parenting and education. The result: they have no friends in China. Their family thinks they're crazy.

> "The incentive is not for your child to succeed. The incentive is for your child to fit into China or the larger social environment that you are in."

**Connection**: This personal example illustrates why parenting practices are so resistant to change. The game parents are playing isn't "optimize my child's outcomes" — it's "fit into my social environment." Deviating from community norms carries real social costs.

### Can Poor Kids Succeed? (Student Q&A)

#### Main Idea

A student asks whether poor kids can ever break through. The lecturer acknowledges it happens but identifies it as exceptional rather than systematic.

#### Historical and Personal Mechanisms of Social Mobility

1. **Leaving your community** — The lecturer shares his own story: his family immigrated to Toronto, and his father was a dishwasher. He succeeded by leaving Canada (which he describes as rigid for poor people) and getting a scholarship to the United States. This requires being extremely individualistic and accepting high risk.

2. **War and revolution** — Historically the most common mechanism of social mobility. Perform well in war, get promoted. But the risk is death.

3. **Marrying up** — The lecturer references an earlier class about dating markets. Women prefer high-status partners because marrying into a low-status family disadvantages their children.

#### The Role of Luck

> "You have to get really lucky."

The lecturer is blunt: hard work and talent are necessary but insufficient. The odds are against poor people. Success from poverty requires a specific personality type — highly individualistic, very ambitious, high risk tolerance. Most people don't have this combination.

A student pushes back, asking whether luck can be strategic. The lecturer agrees partially:

> "Luck is a form of strategy. You're trying to position yourself in a place that allows you to get lucky."

He left Canada for the US because he knew effort was more likely to be noticed there. But strategic positioning still depends on luck. These individuals remain a tiny minority — roughly one percent of the population.

### Why Societies Collapse: Elite Overproduction

#### Main Idea

The system described so far — rich and poor living in separate worlds with different strategies, reproducing their positions through parenting — appears extremely stable. The lecturer calls this **equilibrium**. So why do societies collapse?

The problem is on the rich side. Rich people are trained from birth to negotiate for better outcomes. They have **high expectations** (versus the poor who have **low expectations** — survival is enough). But power is a **zero-sum game**. Only a few can be at the top.

Over time, too many rich people compete for too few positions of power. This is **elite overproduction**.

When this happens, a faction of the rich that can't get power allies with factions of the poor against the current power holders.

> "All revolutions are always between the have-a-lot versus the have-some. It's never between rich and poor."

#### Supporting Material: Historical Examples

- **Chinese Revolution**: Mao Zedong, Zhou Enlai, Deng Xiaoping — none came from poor families, but they weren't rich enough. The revolution was urban elite versus rural elite.
- **Chinese dynastic history**: Revolutions were typically led by merchants — people with money but low social status who wanted to convert wealth into power.
- **Julius Caesar**: Popular because he promised Roman citizens debt cancellation, freedom from slavery, and land redistribution. That's why he was assassinated.
- **Donald Trump**: The lecturer draws a parallel — Americans buried in credit card debt, student loans, and mortgages believe Trump could cancel those debts, similar to the appeal of historical revolutionary figures.

---

### The Three Seeds of Revolution

#### Main Idea

The specific problems that create revolutionary conditions are remarkably consistent across history:

1. **Indebtedness** — The rich monopolize wealth. The poor must borrow. Interest rates compound until the debt becomes unpayable.
2. **Slavery** — When you can't pay your debt, your body becomes collateral. Your children inherit your debt slavery.
3. **Landlessness** — Debt leads to loss of land.

When these three conditions reach critical mass, an elite faction can mobilize the poor with a simple promise:

> "Follow me into revolution, and I will clear your debts. I will give you land, and I will end slavery."

#### Supporting Material: The Universal Revolutionary Promise

Every revolution, regardless of ideology, makes the same promise — just with different branding:

- **Communism**: "We'll create a communist paradise" = debt cancellation + land redistribution + end of slavery
- **Islam**: Mohammed's "kingdom of heaven" = no debt, land to feed yourself, no Muslim can be enslaved
- **Julius Caesar**: Promised the same three things to Romans
- **Donald Trump**: Modern version — Americans in debt to credit cards, student loans, mortgages, hoping for relief

**Connection**: The specific ideology doesn't matter. The underlying game-theoretic structure is identical across civilizations and centuries.

### Social Mobility as the Best Governance

#### Main Idea (Student Q&A)

A student named Alan asks whether elites deliberately maintain limited social mobility to preserve stability without triggering revolution. The lecturer confirms this insight and goes further.

**Social mobility is the best form of governance**, regardless of political system. The evidence:

- **1950s America** (democracy): High social mobility → people worked hard → country became wealthy
- **1950s China** (communist): After decades of war, enormous opportunities for social mobility → people worked hard for the same reasons, despite a completely different political system

> "You don't need democracy. You don't need any system. As long as you have social mobility, people will be happy."

#### The Decay Cycle

The problem: social mobility is inherently temporary. Within less than a generation:

1. Talented people fill positions of power
2. They don't want equally talented outsiders to replace them — they want their *children* to replace them
3. Positions fill up. Waiting lists form.
4. Talented poor people have nowhere to go

#### Supporting Material: The Chinese Imperial Examination (Keju)

The lecturer traces the decay cycle through the Chinese **Keju** (civil service examination):

- Early dynasty: talented people take the exam, best rise to the top
- Next generation: officials teach their children how to pass → advantage accumulates
- Later: corruption — cheating on the exam
- Eventually: positions are simply purchased
- Social mobility dies → revolution follows

Specific examples:

- **Hong Xiuquan**: Failed or couldn't advance through the Keju. No position available. Declared himself the brother of Jesus. Started the Taiping Rebellion — tens of millions died.
- **Mao Zedong**: By his time, the Keju had been eliminated entirely. No ladder to climb. Turned to revolutionary ideology.

### The Game Reset Theory of Revolution

#### Main Idea

Revolution is a **game reset**. The lecturer offers a game-theory framing:

> "A hundred people are playing a game, ten people are winning, and they keep on winning, and they can only win. So everyone else is like, screw this game. Let's start a new game."

The ten winners resist. The ninety others have no recourse except revolution — which resets the game and temporarily restores social mobility.

But the cycle repeats: once new people gain power, they rig the game for their children. Schools for the rich become different from schools for the poor. The system is designed so certain people succeed and everyone else fails.

> "The system is set up to make sure that certain people succeed and everyone else fails."

This is what the lecturer identifies as the fundamental course of human history:

> "Either you climb up the ladder. If they don't allow you to climb up the ladder, then you'll just break the game."

### Key Takeaways

- Self-control, resilience, and self-assessment are *results* of success, not *causes* of it
- Correlation does not equal causation — a foundational principle for understanding social science claims
- Parenting strategies are shaped by socioeconomic position, not ignorance — poor parents are making rational choices for their environment
- Poor children's behavior in experiments like the marshmallow test reflects rational adaptation to unstable environments, not character deficiency
- Social mobility is the strongest predictor of societal health, regardless of political system
- Revolutions follow a consistent pattern: elite overproduction + mass debt/slavery/landlessness → factional elite allies with the poor → game reset
- Escaping poverty requires high risk tolerance, individualism, ambition, and luck — making it the exception, not a scalable solution

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Marshmallow test**: Experiment by Walter Mischel testing children's ability to delay gratification, later reinterpreted as a test of trust in authority
- **Growth mindset / Fixed mindset**: Carol Dweck's framework — those who treat failure as learning opportunity vs. those who give up
- **Deliberate practice**: K. Anders Ericsson's concept of strategic, self-reflective practice vs. mere repetition
- **Dunning-Kruger effect**: Cognitive bias where the least competent individuals are most confident in their abilities
- **Correlation vs. causation**: The principle that two things occurring together does not mean one causes the other
- **Elite overproduction**: When too many wealthy/powerful people compete for too few positions of power, destabilizing society
- **Game reset**: The lecturer's game-theory framing of revolution — when the current game is unwinnable, players destroy it and start over
