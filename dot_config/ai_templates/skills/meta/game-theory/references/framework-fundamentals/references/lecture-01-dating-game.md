## Game Theory and Human Behavior — Introductory Lecture

### Overview

This is an introductory lecture for a semester-long course on game theory as a framework for understanding human behavior, societies, and civilizations. The instructor surveys five existing theories of what drives human behavior, then introduces game theory as a superior alternative. A marriage market example is used to demonstrate game theory concepts, leading into a broader analysis of fertility rates, civilizational life cycles, and geopolitical predictions.

---

### Five Theories of What Drives Human Behavior

#### Main Idea

The instructor opens with the semester's central question: how do societies behave, and what motivates us? Five established theories are presented as the intellectual landscape before game theory is introduced.

1. **Religion** — Humans are caught in a war between good and evil, between Satan and God. Religion exists to show us the path toward goodness.

2. **Biology** — The drive to pass on our genes is the point of existence. Men and women have fundamentally different reproductive strategies: men seek to maximize the number of partners, women must be selective because of the enormous investment pregnancy and child-rearing require.

3. **Race and Culture** — The world is a struggle between different races and cultures for dominance, each with supposedly distinct characteristics. The instructor explicitly flags this as a theory people have believed in, not one he endorses.

4. **Economics** — Self-interest and money drive human behavior. People seek to maximize wealth.

5. **Liberalism (from the Enlightenment)** — History is an inevitable march toward enlightenment, rationality, truth, and justice. Human development trends toward paradise. This is called **liberalism**.

**Connection**: These five theories set the stage by showing the diversity of existing frameworks. The instructor positions them as incomplete, creating space for his alternative: game theory.

---

### Introducing Game Theory

#### Main Idea

The instructor's central thesis for the semester: **game theory** is the best way to understand what drives nations, societies, and individuals. He defines game theory as having three components:

1. **Players** — The participants in the game
2. **Rules (Constraints)** — The boundaries or limits of the game. In mathematics, these are called **boundary conditions**.
3. **Incentives** — How you win the game, what you get from it

> "If you figure out who the players are, if you understand the rules, if you understand the incentives, then you understand how the game works, and then, therefore, you can predict how the game will turn out."

#### Three Promised Benefits of Learning Game Theory

The instructor frames the course as a value proposition with three benefits:

1. **You become a better person** — Education's real purpose is not grades or jobs but becoming a more thoughtful, curious, moral, and imaginative person who makes better decisions.

2. **You understand the world** — The class will analyze current events (Ukraine war, Israel-Iran tensions, South America, China-Japan conflict, US-China relations) to understand why things happen and what they lead to—not just to label them as stupid or wrong.

3. **You gain predictive powers** — Understanding the game gives you the ability to predict how the world and your own life will develop, granting sovereignty over your destiny.

> "The point of education, guys, it's not to get good grades or get in university or get a good job. What I believe is the point of education is to make you into a better person."

### The Marriage Market Game — A Game Theory Demonstration

#### Main Idea

The instructor builds a simplified model to demonstrate game theory in action. Five boys and five girls want to get married. Each is ranked from 5 (best) to 1 (worst) based on three criteria of attractiveness:

- **Genes** — Good looking, tall, healthy, long lifespan
- **Wealth** — Personal and family money
- **Status** — Social connections, high-status job, powerful family

#### Why Pure Biological Strategy Fails

According to **evolutionary psychology**, all five women should try to mate with the number-five male (the best). But this is suicidal for the species—society would collapse. You can't have all women pursuing one man.

#### The Economic/Rational Solution — Nash Equilibrium

When male number five tries to maximize by pursuing all five women, the women react strategically:

- Female 5 recognizes she's being played and settles for male 4 (not much difference)
- Female 4 follows suit and pairs with male 3
- Male 5 ends up stuck with the worst option

The same cascading failure happens to any male who tries this strategy. The rational outcome is cooperation: 5 pairs with 5, 4 with 4, 3 with 3, and so on.

**Nash equilibrium** — The state in which all players maximize their outcome. In this game, it means everyone pairs with their equivalent rank. The world is orderly, everyone has a partner, and children are born.

#### Why Nash Equilibrium Fails in Real Life

The instructor immediately undercuts the theory: in real life, no one follows Nash equilibrium.

He describes a classroom exercise from a previous semester where he asked students to imagine they were 30, had given up on finding a perfect partner, and to write down their minimum requirements for marriage:

- **Boys**: "As long as she likes me, I'm good." Minimal requirements.
- **Girls**: "If I don't love him, I need about a million dollars a month."

In practice, all women pursue males 4 and 5. Males 3, 2, and 1 are left behind—watching Netflix, playing video games, consuming pornography. The instructor calls these people **incels** (involuntary celibates) who have given up on life.

> "They've given up on life, and life has given up on them."

#### The Key Insight — Status, Not Sex

The game theory analysis reveals the missing piece: the real incentive is not sex or procreation but **status**.

- People want to marry someone they can brag about, post on Instagram, walk through the mall with to provoke jealousy
- Women, even those ranked as the best, reason that since having children is their choice, the partner needs to be exceptional

> "It's a game about status, not a game about sex or procreation."

**Connection**: This explains why people behave "irrationally"—they are actually rational, but you have to correctly identify which game they are playing. The game is status, not reproduction.

### Superstructure — Why the Game Changes Over Time

#### Main Idea

If humans have always played status games that lead to societal collapse, how has humanity survived so long? The answer: the game changes over time according to the **superstructure** of society.

**Superstructure** — The big picture context that determines the nature of the game. It encompasses demographics, economics, culture, politics, religion, population size, wealth levels, and external threats.

#### Three Historical Superstructures

The instructor presents three superstructure types that correspond to different stages of civilization:

**Superstructure 1 — Early Society (Birth)**

- ~200 people, a village
- Poor, low technology (no clean water, no understanding of germs)
- High maternal and child mortality
- Low competition (no rival societies)
- **Marriage game**: No dating. Women have a husband but also sleep with other men to disguise paternity. This ensures all men in the village believe the child could be theirs and will protect it. The strategy is about social cohesion through widespread sexual access.

**Superstructure 2 — Growing Society (Maturation)**

- ~10,000–20,000 people
- Growing wealth, some technology (clean water)
- High competition with neighboring societies
- **Marriage game**: No dating. **Arranged marriages**. The priority is producing as many children as possible to survive competition. You marry whoever your parents choose—your childhood friend, a neighbor. Romantic preference is irrelevant.

**Superstructure 3 — Overpopulated Society (Collapse)**

- Billions of people (e.g., China)
- Excessive wealth producing inequality
- High technology (childbirth is safe, children survive)
- **Equilibrium** — competition exists but outright war is avoided
- **Marriage game**: The **dating game** emerges. Status is rare and the only way to change your status is by "marrying up." This creates a competitive dating market that drives down fertility rates. Society trends toward collapse.

#### The Life Cycle of Civilizations

These three superstructures map onto a universal pattern: **birth → maturation → collapse**. Every civilization in history has followed this trajectory. There is no way around it.

> "If you look at history, all civilizations go through this process of birth, maturation, and then collapse. And there's no way around it."

**Connection**: Game theory applied to the marriage market becomes a diagnostic tool for civilizational health. The marriage game reveals the superstructure, which in turn reveals where a civilization came from and where it's heading.

### Fertility Rates as a Civilizational Indicator

#### Main Idea

The instructor shifts to real-world data. A global map shows fertility rates by color, with red indicating above-replacement (2.1+) and cooler colors showing below-replacement rates.

- **Africa** (dark red, 6–7+ fertility rate) — Growing society with middling technology, high mortality, lots of competition. Families need many children to survive.
- **North America, Europe, East Asia** (below replacement) — Wealthy, overpopulated, status-driven societies where women are forced to pick the best mate or not have children at all.

#### The Immigration Band-Aid

North America and Europe are "kinda okay" because they can **import immigrants** to sustain their labor force. East Asia cannot or will not do this, making their situation far more dire.

#### The Collapse Indicator

> "If you look at history, the best indicator that a society is about to collapse is if the women who are wealthy and well educated refuse to have children."

The instructor draws a historical parallel: this happened to the Romans before their collapse. It's happening now across America, Britain, Europe, and China.

### South Korea — The Worst Case Scenario

#### Main Idea

South Korea represents the extreme endpoint of the status-driven dating game. Its fertility rate is between 0.6 and 0.8—the lowest in the world. The instructor declares it could be functionally gone within fifty years.

#### Supporting Material: Population Pyramid Projections

The instructor walks through South Korea's projected population pyramids:

- **2020**: Still a majority working-age population—manageable
- **2040**: Huge dependent elderly population, shrinking working-age cohort, almost no children
- **2060**: Working population halved, massive retiree population
- **2100 (best case)**: A "zombie society" where nobody works and the economy has collapsed

#### Why South Korea Can't Fix This

South Korea is an extremely materialistic society. The only path to success is making money. For a middle-class family, the rational strategy is to have one child and invest everything into that child's education, hoping they pass college examinations and get a job at Samsung ("the only company in South Korea").

The instructor argues South Korea is also strategically doomed: it can't fight a war because its adult population is too old. If North Korea ever threatened, South Korea's military capacity would be negligible.

> "The prospects for South Korea is utterly hopeless."

---

### Israel — The Anomaly

#### Main Idea

When plotting GDP per capita against fertility rate, one country stands out as an outlier: **Israel**. It is the only wealthy, Western, high-tech society in which women choose to have more than two children.

The instructor argues this makes Israel the country most likely to be dominant over the next fifty years—a conclusion he acknowledges is counterintuitive given its small population (nine million), desert geography, and hostile neighborhood.

#### Why Israel Is Different

The answer lies in the incentive structure. Because the world perceives Israel as under threat, fertility becomes a form of **status** within Israeli society:

- Having children = loving Israel
- Having children = contributing to national survival
- Having children = patriotism

> "In Israel, fertility is status."

Israel also has structural advantages the instructor highlights: democracy, innovation, technology, openness, freedom to criticize the government, and social mobility.

#### Contrast with the Western World

The West has replaced religion and patriotism with materialism. Status is measured by Instagram followers, YouTube subscribers, and money. This incentive structure discourages childbearing.

### Saudi Arabia — The Student Question

#### Main Idea

A student asks why Saudi Arabia also shows high GDP and high fertility, similar to Israel. The instructor treats it as a different case:

- Saudi Arabia's wealth comes entirely from oil revenue, redistributed as a welfare state (free schooling, healthcare, housing, guaranteed jobs)
- Religious duty (Islam) creates an imperative to have many children
- But Saudi Arabia lacks **human capital** — it's not an open, innovative, democratic society
- It's vulnerable: what happens if oil runs out or a war hits?

**Connection**: Saudi Arabia is an outlier for structural reasons (oil welfare + religion), not because of the same status dynamics that drive Israel. The instructor uses this contrast to show that game theory doesn't give definitive answers—it gives a guide for asking questions and doing research.

> "Game theory doesn't give us the answers. Game theory just gives us a guide to ask questions and do research."

---

### The Analytical Framework — Summary

The instructor closes by crystallizing the methodology he will teach all semester:

1. Identify the **players**, **rules**, and **incentives** of any situation
2. From those, identify the **superstructure** (the big-picture demographic, economic, cultural, political, and religious context)
3. Once you have both, you can figure out **where the situation came from** and **predict where it's going**
4. You can also work in reverse: examine the superstructure to figure out what game is being played

---

### Key Takeaways

- Game theory (players + rules + incentives) is presented as the most powerful framework for understanding human behavior at every scale
- The real game in modern dating markets is status, not reproduction—this is why people behave "irrationally"
- The superstructure of society determines which game is being played, and it changes over time
- Declining fertility in wealthy, educated populations is the strongest historical indicator of civilizational collapse
- Follow current events—the class requires active engagement with world news
- Game theory is a tool for asking better questions, not for generating definitive answers

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Game Theory**: Framework for analyzing behavior through three components—players, rules, and incentives
- **Nash Equilibrium**: The state in which all players have maximized their outcome given others' strategies; no player benefits from changing their strategy unilaterally
- **Superstructure**: The macro-level context (demographics, economics, culture, politics, religion) that determines the nature of the game being played
- **Incels (Involuntary Celibates)**: Males at the bottom of the status hierarchy who have withdrawn from the dating market
- **Boundary Conditions**: Mathematical term for the rules or constraints of a game
- **Replacement Rate**: The fertility rate (2.1 children per woman) needed to maintain a population at its current level
- **Human Capital**: The productive capacity of a population based on education, skills, innovation, and civic participation
