## Game Theory Applied to Education

### Overview

This is the second lecture in a game theory course. The instructor uses the education system—particularly his own experience reforming a Chinese international school program—as a case study to teach how game theory works. The lecture builds from identifying players and their stated motivations, to revealing their actual motivations, to showing how the convergence of those real interests constructs the "game" that determines how institutions behave.

---

### The Three Purposes of School

#### Main Idea

The instructor establishes a framework for what school is supposed to accomplish before arguing that most schools fail at all three goals—and often produce the opposite effect.

The three purposes are:

- **Literacy** — reading and writing. The primary purpose, because functioning in modern society requires absorbing and conveying information.
- **Core competencies** — critical thinking, cooperation, collaboration, communication.
- **Lifelong learning** — in the age of AI and globalization, school is only the beginning. The world changes every five years, so students must love learning and know how to learn independently.

#### Supporting Material: How Schools Fail Each Purpose

The instructor walks through how most schools actively undermine each goal:

- **Literacy failure**: Students aren't required to read books. University professors, shocked by this, assign paragraphs or videos instead. Attention spans have shrunk to about five minutes.
- **Core competencies failure**: School is structured as a zero-sum competition. Class ranking makes collaboration irrational—for you to get ahead, you need to outperform your classmate. This contradicts the stated goal of teaching cooperation.
- **Lifelong learning failure**: School teaches students to hate learning. The instructor points to the Chinese tradition after the National Interest Examination at age eighteen—students rip pages from their books and throw them in the air as a ceremony of liberation.

> "I never ever wanna read a book again. I never ever wanna take a test again. I never wanna learn again."

**Connection**: By establishing what school should do and showing how it fails, the instructor sets up the central question of the lecture: why does this happen? Game theory becomes the analytical tool to answer that question.

### The Shenzhen Story — A Reform Case Study

#### Main Idea

The instructor tells a personal story from 2008 to illustrate game theory principles through lived experience. He was hired by a school in Shenzhen, South China, to build a program sending students abroad. He diagnosed the problems, implemented reforms, achieved measurable success—and was then fired and permanently excluded from management work.

#### Supporting Material: Diagnosing the Problem (2008)

About ten percent of students (roughly eighty per year) planned to study abroad, but their preparation was ineffective:

- Regular Chinese classes: fifty kids in a room, no communication, no questions, no debate
- SAT word list memorization instead of actual reading and writing
- Everyone doing Model United Nations—no differentiation

#### Supporting Material: The Reforms

The instructor made three structural changes:

1. **Seminar system** — replaced large lecture classes with small groups of ten to twenty students discussing books together, taught by American teachers
2. **Reading culture** — established a five-thousand-book English library; required extensive reading to teach the joy of reading rather than rote memorization
3. **New activities replacing Model UN**:
   - **The Coffee House** — the first student-run coffee house in a Chinese high school. Students ran a real business: finance, service, waitering, entrepreneurship, cooperation. The instructor notes it still exists at the school today.
   - **The Daily Newspaper** — the first daily newspaper in a Chinese high school, possibly worldwide. Students collected information, wrote articles, edited, and published every day, sometimes working until 2 AM, then delivering at 7 AM.

#### Supporting Material: The Cultural Principles

The instructor understood that structural changes required cultural change. His three principles:

- **Transparency** — admitting mistakes together and correcting them openly
- **Innovation** — constant iteration, which created some chaos but built resilience
- **Openness** — honest communication about everything

The idea was not to build a perfect curriculum but an evolving one.

#### Supporting Material: The Outcome Paradox

The program became the most famous study abroad program in South China. Students went to Yale, Wharton, Cornell. It had the best college admissions record in the region.

Then:

1. He was fired
2. Teachers, parents, and students were happy to see him go
3. He set up a similar program in Beijing, which also succeeded
4. After four years of success, he was never allowed to work in management again

The word everyone used to describe him was not "reformer" or "visionary" but **dictator**. And more commonly: **asshole**.

> "I insisted on fairness... and as you can imagine, that pissed off a lot of powerful people because that's not how the game is played in China."

> "It's actually not how this game is played anywhere in the world. The game is not fair. The game is established by stakeholders, and they play the game according to their interest."

**Connection**: The story is the emotional and narrative anchor of the entire lecture. It makes the abstract framework of game theory concrete—the instructor's personal failure becomes the proof that understanding player motivations matters more than having good intentions or good results.

### Identifying the Players and Their Motivations

#### Main Idea

In game theory, you must identify all the **players** (stakeholders) and understand their real motivations—not their stated ones. The instructor first presents his original (wrong) assumptions, then corrects them with what he learned.

#### The Players

- Students
- Parents
- Teachers
- Administrators (school leaders)
- Government
- Colleges (US universities)

#### The Instructor's Original (Wrong) Assumptions

| Player | Assumed Motivation |
|---|---|
| Students | Love learning, get into Ivy League, be the best |
| Parents | Want successful, independent kids; want face |
| Teachers | Love teaching, love students, want space to teach their passion |
| Administrators | Want good results |
| Government | Want innovation, good workers, strong China |
| Colleges | Want students with the best potential, future leaders |

#### The Corrected Understanding

The instructor introduces a core game theory principle:

> "All the players are motivated by achieving the best possible results by doing the least amount of work possible."

> "People are lazy, and people are greedy. It's that simple."

| Player | Actual Motivation |
|---|---|
| Students | Get into Ivy League but do as little work as possible; if hard work is required, settle for a lesser school |
| Parents | Control their kid; don't want independent thinking, want obedience; face matters more than actual learning |
| Teachers | This is a job; do the minimum to collect salary and go home; no passion |
| Administrators | Keep powerful parents happy; ignore poor and middle-class parents; protect their own position |
| Government | Don't want innovation if it causes problems; want stability and compliance |
| Colleges | Want tuition money; will accept anyone willing to pay |

**Connection**: The gap between assumed and actual motivations explains the instructor's failure. He built a system based on idealized motivations. The real game was being played according to entirely different rules.

### Ranking Player Power

#### Main Idea

Not all players are equal. Game theory requires ranking players by their actual power and influence over the game's construction.

The instructor's ranking for this education game:

1. **Parents** — most important. They pay to play and can cause the most problems.
2. **Teachers** — they implement the rules of the game. How teachers behave determines how the game is played.
3. **Administrators** — important but less so than parents and teachers.
4. **Students** — despite being the majority, they don't actually matter. What students think and want is irrelevant to how the game is constructed.
5. **Government** — doesn't care as long as the school doesn't cause problems.
6. **Colleges** — don't care either. US colleges primarily want students willing to pay tuition. Even the Ivy League wants students from powerful families, not necessarily the best students.

> "They say they want really passionate students who are curious, who are imaginative from China. They don't care. For them, it's just business."

### The Convergence Point — How the Game Is Constructed

#### Main Idea

A game is constructed when the interests of different players converge. The overlapping zone of what all major players actually want determines the rules and incentives of the game.

When you map the real interests:

- Parents want white faces (foreign teachers as status markers), good grades, and prestige outcomes
- Teachers want to do minimal work
- Administrators want to keep powerful parents happy

The convergence produces this version of school:

- **White faces** — foreign teachers hired for appearance, not quality
- **Easy grades** — everyone gets an A regardless of effort
- **A few top students** — given scholarships and sent to top universities for the school's marketing
- **Lots of cheating** — tolerated because enforcing standards creates conflict
- **High turnover** — students leave, teachers leave, new ones come in, nobody cares
- **Beautiful marketing** — nice buildings, fancy activities, great websites, motivating admissions officers

> "If you don't like a teacher, just complain, and then the teacher has to change."

The instructor notes this applies globally, not just in China.

**Connection**: This is the core analytical payoff of the lecture. The game theory framework explains why schools are the way they are—not because of bad individuals, but because the convergence of rational self-interest among all players produces a dysfunctional system.

---

### Reform Must Stay Within the Convergence Point

#### Main Idea

The instructor draws a critical lesson for anyone attempting institutional reform: change is possible, but only within the convergence zone that players will accept.

His mistake in 2008 was trying to create an entirely new game with his own rules—acting as "god" of the system. Players rejected this because it fell outside their convergence point.

> "Whatever game you set up has to be within this convergence point in order for players to accept it."

This applies universally: political reform, institutional change, any domain. You must:

1. Identify where stakeholders currently converge
2. Figure out how to move them from one part of the convergence zone to another
3. Accept that this is slow, incremental work

**Connection**: This reframes the Shenzhen failure. The program's outcomes were excellent, but the process violated the convergence point. The instructor was called a dictator not because he was wrong, but because he was playing a game the other players hadn't agreed to.

### The Superstructure — Why Players Want What They Want

#### Main Idea

A student asks what causes different stakeholders to have different interests. The instructor introduces the concept of **superstructure**—the macro-level conditions of a society (demographics, economy, politics, religion, culture) that shape individual motivations.

He offers three metrics for understanding where a society sits in its development:

- **Energy** — the drive to work hard, be focused, care about quality. Not just hours worked but genuine engagement.
- **Openness** — willingness to learn, grow, admit mistakes, and improve.
- **Cohesion** — seeing yourself as part of a community; wanting the community to thrive, not just yourself.

#### Supporting Material: Finland vs. China Over Time

**Finland**: small country, five million people, amazing schools. Why? Because Finnish society has high energy, openness, and cohesion. This means they invest in every child's future, make teaching a prestigious profession, pay teachers well, and give them autonomy.

**China in the 1980s**: schools were excellent. Teachers felt respected, believed in their responsibility, took pride in their work. Students enjoyed learning. There were fewer tests, less pressure, and students learned more.

**China today**: all three metrics have declined due to wealth generation, inequality, and corruption.

- **Cohesion → Individualization**: "I don't care if other kids do well. It's a zero-sum game. I'd rather my kid does okay and every other kid gets screwed over."
- **Openness → Fear**: If an administrator admits a mistake, parents demand they be fired. So administrators hide in their offices. Schools can never improve because no one can acknowledge errors.
- **Energy → Apathy**: If you do a good job, parents just demand more. You can't please everyone, so you do as little as possible. Meanwhile, students aren't motivated because wealthy parents can just buy them an apartment and a car regardless of school performance.

> "If students work hard, teachers are motivated to teach well."

The reverse is also true: when students don't care, teachers have no reason to either.

**Connection**: The superstructure concept answers why players have the motivations they do. It's not individual moral failure—it's a systemic response to macro-level social conditions. The game theory analysis sits on top of this structural foundation.

### Players Are Playing Multiple Games Simultaneously

#### Main Idea

Each player's behavior in the education game is shaped by other games they're simultaneously playing. This is why motivations are complex and often seem irrational when viewed from a single-game perspective.

#### Supporting Material: Parents Playing Multiple Games

Parents have different identities and each identity involves a different game:

- **Family game** (siblings): Brothers compete against each other—who has more money, better kids, a more beautiful wife. Your child's education is a weapon in this competition.
- **Colleague game**: You need to get along with colleagues, which means your child must be able to befriend the children of your colleagues. Education choices must not alienate you from your professional network.

The fear driving all of this is **ostracism**—being kicked out as a player entirely.

> "The thing that people fear the most is ostracism... where you are no longer a part of the group. They kick you out as a player."

This creates a paradox: you're trying to beat other players while simultaneously trying not to piss them off and get thrown out of the game. This tension prevents "cheating" in the social sense—doing anything too different.

#### Supporting Material: Why the Instructor's Reform Was Threatening

The rebellion against his reforms wasn't just about education preferences. He was subverting traditional Chinese values and norms. If parents believed in his approach, they risked being ostracized by the larger community.

> "If people believed in me, then they risk themselves being ostracized by the larger community."

#### Supporting Material: Students Playing Multiple Games

Students also play multiple simultaneous games, ranked by actual importance:

1. **Pleasing parents** — most important, ensures comfortable life
2. **Friendship game** — having many friends, being popular, ensures future success
3. **School performance** — the game that actually matters least

> "You're not in school to learn. You're in school to please parents and teachers. There's a difference."

> "Grades are a way of showing how well you're pleasing the parents and the teachers."

### Identity, Adaptation, and the Nature of the Game

#### Main Idea

The lecture concludes with a broader philosophical point: we are shaped by the games we play. We adapt to whatever game we're in, and our identity changes as games change.

- How you behaved in kindergarten differs from high school, which will differ from adult life
- You must adapt to new games with different players and new requirements
- You might enter school wanting to learn and be creative, but you quickly recognize that's not what's rewarded
- Getting along, having friends, helping others cheat—that's what's rewarded, so that's what you do

> "Game theory, it's not about ideas. It's not about ideals. It's not the way things should be. It's the way things are."

> "The way things are is determined by who players are. And the way players behave is a response to the game they believe they are playing."

**Connection**: This is the thesis statement of the entire course so far. Game theory is descriptive, not normative. It explains behavior by understanding the structure of games and the rational responses of players within them.

---

### Key Takeaways

- Identify all players in a system and rank them by actual power, not stated importance
- Distinguish between stated motivations and actual motivations—people want maximum results with minimum effort
- The "game" emerges from where player interests converge
- Reform is only possible within the convergence zone; stepping outside it gets you expelled from the game
- Each player is simultaneously playing multiple games across different identities (family, work, social)
- The fear of ostracism is a primary driver of behavior
- The superstructure of society (energy, openness, cohesion) shapes what players want
- Game theory is descriptive: it explains what is, not what should be

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Superstructure**: the macro-level picture of a society—demographics, economy, politics, religion—that shapes individual behavior and institutional design
- **Convergence point**: the zone where the interests of different players overlap, determining the rules and structure of the game
- **Energy** (societal metric): the collective drive to work hard and care about quality
- **Openness** (societal metric): willingness to learn, admit mistakes, and improve
- **Cohesion** (societal metric): sense of community and investment in collective well-being
- **Ostracism**: being expelled from the game entirely; the primary fear that constrains player behavior
- **Players/Stakeholders**: the actors in a game whose motivations and power levels determine how the game is constructed
