## How the Global Economy Collapses — Engineered Financial Crises

### Overview

This is a speculative lecture exploring the argument that financial collapses are not natural events but are engineered by powerful financial actors ("transnational capital"). The speaker traces the history of centralized banking from the 1688 Glorious Revolution through the 2008 financial crisis, and speculates about a future engineered collapse of the US economy tied to a geopolitical shift toward Israel.

**Important caveat**: The speaker explicitly states multiple times that this is speculation, not scholarly work. He says he has never studied economics and presents these ideas "for fun, for entertainment."

---

### The Boom-Bust Cycle — Standard vs. Alternative Explanation

#### Main Idea

In mainstream economics, the **boom-bust cycle** describes how capitalist economies naturally oscillate between periods of growth and contraction. The conventional explanation is that during good times, people become overconfident, overspend, and then the economy contracts — like gaining too much weight and then needing to lose it.

The speaker's core thesis rejects this. He argues that no one in economics can properly explain what triggers a bubble to pop — what the actual mechanism is. His alternative: collapses are **engineered** by people who have the power to cause economies to rise and fall.

#### Supporting Material: Andrew Ross Sorkin's Explanation

The speaker references Andrew Ross Sorkin (described as "probably the most influential financial journalist in America") and his book about the 1929 stock market collapse. A passage is read aloud:

> "Lengthy, uninterrupted booms like the one in the nineteen twenties produce a collective delusion. Optimism becomes a drug or a religion or some combination of both. People lose their ability to calculate risk and distinguish between good ideas and bad ones."

**Connection**: The speaker calls this a "sad explanation" — it frames collapse as gravity, as inevitability. He uses it as a foil to set up his alternative thesis that there are identifiable actors behind the scenes engineering these events.

### How Banks Create Money — The Liquidity Illusion

#### Main Idea

The speaker introduces the idea that banks don't simply hold and redistribute money — they effectively create it. If a bank takes in $1 million in deposits and lends out $1 million, it should have zero. But the answer, he says, is $2 million — because each bank has the ability to generate liquidity. Money, in this framing, is described as "just an idea," "a concept," "a collective hallucination."

#### The Interest Rate as a Coordination Signal

The speaker then introduces a distinction between two ways of understanding **interest rates**:

- **Standard explanation**: Interest rates guide consumer behavior. Low rates (1%) encourage people to borrow and buy homes. High rates (5%) discourage borrowing.
- **Alternative explanation**: Interest rates are actually a coordination signal for banks — telling them whether to release more or less liquidity into the system. Low rates mean "make loans easier." High rates mean "tighten lending."

The mechanism that connects all banks together is the **central bank**, which sets the interest rate as a **signaling mechanism**.

> "The interest rate is not set in order to guide consumer behavior. It is set in order to coordinate liquidity in the marketplace."

### Historical Origins — The Glorious Revolution and the Bank of England

#### Main Idea

The speaker traces the origins of the current financial system to **1688** and the **Glorious Revolution** — framed as a marriage between the British Empire and the Dutch Republic.

#### The Logic of the Merger

- The Dutch Republic was the richest area in the world (controlled the spice trade) but couldn't defend itself militarily
- It was Protestant and under attack from Catholic France, the Catholic Church, and the Habsburgs
- Solution: Dutch wealth moves to England, and in **1694**, the **Bank of England** is created

#### The Bank of England Innovation

The Bank of England is described as a **private bank**, not accountable to the public. Its innovation:

- It prints money
- Parliament buys it
- This means lending to a nation-state (parliament) rather than a king — much safer, because nations persist even if individual leaders fall
- England, as an island protected by the Royal Navy, was the safest place in Europe

#### Three Characteristics of This System

1. **Profits are privatized, losses are socialized** — If England wins a war, investors profit. If England loses, the nation (the people) absorbs the cost.

2. **Wealth is generated through activity (especially wars)** — Capital needs to be deployed and grow, which drove British imperial expansion: the Napoleonic Wars, the Great Game (Russia vs. Britain for Asia), conquest of India, the Opium Wars in China.

3. **Transnationalism / Open borders for capital** — Capital must move freely across borders, and systems must be built to facilitate this movement.

### The Intellectual Foundations of "Money is God"

#### Main Idea

Because the system described above is obviously unfair, the speaker argues that transnational capital sponsored intellectuals to develop ideologies that would make people accept it. He frames this as building the philosophical case for materialism — the idea that "money is God."

#### The Philosophers

- **John Locke** — Argued private property is a God-given right, as fundamental as freedom or happiness. Founded **empiricism**: we can only know what we experience directly, so don't think about God (beyond experience).

- **David Hume** — Argued for **skepticism**: most of what we "know" is just custom or habit, not verified experience. Undermines claims to knowledge beyond the material.

- **Jeremy Bentham** — Founded **utilitarianism**: if something gives pleasure, it must be good. If spending money makes you happy, it's good.

- **John Stuart Mill** — Refined utilitarianism.

The speaker then connects this to the concept of **liberty**: freedom to pursue money-making, because that's the "ultimate good." A country that prevents you from making money is a dictatorship; a good country lets you enjoy the pleasures of wealth.

#### Later Extensions

- **Marx** — Introduced **dialectical materialism**: the world is defined by class struggle (poor vs. rich), removing the divine from the equation entirely
- **Darwin** — Evolution: humans are just animals, not divine beings
- **Freud** — Reduced human motivation to base drives (sex, pleasure)

**Connection**: The speaker presents all of these thinkers as part of a single intellectual project — removing God and the divine from public life and replacing it with money and material pleasure as the organizing principle of society.

### Transnational Capital Moves to America

#### Main Idea

After conquering the British system, transnational capital sought new markets — specifically America. The problem: Americans had just fought a revolution to escape the British Empire and didn't want Bank of England / City of London control.

#### The Agents

The solution was to use American agents who would monopolize industries with British capital:

- **John D. Rockefeller** — monopolized oil
- **Andrew Carnegie** — steel
- **J.P. Morgan** — finance
- **Cornelius Vanderbilt** — railroads/shipping

These agents then collaborated to create an American version of the Bank of England: the **Federal Reserve System** (established 1914).

#### What Followed the Federal Reserve

The speaker notes three events that followed the creation of the Fed:

1. America enters **World War I**
2. The **1929 stock market collapse** and the Great Depression
3. America joins **World War II** (1941)

**Connection**: The speaker implies these were not coincidental — the Federal Reserve meant America was now controlled by transnational capital, operating under the same logic: profits privatized, losses socialized, open capital movement.

### The 2008 Financial Crisis — Engineered Collapse

#### Main Idea

After America won the Cold War and enjoyed a "unipolar moment," manufacturing shifted to China while the US economy became finance-dominated. This set the stage for the 2008 crisis.

#### What Made Subprime Possible

**Subprime** = lending money to people who can't pay it back (in this case, lending to poor people to buy homes).

Three enablers:

1. **Bill Clinton's homeownership push** — A policy goal to increase minority homeownership, described as "noble" but opening the door to risky lending

2. **Repeal of the Glass-Steagall Act (1999)** — Glass-Steagall separated retail banks (which hold deposits) from investment banks (which take risks). Repealing it combined both functions, creating massive banks that needed new financial products to generate profit.

3. **Massive foreign capital inflows** — GCC countries, China, European pension funds, and Japan all poured money into US markets. Japan specifically through the **yen carry trade**: borrowing at 0% domestically (due to deflation policy) and buying US treasuries at 5% — described as "one of the dumbest things in the world."

#### CDOs and the Mechanics of Collapse

Banks created **CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations)** — essentially bundled mortgage investments. The logic: mortgages generate guaranteed monthly payments, so they're safe investments. The risk of default was dismissed under the doctrine of **"too big to fail"** — if enough homeowners defaulted, the entire system would collapse, so the system would never let that happen.

#### Why It Actually Collapsed

The speaker's key argument: the defaults alone didn't have to crash the system. Banks could have simply "rolled over" the defaults — extending payment deadlines indefinitely. The money is an illusion anyway.

So why did it collapse? Because specific people made it profitable to collapse:

- **John Paulson** — Made $20 billion by betting the housing market would collapse
- The speaker illustrates with a simplified example: If you've lent money to someone who can't pay, you could keep pretending. But if someone else bets you that borrower *won't* default, you can make more money by *forcing* the default and collecting on the bet.

> "You're gonna make more money from collapsing the system than just letting it go on."

- **Jamie Dimon / JPMorgan** — Profited by buying up collapsed banks at discount prices, consolidating the banking industry

#### Post-Crisis Asset Consolidation

Before 2008, most homes were owned by individuals. After the crisis, large institutions and banks bought up distressed properties cheaply. The speaker shows data indicating a massive shift from individual homeownership to institutional ownership (entities owning 1,000+ homes).

### Bubbles Don't Have to Pop

#### Main Idea

The speaker challenges the "gravity" explanation by pointing to current bubbles that haven't collapsed:

1. **Private credit bubble** (~$2 trillion) — Private banks lend to private companies that are losing money but keep extending credit rather than forcing bankruptcy. It hasn't collapsed because a small number of actors choose not to let it.

2. **AI bubble** — Companies like NVIDIA and OpenAI don't make money on their products (ChatGPT costs more to run than it generates in revenue). It's sustained because "these are just a few companies lending money to each other" — an inside game.

> "They collapse when it's profitable for a few individuals to make it collapse."

### China's Rise — Orchestrated by the BIS

#### Main Idea

After 2008, with America and Europe damaged, transnational capital decided to shift the center of economic gravity to China. The coordinating body: the **Bank of International Settlements (BIS)**, based in Basel, Switzerland — described as "the central bank of central banks."

#### The BIS

The BIS coordinates all national central banks. The speaker emphasizes it is **not public** — it operates for the benefit of powerful private interests.

#### Supporting Material: BIS and Nazi Germany

A passage is read from the book *Tower of Basel* describing how Nazi Germany, despite Hitler's rhetoric of economic independence, needed the BIS to facilitate international transactions. This is why Germany never invaded Switzerland or Sweden — they were more useful as financial hubs than as conquered territory.

> "Nazi Germany needed a financial channel to the neutral countries. It ran through Basel."

**Connection**: The BIS funded the Nazi war machine because it was profitable. After WWII, rather than being dismantled, the BIS became even more powerful.

#### Supporting Material: Marshall Plan and BIS

Another passage describes how Marshall Plan aid came with conditions — remodeling European societies on the American consumerism model — with the BIS at the center of new international payment mechanisms.

> "The American dream — a house in the suburbs, a car, and numerous household appliances — was projected as a near guaranteed benefit of American style freedom."

#### How China's Rise Was Engineered

The mechanism: the **exchange rate** (Renminbi to USD).

- Just as interest rates signal domestic liquidity, exchange rates signal internationally whether to trade with a nation
- Starting around 2008, the Chinese currency began appreciating — signaling to the world "trade with China"
- China responded by importing commodities and building massive infrastructure (high-speed rail, airports)
- All financed by bank loans

The speaker shows data:

- In 2008, Chinese, US, Japanese, and European banking systems were comparable in size
- After 2008, China's banking system exploded in size
- China now has the **four largest banks in the world** (larger than JPMorgan)
- But this "size" is mostly liabilities — debts that function as assets in the banking system

#### Why China's System Hasn't Imploded

Chinese debt is **localized** (held at local/regional level) rather than **nationalized** (like Japan's). The Bank of China itself doesn't hold as much debt relative to, say, the Bank of Japan.

#### China's Export Dominance

The speaker shows data: in 2000, America dominated global manufacturing exports. By 2024, Chinese exports dominate nearly everywhere.

#### Why China Didn't Become the Hegemon

Two reasons:

1. **China doesn't want to be the hegemon** — It's not interested in being a global reserve currency or maintaining military bases overseas
2. **America won't let it** — Hence tariffs and trade restrictions

#### Main Idea

Since China won't take the hegemon role, the speaker speculates that the next shift will be toward **Israel**. For this to happen, transnational capital first needs to collapse the American economy.

#### America's Three Problems

3. **Military weakness** — The Iran situation suggests American military power is not as strong as assumed

#### Possible Collapse Mechanisms

- Collapse the private credit bubble
- Collapse the AI bubble
- Collapse both simultaneously

#### Why Israel?

Three reasons transnational capital would invest in Israel:

1. **Wars** — The "Greater Israel Project" (conquering/controlling the Middle East) generates activity, and wars are described as "the most profitable enterprise in the world"
2. **Reconstruction** — Post-war rebuilding requires massive capital investment
3. **Global trade hub** — Israel's geographic location gives it control over trade routes, including Africa

#### How the Collapse Works for Capital

A stock market / economic collapse allows transnational capital to:

- Exit America with maximum profit
- Buy up **distressed assets** (water, oil, resources) at rock-bottom prices
- Create chaos, because "in chaos, there's profit"

> "If everyone's sitting around doing nothing, you can't make any money. But if you are fighting each other, there's blood on the streets, you can make a lot of money."

The speaker addresses the obvious question: why would America allow this? His answer: some believe it's in America's long-term interest for transnational capital to leave.

The analogy: transnational capital is a **cancer** on the American host. Removing it (like chemotherapy) is painful — potentially causing destruction of wealth, conditions for civil war, years or decades of suffering — but some, like Donald Trump, believe this pain is necessary.

> "Make America Great Again. And how do you do that? You get rid of these parasites."

The speaker clarifies this is **not necessarily a deliberate strategy** — every collapse will destroy enormous wealth and cause real suffering. But certain actors believe the long-term benefit outweighs the short-term pain.

### Key Takeaways

- Financial collapses, in this speculative framework, are engineered events — not natural consequences of market cycles
- The interest rate functions as a coordination signal for bank liquidity, not primarily as a consumer behavior guide
- The Bank of International Settlements operates as a "central bank of central banks" coordinating global financial flows
- The 2008 crisis was made profitable for specific actors (Paulson, JPMorgan) through mechanisms like credit default swaps
- Bubbles don't have to pop — they pop when it becomes more profitable to collapse them than to sustain them
- China's post-2008 rise was orchestrated through exchange rate manipulation coordinated by the BIS
- The speaker speculates a future US economic collapse will shift capital toward Israel

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Boom-bust cycle**: The oscillation between economic expansion and contraction in capitalist systems
- **Subprime**: Lending money to borrowers who are unlikely to be able to repay
- **Glass-Steagall Act**: US law (repealed 1999) that separated retail banking from investment banking
- **CDO (Collateralized Debt Obligation)**: Financial product bundling mortgages as investments
- **Yen carry trade**: Borrowing Japanese yen at near-zero interest to invest in higher-yielding US assets
- **Too big to fail**: The doctrine that certain institutions are so interconnected that their failure would collapse the entire system
- **BIS (Bank of International Settlements)**: Basel-based institution described as the central bank of central banks
- **Signaling mechanism**: How interest rates and exchange rates coordinate behavior across banks and nations
- **Transnational capital**: The speaker's term for the network of financial actors who operate across national borders and control capital flows
- **Quantitative easing**: Injecting large amounts of money into the financial system _[speaker uses this term loosely]_
