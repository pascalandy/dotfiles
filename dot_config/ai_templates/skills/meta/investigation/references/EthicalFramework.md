# Investigation Ethical Framework

Shared ethical requirements that apply to all investigation sub-skills. Every workflow must verify these before starting.

## Authorization Checklist

Before ANY investigation, confirm all four:

- [ ] Explicit authorization from the requesting party with a stated purpose.
- [ ] Defined scope: target entities, information types, and boundaries.
- [ ] Legal compliance verified for the relevant jurisdictions.
- [ ] Documentation plan in place for findings, sources, and methodology.

Stop if any item is unchecked.

## Boundaries

### Always

- Use only publicly available sources.
- Document every source and the method used to reach it.
- Respect platform Terms of Service.
- Apply proportionality: collect the minimum necessary for the stated purpose.
- Protect subject privacy beyond the authorized scope.
- Distinguish verified facts from inferences in every report.
- Use multi-source verification for any claim that matters.
- Disclose limitations, gaps, and potential biases.

### Never

- Access private systems without authorization.
- Use pretexting, impersonation, or social engineering.
- Circumvent access controls or authentication.
- Purchase illegally obtained data.
- Stalk, harass, or intimidate subjects.
- Exceed the authorized scope.
- Share findings with unauthorized parties.
- Make false representations to obtain information.

## Legal Considerations by Domain

### People Investigations

- FCRA compliance for employment and credit contexts.
- State background check and anti-stalking laws.
- Privacy torts and harassment statutes.
- GDPR if EU subjects are involved; CCPA for California residents.

### Business Investigations

- Trade secret protections.
- Competitive intelligence boundaries.
- Securities law (insider trading risk).
- CFAA for technical reconnaissance.

### Infrastructure Investigations

- CFAA for scanning and probing.
- Authorized penetration testing scope.
- Responsible disclosure obligations.
- Data breach notification laws.
- Export controls on threat intelligence.

## Proportionality Test

Before collecting data, answer:

1. Is this within the authorized scope?
2. Is this necessary for the stated objective?
3. Is there a less invasive way to get the same answer?
4. Could collection harm the subject?
5. Can the collection be justified if challenged?

If any answer is no, stop and reassess.

## Data Handling

- **Collection**: Minimize to authorized scope. Document sources immediately. Timestamp all findings.
- **Storage**: Apply access controls. Set retention limits. Plan secure destruction.
- **Sharing**: Only to authorized recipients. Secure transmission. Need-to-know basis. Redact unnecessary PII.
- **Retention**: Defined period. Regular review for deletion. Secure destruction. Audit trail maintained.

## Escalation

- **Scope uncertainty**: Pause, document the question, seek clarification before proceeding.
- **Legal concern**: Stop immediately, document the concern, consult counsel.
- **Ethical dilemma**: Apply proportionality test, consider harm, seek peer review, document the decision.
- **Sensitive finding**: Assess disclosure obligations, consider subject harm, follow responsible disclosure.

## Red Lines

These actions are never authorized regardless of context:

- Hacking or unauthorized system access.
- Password cracking without explicit authorization.
- Physical surveillance or trespass.
- Bribery or corruption.
- Illegal wiretapping or interception.
- Purchasing stolen data or credentials.
- Creating fake identities for access.
- Threatening or coercing sources.

## When to Stop

Stop the investigation entirely if:

- The purpose shifts to harassment, stalking, or coercion.
- The subject has clearly opted out of contact and the purpose is reconnection.
- Continued investigation requires illegal methods.
- There is reasonable suspicion the requestor has malicious intent.
