# Game Theory — Analytical Meta-Skill

A collection of reusable analytical lenses for reasoning about human behavior, institutions, and geopolitical events through the framework of **players, rules, and incentives** — plus the laws, diagnostics, and critiques that build on it.

## Problem

Most analyses of human and geopolitical behavior fall into one of five weak frames: religion, biology, race/culture, economics, or liberal progress narrative. Each captures something partial. None of them predict outcomes reliably, and each tends to moralize rather than diagnose.

## Solution

Treat any situation — a market, a marriage, an institution, a war, a civilization — as a **game** with identifiable players, rules, and incentives, embedded in a **superstructure** (demographics, economics, culture, politics, religion) that determines which game is actually being played. Add a small set of named **laws** and **diagnostic patterns** that recur across scales, and you have a toolkit that:

1. reveals the *real* game behind the stated one,
2. predicts outcomes more reliably than moral or ideological frames,
3. and transfers across domains — dating markets, schools, banks, empires, and live conflicts all share structural primitives.

## Usage Examples

**Example 1 — framework fundamentals**

> User: Why do women in South Korea have fewer than one child on average?

The skill loads `framework-fundamentals`, identifies players (educated women, men, state, corporations), rules (competitive exam state, one-corporation labor market, materialistic status code), incentives (status through Samsung-track child, not multiple children), and diagnoses this as superstructure-3 (overpopulated / status-driven) dating game collapsing fertility.

**Example 2 — the four laws**

> User: Can a small power like Iran actually withstand a US strike?

The skill loads `the-four-laws` → applies Law of Asymmetry (empire's mass decays into rent-seeking, insurgent's EOC rises) and Law of Escalation (control > dominance; decapitation strikes often galvanize rather than defeat). Reasons about chokepoint geography, cost ratios, and martyrdom incentives.

**Example 3 — institutional unmasking**

> User: What's really going on with the Epstein network?

The skill loads `institutional-unmasking` → applies host/parasite model (nation-state as host, transnational elite network as parasite), consciousness-as-wealth, layered-power diagnostic, intra-elite civil war reading.

**Example 4 — applied scenario**

> User: Help me think through the Trump "World Order" endgame.

The skill loads `references/applied-scenario-analysis/MetaSkill.md` and composes it with the laws and civilizational-lifecycle diagnostics, using `references/applied-scenario-analysis/references/lecture-18-trump-world.md` as a worked example: resource-geography fortress thesis, controlled-demolition reading of global-order breakdown, Dugin's Third Rome template.

## How to Use This Collection

- **Start shallow.** Read the root `SKILL.md` (this file) and the router.
- **Pick the lens.** Let the router dispatch to one sub-skill for the current question.
- **Compose when needed.** Applied scenario analysis almost always composes with `framework-fundamentals` + one or more laws + civilizational-lifecycle.
- **Reference lectures are source material, not the skill itself.** Each sub-skill's `references/` holds the lecture notes the lens draws from. Cite them to ground reasoning, but the lens — not the lecture — is what's being applied.

## Intellectual Posture

- **Game theory gives better questions, not final answers.** The instructor's own words: the framework is a research guide, not an oracle.
- **Identify the game before moralizing.** Behavior that looks irrational is usually rational once the correct game (often status, proximity, or survival) is identified.
- **Track superstructure first.** Demographics, wealth distribution, religious/ideological scripts, and external threats set which game is being played. Change the superstructure and you change the game.
- **Hold conclusions loosely.** The source material is opinionated and in places contentious. Use the analytical primitives; interrogate the specific claims.
