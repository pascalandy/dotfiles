## Zoroastrianism — The World's First Great Religion

### Overview

This lecture introduces Zoroastra (Zarathustra) as arguably the most influential person in human history, the creator of Zoroastrianism — the world's first great religion — which gave rise to the Persian Achaemenid Empire and laid the structural foundation for Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. The lecture explores the cosmology behind Zoroastrianism, its central concept of **Asha** (truth/virtue), and draws connections to Plato, Kant, Rumi, and Nietzsche to illuminate what Asha truly means.

---

### The Intuitive Understanding of the Universe

#### Main Idea

Before introducing Zoroastrianism, the lecturer establishes how humans have traditionally understood the cosmos. The universe is understood as fundamentally conscious, constantly vibrating. Lower, slower vibrations give rise to matter and to human bodies. Our bodies inhabit the material universe, but our minds inhabit the spiritual universe. We are in constant dialogue with this conscious universe.

#### Supporting Material: The Monad and the Candle Metaphor

God is called the **Monad** (also "the One" or "Nus") — a perfect being. The problem with perfection is that it lacks imagination and creativity. So when God emanated, he created humans — material, imperfect beings who feel pain, make mistakes, suffer, and die. Through this fallibility, humans become creative. Both God and humans are in a "process of becoming" — co-creators moving toward perfection, eternity, and infinity.

The system has key characteristics:

- **Free will** — Only through complete freedom can true creativity exist. If the Monad controls us, nothing is genuinely creative; it's all planned.
- **The individual** — What matters is what happens inside each person. Creativity arises individually, not collectively.
- **Reincarnation and eternity** — When you die, consciousness returns to the spirit world for reflection. If you lived virtuously, you ascend to a higher plane (closer to the Monad). If you did harm, you descend to a lower realm and confront the hurt you caused. This is the origin of the ideas of heaven and hell.

The **candle metaphor**: Imagine a candle surrounded by mirrors. Each mirror reflects the candle. The Monad is in each of us; we are all part of the Monad. What matters is what happens to us individually.

**Connection**: This cosmological framework is presented as the intuitive baseline that all humans are born with — the pre-existing understanding that Zoroastra would later formalize into a structured religion.

### The Rise of War, Patriarchy, and Property

#### Main Idea

As populations grew, war emerged. War gave rise to **patriarchy** and **property** — soldiers were incentivized to fight with promises of wives, household mastery, and material spoils. These three forces — war, patriarchy, property — are interlinked and gave rise to the concept of **capital**.

The Bronze Age represented the height of capital, and therefore the height of war, slavery, corruption, violence, and evil. In response to this darkness, **prophets** emerged — figures who reminded humanity of its divine origin and its connection to the Monad.

> "We must remember that we are part of the monad, and that we're here to celebrate life, not to destroy life. To be creative, not to be destructive. To love, not to make war."

**Connection**: This historical context explains why Zoroastra appeared when he did — as a response to the moral collapse of the Bronze Age.

---

### Zoroastra and the Birth of Zoroastrianism

#### Main Idea

Zoroastra was a priest, likely from Northern Iran, who lived somewhere between 2000 BCE and 1000 BCE (probably toward the end of the Bronze Age). He was disgusted by the corruption of priests who had abandoned their role as teachers and spiritual guides in favor of money and false teachings. He wandered, spreading a new message.

Northern Iran at that time was a colonial mining region — far from the wealth centers of Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Levant. It was a place of extreme human suffering, comparable to modern exploitative mining operations. People were kidnapped and enslaved to work mines for the Bronze Age economy.

#### Supporting Material: The Polytheistic Context

The existing system was polytheistic — people fought for their gods, made sacrifices, and believed that if they gave enough, their gods would grant them victory in war. The gods were indifferent to good and evil.

Zoroastra reimagined this system. He placed **Ahura Mazda** ("Lord of Wisdom") at the top of a new hierarchy — essentially the Monad. Ahura Mazda is represented by fire. In Chinese, Zoroastrianism translates to "the religion of white fire" — a religion of wisdom.

**Connection**: Zoroastra didn't invent spirituality from nothing; he restructured an existing polytheistic system into something that pointed back toward the Monad, toward wisdom and virtue.

### Asha and Druj — The Core Duality

#### Main Idea

Zoroastra taught that the war of heaven and hell is within us. Two forces divide every person:

- **Asha** — truth, virtue, righteousness
- **Druj** — the lie, falsehood

The Greeks admired the Persians for three things: horse riding, archery, and telling the truth. But Asha is far more than simple truth-telling — it is a complete system of virtue. By being virtuous, you become closer to Ahura Mazda and become his representative on Earth.

> "Asha is the best of all that is good and Asha is happiness. Happiness belongs to the one who follows the righteous path for the sake of the best righteousness."

**Connection**: The duality of Asha and Druj becomes the engine of the entire Zoroastrian moral framework — and later, the template for monotheistic morality worldwide.

### The Categorical Imperative — Illuminating Asha

#### Main Idea

To clarify what Asha means in practice, the lecturer introduces Immanuel Kant's **Categorical Imperative** (18th century) as a parallel framework. Three principles:

1. **The Law of Universality** — Act as though whatever you do, everyone in the world will immediately do as well. If you're violent, everyone becomes violent. If you curse God, everyone curses God. Would you want to live in that world? This is higher than the Golden Rule ("do unto others..."). It asks you to imagine that you are God, and everything you do is reflected throughout the universe.

2. **Free Will** — Whatever you do cannot be coerced. Virtue must come from your own desire, will, and choice. Even if someone chooses evil, the principle of free will must be maintained.

3. **Humans as the End** — Humans are never a means to an end. Every individual life is as valuable as all human life together. You cannot sacrifice one person for the sake of others.

**Connection**: These three principles together approximate the concept of Asha and produce three revolutionary ideas:

- **The individual** — What matters is what's inside you, regardless of what everyone else does
- **Free choice** — Never feel forced; act because you will it
- **Truth** — The Monad will know; your family, community, and nation don't matter — only what you feel in your heart

---

### Monotheism and Zoroastrianism's Legacy

#### Main Idea

These concepts — the individual, free choice, truth — constitute the structure of **monotheism**. Zoroastrianism directly gave birth to three religions: Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, representing two to three billion people on Earth.

> "That's why I say Zoroastrian is the most important individual who's ever lived. Because he will create the structure for monotheism."

Zoroastra also profoundly influenced Greek philosophy. The Greeks didn't just see him as a poet — they considered him the first scientist, astronomer, philosopher, and magician. Their sentiment was one of reverence.

### Plato's Allegory of the Cave — A Zoroastrian Metaphor

#### Main Idea

Plato took Zoroastrian ideas and created the **Allegory of the Cave**. Prisoners are chained in a cave, unable to move, staring at shadows on a wall cast by a fire behind them. They name these shadows and call them reality. One day, a prisoner's chains vanish. He stumbles out into sunlight (Asha, Ahura Mazda). He's blinded at first — used to the darkness. Over time, he sees the world's beauty: birds, trees, animals.

But this isn't enough. Knowing the truth while others remain imprisoned is incomplete. True Asha demands going back down to tell others, to help them break their chains and see the truth.

> "Asha is not just self-realization, self-truth. It is helping others see the truth for themselves."

#### Supporting Material: The Choir Metaphor

Another way to understand Asha: a choir. Ahura Mazda is singing a song. We must sing along. If we do, the world is harmonized and beautiful. If most of us are out of tune, we need to get into tune.

**Connection**: Both metaphors — the cave and the choir — emphasize that Asha is active, not passive. It requires both personal transformation and the responsibility to transform others. The problem, of course, is that when you return to the cave and tell people their world is a shadow prison, they think you're crazy.

### The Gathas — Zoroastra's Own Words

#### Main Idea

Zoroastra composed poetry called the **Gathas** in the **Avestan** language. He didn't write them (everyone was illiterate); he sang them. Key themes from translated excerpts:

- **Individual judgment**: What your family, clan, or tribe does doesn't matter. When you die, you face Ahura Mazda alone.

> "Listen with your own ears, with a bright mind. Choose truth from false creed, each person for his own self before the final judgment comes."

- **The twin spirits**: Every person contains both good and bad. Virtue requires unity of thought, word, and action — good thoughts, good words, good deeds.

> "Now the two primal spirits who reveal themselves in vision as twins are the better and the bad in thought and word and action. Between these two, the wise ones choose, alright, the foolish one, not so."

- **Choosing good in an evil world**: Even in a world run by **Davas** (demons) who chose violence, you can and must choose to be good. God will know.

- **Free will and individuality**: These are the core concepts. You always have the choice to resist. Never say you're being coerced.

**Connection**: The Gathas establish the foundational theology that would echo through Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — individual moral responsibility, final judgment, the struggle between good and evil, and the centrality of free will.

### Rumi — The Reincarnation of Zoroastra

#### Main Idea

**Rumi** (13th century), considered the greatest Persian poet, is presented as a spiritual reincarnation of Zoroastra — a poet-prophet speaking the same truths in a different era. His poetry illuminates Zoroastrian cosmology.

#### Supporting Material: Selected Rumi Poetry

On seeing through God's eyes rather than your own:

> "I see so deeply with myself, not needing my eyes. I can see everything clearly. Why would I want to bother my eyes again now that I see the world through his eyes."

On the unity of all religions and the divine spark within:

> "Not Christian or Jew or Muslim. Not Hindu, Buddhist, Sufi or Zen... I belong to the beloved, have seen the two worlds as one."

On the soul's origin and the prison of material existence:

> "My soul is from elsewhere, I'm sure of that, and I intend to end up there... Meanwhile, I'm like a bird from another continent, sitting in this aviary."

**Connection**: Rumi's poetry is used to show that all prophets speak the same essential truth — we are all children of God, labels and organized religions are human constructs, and what matters is our individual connection to the divine.

#### Supporting Material: The Joke About God and Satan

God tells Satan: "Humans have discovered religion, therefore I have won." Satan replies: "Yeah, but then I'll just organize it."

**Connection**: The lecturer supports religion but critiques organized religion. The differences between religions exist because those in charge need to differentiate in order to justify their power and exploit followers. At the core, all religions point to the same divine connection.

### Nietzsche — Thus Spoke Zarathustra

#### Main Idea

**Friedrich Nietzsche** is presented as another reincarnation of Zoroastra. Nietzsche wrote *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* based on experiences during long mountain walks where he felt "seized" by a force compelling him to speak — he believed this force was Zoroastra himself. Nietzsche channels Zoroastrian ideas through a modern lens.

#### Supporting Material: The Prologue — Leaving the Cave

Zarathustra spent ten years in a mountain cave enjoying solitude and wisdom. But then his heart changed. He realized wisdom hoarded is incomplete:

> "I'm weary of my wisdom like the bee that hath gathered too much honey. I need hands outstretched to take it."

**Connection**: This is the Allegory of the Cave retold — the enlightened person must descend to share truth with others. Asha cannot be achieved alone.

#### Supporting Material: Virtue From Vice

> "Out of thy poisons brewed thou balsam for thyself... nothing evil grow in thee any longer unless it be the evil that grow out of the conflict of thy virtues."

The central message: virtue must come from vice. Good can only emerge from evil. We are born in the Druj so that we may discover Asha. Living in a time of evil is God's gift — the opportunity to create good.

**Connection**: This is not passive acceptance of evil but a framework for transformation. Without vice, there can be no virtue.

#### Supporting Material: Self-Destruction as Path to Truth

> "Ready must thou be to burn thyself in thy own flame... How could thou become new if thou had not first become ashes?"

True knowledge requires constantly destroying what you think you know. Leave your comfort zone, negate your teachers, rebuild your understanding from scratch. The truth must be yours — fought for every day.

> "Be ashamed of him. Perhaps he have deceived you. The man of knowledge must be able not only to love his enemies, but also hate his friends."

**Connection**: This is the most radical expression of Asha — you must eventually destroy your teachers and rebuild knowledge independently. Clinging to authority prevents genuine enlightenment.

#### Supporting Material: Against Organized Religion and Academic Priesthood

Nietzsche attacks both organized religion and academic institutions:

> "I should only believe in a god that would know how to dance."

Organized religion is somber, controlling, and denies free will. Asha recognizes the world as beautiful — dance, sing, make love, laugh. If a priest tells you to meditate your entire life to avoid sin, he's a devil denying your capacity for action and love.

> "I've departed from the house of the scholars and the door have I also slammed behind me... Freedom do I love in the air over fresh soil."

Universities are temples for the comfort of intellectual priests who have departed from reality. True knowledge is found in the everyday, with ordinary people, through suffering.

**Connection**: Both passages reinforce that Asha is active, joyful, and found in lived experience — not in institutional authority or somber obedience.

### Zoroastrianism vs. Buddhism

#### Main Idea

The lecturer draws a sharp distinction: Zoroastrianism is not Buddhism. While the structures and frameworks are similar, Zoroastrianism insists that **compassion is not enough** — you must act. Justice requires action. If you see injustice, you must speak out. Indifference, even compassionate indifference, is complicity.

> "Being indifferent is simply being complicit in the system."

**Connection**: This distinction explains why civilizations influenced by Zoroastrianism emphasized creativity and action — the religion demands engagement with the world, not withdrawal from it.

### Q&A — Applying Asha

#### On Hitler and Forgiveness

A student asks whether Hitler's actions could count as Asha if he genuinely believed he was pursuing justice. The lecturer's response: Ahura Mazda is complete forgiveness, compassion, and love. Whatever we do in this world will be forgiven in the end — everyone will be forgiven. There is no hell except what we create in our hearts. The core of Asha is: don't worry about other people, worry about yourself. Don't compare yourself to others.

Two things keep us from Asha: **ego** (wanting to be liked) and **fear** (fear of rejection, of offending others, of being politically incorrect).

> "What matters is my own individual pursuit of the truth. What others think of me does not matter."

#### On Whether Asha Changes Over Time

Asha is not static. It changes as you grow. God is creativity — moving toward Asha means constantly reinventing yourself and discovering new ideas about yourself. There is no finale. Follow your heart, dissipate your fears, and Asha will come naturally.

#### On Whether We Ever Fully Reach Asha

There is no endpoint. It is a constant process of becoming. We are reincarnated to continue moving closer to Asha across lifetimes. Even if you reached it, others haven't — you have a duty to help them. The universe finds meaning in the collective striving.

> "There can be no perfection. There can just be only a process of becoming."

### Course Correction and Teaching Philosophy

The lecturer corrects a previous error about the story of Rachel and Jacob from the Bible (Jacob received Rachel immediately but then worked seven years to pay the debt, not the reverse). He acknowledges making frequent small errors and frames this as part of the course's nature — it's about questions, not answers.

He also corrects a larger previous claim: Christianity is not the first monotheistic religion; Zoroastrianism is. He models the very Zoroastrian principle he teaches — constantly destroying and rebuilding one's knowledge.

> "If you really want to pursue knowledge, you must be willing to, as Nietzsche, as Zarathustra says, constantly destroy yourself, to burn yourself into ashes so that you may build yourself anew."

### Key Takeaways

- Zoroastrianism is the structural foundation for Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — the world's first monotheistic religion
- **Asha** (truth/virtue) is the central concept: good thoughts, good words, good deeds — unified in action
- The Categorical Imperative (universality, free will, humans as ends) closely maps onto Asha
- Asha demands action, not just contemplation — seeing injustice and staying silent is complicity
- There is no final destination; life is a constant process of becoming, of moving toward virtue
- True knowledge requires destroying what you think you know and rebuilding from lived experience
- The two obstacles to Asha are ego and fear
- All prophets speak the same essential truth; differences between religions are organizational, not spiritual

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Zoroastrianism**: The world's first great religion, founded by Zoroastra, centered on the concepts of Asha and Ahura Mazda
- **Ahura Mazda**: "Lord of Wisdom" — the supreme god in Zoroastrianism, represented by fire
- **Asha**: Truth, virtue, righteousness — the force that draws one toward Ahura Mazda; encompasses good thoughts, good words, good deeds
- **Druj**: The lie, falsehood — the opposing force to Asha
- **Monad**: The One, the divine source from which all consciousness emanates
- **Davas**: Demons or forces that lead one toward the Druj
- **Gathas**: The sacred hymns/poetry composed by Zoroastra in Avestan
- **Avestan**: The ancient language in which Zoroastra's teachings were composed
- **Categorical Imperative**: Kant's highest moral law — act as though your actions would be reflected universally
- **Allegory of the Cave**: Plato's metaphor (rooted in Zoroastrian ideas) about escaping shadow reality to discover truth
