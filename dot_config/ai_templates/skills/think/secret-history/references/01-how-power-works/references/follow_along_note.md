## How Power Works: Money, Individual, and Nation State

### Overview

This is the opening lecture of a semester-long course that introduces students to a critical framework for understanding how power operates in the modern world. The professor establishes three core illusions—money, the individual, and the nation state—as constructs designed to control human behavior. The lecture draws on Immanuel Kant's epistemology, the history of banking, ancient Greek philosophy, and the origins of public schooling to argue that most of what students have been taught is a system of brainwashing maintained by powerful interests.

---

### Immanuel Kant and the Nature of Reality

#### Main Idea

The professor opens with **Immanuel Kant**, described as "the greatest philosopher in western history," to establish the epistemological foundation for the entire course. Kant's central teaching: we can never access **objective reality**. There is a real world out there—what Kant calls the **noumena** (or "things in themselves")—but it is permanently inaccessible to us. Why? Because our senses warp and restructure reality into something we can process, which Kant calls the **phenomena** ("things that appear to us").

> "Reality is what we imagine it to be. There is no objective reality. Life is a constant process, a constant act of imagination."

#### Supporting Material: Time and Space

The professor uses time and space as Kant's own example. Time and space do not exist outside of us—they do not exist in reality itself. But for us to understand reality, we need to impose time and space onto it. They are structures our minds add in order to make sense of the noumena.

**Connection**: This example makes Kant's abstract distinction concrete. If even time and space are mental impositions, then everything we perceive is already filtered and constructed. This sets up the course's central project: learning to see through constructed realities.

### Course Structure: Past, Present, Future

#### Main Idea

The professor outlines the semester's structure across three temporal dimensions:

- **Present**: Study **geopolitics** (wars in Ukraine, the Middle East) and try to build an **analytical model** explaining why these events happen
- **Future**: Use the analytical model to make **predictions**—predictions that test whether the model is correct
- **Past**: If the model proves accurate, apply it backward to uncover what the professor calls "the secret history of the world"

The professor compares this method directly to how artificial intelligence models are validated—you test them against reality.

> "All the history that you learn in school, all that history that you think you know, it is false. The history that you believe, it is a system implanted into your brains by powerful people."

The overarching question driving the semester: **How does power work?** The professor promises that understanding this leads to liberation and freedom from manipulation.

### Example One: Money

#### Main Idea

The professor conducts a thought experiment to demonstrate that **money is created out of nothing** and that the belief in money's scarcity is a deliberate illusion maintained by powerful institutions.

#### Supporting Material: The Bank Thought Experiment

The professor role-plays as a bank:

1. Students deposit $5 million into the bank at 1% interest
2. The bank lends $5 million to a friend building a restaurant at 10% interest
3. The bank makes a 9% profit margin

The professor then asks: how much money is in the bank? Students answer logically—zero (or, accounting for fractional reserve requirements, $500,000). The professor reveals the actual answer: **$9.5 million**. The bank has created money out of nothing.

A student raises the concept of **fractional reserve banking**—the requirement to keep a percentage (say 10%) of deposits on hand. The professor acknowledges this but shows it doesn't change the fundamental reality: banks create money that didn't previously exist.

#### Supporting Material: Chinese Banks

The professor points to Chinese banks (Bank of China, ICBC, Agricultural Bank of China) becoming the world's largest banks over the past twenty years. This didn't happen because Chinese people got rich and deposited money. It happened because these banks **created money out of nothing** to finance China's massive infrastructure projects—skyscrapers, roads, and other developments.

**Connection**: This real-world example validates the thought experiment. Banks are allowed to create money. It happens routinely at enormous scale.

#### Supporting Material: The History of Banking and Receipts

The professor traces the origin of the banking system:

1. **Merchants** originally engaged in finance because trade required money
2. Wealthy merchants started **banks** to support trade, dealing primarily in **gold**
3. Depositors would leave gold with the bank and receive a **receipt** (contract) promising the gold back on demand
4. These receipts became tradable instruments—easier to carry than gold, facilitating trade across borders (e.g., Italy to England)
5. When banks began lending, they issued **receipts instead of gold** to borrowers—because receipts were more convenient
6. This doubled the effective money supply: the gold stayed in the vault, but receipts for twice the gold now circulated

> "By doing this, what I've done is I've doubled the gold in my bank. I've created money out of nothing."

#### Supporting Material: Why Money Wasn't Needed Historically

For most of human history, ordinary people didn't need money. They lived in villages, grew crops, and **bartered** directly. Money served primarily symbolic purposes—especially to represent **debts that could never be repaid**. The most common example: if you killed someone, you paid their family money as a symbolic acknowledgment that the debt (a life) could never truly be settled.

Merchants used money because they needed to facilitate trade across distances—and they all knew each other, so trust wasn't an issue.

#### The Problem: Bank Runs and Sovereign Default

Two critical vulnerabilities in the early banking system:

1. **Bank runs**: If too many receipt holders demand their gold simultaneously, the bank goes bankrupt. The receipts exceed the actual gold reserves.
2. **Sovereign default**: Banks primarily lent to **kings and nobles** who needed money for wars. Kings could die in battle (losing the investment) or simply refuse to repay.

#### Supporting Material: The Rise of Central Banking

To mitigate these risks, banks formed **cartels**—partnerships typically sealed through **intermarriage**. These cartels provided:

- **Liquidity backup**: If one bank faced a run, partner banks could provide gold
- **Enforcement power**: If a king refused to repay, the cartel of banks could collectively fund a new enemy to overthrow the king and recover the debt

> "This system is what we call today central banking. And what you will learn in this class is central banking controls the world today."

#### The Core Argument: Why Poverty Exists

The professor pushes students to confront an uncomfortable question: if banks can print infinite money, why does poverty exist? Students repeatedly fall back on the answer of "scarcity," which the professor identifies as proof of brainwashing.

The professor's answer: **poverty is deliberately maintained by the powerful**. The reasoning:

- The purpose of printing money is not to distribute wealth
- The purpose is to create the **illusion that money is valuable**
- For money to seem valuable, people must **want** it desperately
- For people to want money, they must see others suffering without it
- **Artificial misery** drives the workforce

> "If there weren't poor people, you wouldn't wanna be rich. It's only because you see people suffer that you would want to go make money for yourself."

> "Poverty isn't what you do to yourself, it is what the powerful do to you."

The professor extends this logic to **economic crises** (stock market crashes, recessions): their purpose is to **destroy money** so that scarcity feels real again. And to **wars**: wars exist to **destroy wealth**, not to fight over scarce resources, reinforcing the illusion that resources are limited.

#### Supporting Material: World of Warcraft Analogy

The professor compares real-world economics to a video game like World of Warcraft. In the game, players grind for credits to buy items. If the game developers simply made credits rain from the sky, nobody would play. The real world operates on the same principle—if money were acknowledged as infinite, nobody would work.

**Connection**: This analogy bridges the abstract banking argument to students' lived experience with game economies, making the manipulation mechanism intuitive.

A student challenges: aren't physical resources like food and land genuinely scarce? The professor responds: visit any garbage dump and observe the massive food waste. There is enough food to feed everyone. Hunger is an **artificial crisis**. Food isn't infinite, but it's **abundant**—more than enough exists.

The professor acknowledges he cannot fully convince students in one session—they remain stuck in the "scarcity mindset"—and moves on.

### Example Two: Happiness and the Individual

#### Main Idea

The professor argues that the modern concept of **individual happiness** is historically anomalous and is itself a tool of control. For most of human history, happiness was understood as **collective**—rooted in community well-being, generosity, and shared prosperity.

#### Supporting Material: Student Responses on Happiness

Students list what makes them happy: money, power, freedom, relationships, love, video games, vacations. The professor observes that every answer is framed in terms of **individual** happiness—and notes this is unique in human history.

#### Supporting Material: The Feast Tradition

Throughout history and across cultures, if someone suddenly became wealthy, the first thing they did was **hold a big feast for the entire community**. What mattered was **reputation within the community** and **generosity**. The professor connects this to Chinese culture specifically: someone from a village who makes money in Beijing returns home and hosts a feast for everyone.

**Connection**: This contrasts sharply with the modern instinct (which students express) to hoard wealth—"dig a hole and put it inside" or put it in a bank. The feast tradition illustrates that the default human relationship to wealth was communal, not individual.

#### Main Idea: The Individual Is a Modern Invention

The professor argues that **the concept of the individual did not exist** for most of human history. A person was inseparable from family and community. The worst punishment was not death but **exile/banishment**—being told "you no longer belong to this tribe." This was worse than execution because identity was collective.

### Two Worldviews: Polytheism vs. Science

#### Main Idea

The professor presents two competing frameworks for understanding human agency and asks students which is more accurate:

**Worldview 1 (Polytheism)**: Humans have no individual agency. Powerful gods (Apollo, Dionysus) control events, and they themselves are controlled by more powerful forces (fate, fortune) and even more ancient forces (anger, pride). Everything that happens to you is ultimately beyond your control.

**Worldview 2 (Neuroscience/Psychology)**: We are synapses generating memories. Our understanding comes from experience, controlled by DNA and environment. Through therapy, reflection, and exercises, we can control our emotions and therefore our fate.

Students unanimously choose Worldview 2. The professor declares they are wrong—**Worldview 1 is the more accurate reflection of reality**.

#### Why the Powerful Prefer Worldview 2

The professor identifies three benefits of the scientific/individualist worldview for those in power:

1. **Individual responsibility**: People blame themselves for their failures rather than the system. "The source of all your problems is within you."
2. **Harder work**: If you believe you control your fate, you work harder. Under polytheism, you might relax since the gods will do what they want anyway.
3. **Powerlessness through atomization**: This is the key insight. Worldview 2 makes people **incapable of collective action**. If all your problems are individual, you never identify with others or organize collectively. And collective action is the only way to change the world. Instead, isolated miserable individuals "play video games or watch porn all day."

> "Two is designed to make you think the source of all your problems is within you. So you ignore what other people do and think."

#### Supporting Material: Psychiatry Critique

The professor offers a practical illustration: if you feel sad and depressed, what should you do? Students know the "correct" answers—walk around, talk to a friend. But the system teaches you to see a psychiatrist, who will give you drugs. The professor argues psychiatry is designed not to cure but to **make you dependent on authority**.

#### Supporting Material: Greek Gods and Eudaimonia

In the polytheistic system, gods are not benevolent authority figures. They are vengeful, angry, proud, and chaotic. A king rules not because he's good but because the gods arbitrarily favor him—and that favor can vanish. This is radically different from the modern assumption that authority figures act in our best interest.

The polytheistic worldview produced the concept of **eudaimonia** ("flourishing"): since you cannot control your fate, live each day to the absolute best of your ability. "Seize the day." The professor argues this is why the ancient Greeks produced superior literature and philosophy (Plato, Homer, Aeschylus, Euripides)—they were driven by flourishing rather than mere pleasure-seeking.

> "In the first system, even though it sounds like we have no control, we have no agency, it inspires us to live to the best of our ability. The second system... enslaves us."

**Key contrast**:

| Polytheism | Modern Science |
|---|---|
| No individual control | Full individual control |
| Inspires flourishing (eudaimonia) | Produces pleasure-seeking |
| Enables collective identity | Atomizes into isolated individuals |
| Gods are chaotic, not benevolent | Authority figures claim benevolence |
| Produced Greek golden age | Produces dependency and misery |

### Example Three: School and the Nation State

#### Main Idea

The professor argues that **school is not designed to teach knowledge or skills**. School is a **brainwashing institution** whose primary purpose is to implant belief in the **nation state**.

#### Supporting Material: Apprenticeship vs. Formal Education

The professor poses a scenario: a twelve-year-old wants to become a doctor. Two paths:

1. **Apprenticeship**: Work in a hospital for ten years under a mentor. Start by washing floors, observe, gradually learn to treat patients.
2. **Formal education**: Attend the best schools, then Harvard, then Harvard Medical School.

At age thirty, who is the better doctor? Students agree: the apprentice. The professor adds that in the apprenticeship system, **anyone can become a doctor**—you don't need to be "smart." All human beings are born with the capacity to learn anything. The school system creates artificial categories of "smart" and "stupid" that determine life outcomes.

#### Supporting Material: Three Societies with Mandatory Schooling

The professor identifies the three societies that first introduced **mandatory free public schooling**:

1. **Sparta** — children as young as five or six were put in schools where they were beaten by older children
2. **The Aztecs** — free compulsory education for all children
3. **Prussia** — the model that all modern school systems are based on

What do all three have in common? They were fundamentally **war-making societies**. Sparta was the only Greek city-state with compulsory education because it was dedicated to war. The Aztecs were the greatest military power in pre-Columbian Central America. Prussia had the greatest military in Europe for centuries.

**Connection**: School exists to prepare populations for war and obedience, not for learning.

#### Why Other Societies Rejected Schools

The professor asks: why didn't other societies copy this model? Because **taking children from parents** is the worst thing you can do to a family. School is designed to **separate children from parents**. Why? Because:

- A child with their parent feels **secure**
- A secure child questions authority, thinks independently, and resists brainwashing
- A child separated from parents feels **insecure, anxious, afraid**
- An insecure child transfers trust to the **teacher**, becoming receptive to whatever the teacher says

> "It's only because you've been taken away from your parent that you are now willing to be brainwashed."

A student asks: aren't parents brainwashed too? The professor agrees—parents are brainwashed. But the point is that parental presence provides emotional security, and security enables independent thinking and willingness to challenge authority.

#### What Schools Brainwash You Into: The Nation State

The purpose of school brainwashing is to implant belief in the **nation state**. Schools teach language, history, and geography to make you believe that abstract political entities—"mother China," "the United States," "France"—are real persons you must love, serve, and die for.

The professor points out the absurdity: historically, people identified with their locality—"I'm a Beijinger" or "I'm from Haidian." The idea that you are the same as someone from Yunnan, Tibet, or Guangxi—with whom you share nothing in common—is an artificial construction.

> "History is the false memory of a nation state."

### The Unifying Theory: Monotheism and Alchemy

#### Main Idea

The professor reveals the semester's central thesis. The three constructs—**money**, **the individual**, and **the nation state**—all originate from a single revolution in human thought: **monotheism** (the concept of one true God). Judaism, Christianity, and Islam are identified as "basically the same religion." Monotheism is described as so powerful an idea that it created the entire modern world.

#### Power as Alchemy

The professor defines **power** through the metaphor of **alchemy**—historically, the pursuit of turning lead into gold, or nothing into everything. While alchemy is dismissed in science classes as pseudoscience, the professor argues that power has achieved alchemy:

- **Money**: nothing turned into everything
- **The individual**: a concept that didn't exist turned into the foundation of modern life
- **The nation state**: an abstraction turned into something people kill and die for

> "Power is the capacity to turn nothing into everything. Power is the capacity to make you believe that money is valuable. That the individual can lead to happiness. That the nation state exists."

#### A Note on Accident and Hope

The professor adds a crucial qualifier: this system was **not deliberately designed from the start**. It was "an accident of the human imagination"—created because humans didn't know what they were doing. This implies hope: if the human imagination created this system accidentally, then a **controlled imagination** could create a new system oriented toward **eudaimonia** and human flourishing.

### Key Takeaways

- Reality is constructed through perception; train your imagination to see more clearly
- Banks create money from nothing—money's scarcity is an engineered illusion
- Poverty is artificially maintained to make money seem valuable and to keep people working
- The concept of the individual is historically recent and functions to prevent collective action
- Ancient polytheistic worldviews produced greater human flourishing than modern scientific individualism
- Schools exist to separate children from parents and implant belief in the nation state
- All three control mechanisms (money, individual, nation state) originate from monotheism
- The course is about **how to think**, not what to think

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Noumena**: Kant's term for objective reality, the "things in themselves," which are permanently inaccessible to human perception
- **Phenomena**: The world as it appears to us after being structured by our senses and perception
- **Fractional reserve system**: Banking regulation requiring banks to keep a percentage of deposits on hand; the rest can be lent out
- **Bank run**: When too many depositors demand their money simultaneously, exceeding the bank's actual reserves
- **Central banking**: System of banking cartels that coordinate to manage risk, enforce debts, and create money
- **Eudaimonia**: Greek concept of "flourishing"—living life to the best of one's ability, especially given the uncontrollable nature of fate
- **Polytheism**: Religious worldview with many gods who control human fate; the dominant system for most of human history
- **Monotheism**: The belief in one true God; identified as the origin of money, the individual, and the nation state
- **Alchemy**: The pursuit of turning nothing into everything; used as a metaphor for how power operates
- **Nation state**: A modern political construct that school systems are designed to make citizens believe in and serve
