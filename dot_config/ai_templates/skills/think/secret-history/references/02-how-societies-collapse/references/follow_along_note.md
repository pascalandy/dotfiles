## Why Societies Rise and Fall

### Overview

This is a university lecture that builds on previous classes about monotheism and modernity. The lecturer presents three theories explaining societal decline—financialization, elite overproduction, and civilizational life cycles—then synthesizes them into a unified framework for understanding how societies rise, decline, and collapse. The lecture concludes with five predictions for the Western world over the next five to twenty years.

---

### Recap: Monotheism and Modernity

The lecturer briefly reviews the previous class. **Monotheism**—the idea of one true God—represented an intellectual revolution in human history. From it emerged three ideas that became dominant paradigms: **money**, the **individual**, and the **nation state**. These ideas existed before monotheism but became dominant through it. Together, they produced modernity—the world we live in today. This came with both benefits and significant consequences.

The lecturer frames the current class as continuing "the story of humanity" with a specific question: why do societies rise and why do they fall?

He acknowledges the early classes feel abstract and theoretical but explains they are developing ethical models to understand the world. Concrete examples will come later.

---

### Signs of Societal Decline

The lecturer asserts that we currently live in a world in decline, then solicits and catalogs the evidence. The signs mentioned (drawn from class discussion):

- **Conflict and wars** — Ukraine, the Middle East, Southeast Asia (Thailand, Myanmar), and Trump's announced plans to send US troops to Mexico and Venezuela
- **Environmental degradation** — climate change, toxic air, water, and land from excessive pressure on the environment
- **Declining attitudes toward work** — unemployment rising, but also declining motivation and enthusiasm. Referenced as **Bailan** (摆烂) in China, meaning "let it rot," and **quiet quitting** in America
- **Lower birth rates** — happening worldwide; if young people refuse to marry and have children, societies eventually die off. Only exceptions noted: Israel and Georgia (described as small, rare cases)
- **Lower standard of living** — inflation means people earn less and can afford less; wages decline while prices rise
- **Health issues** — diabetes, high blood pressure, increased disease generally
- **Higher stress** — greater anxiety, depression, emotional drain
- **Greater pessimism** — people are less optimistic
- **Debt (public and private)** — governments face **fiscal crises** (spending more than tax revenue), families heavily in debt worldwide
- **Lower cohesion and trust** — people no longer trust each other; less willingness to help strangers (e.g., helping someone bleeding on the street)
- **Immigration** (primarily a Western world issue) — reduces cohesion and standard of living; discussed as a sign of decline
- **Housing prices** — young people can no longer afford homes, true in China and globally
- **Pension crisis** — predicted to become a major government problem in five to ten years

The lecturer notes that for societies on the rise, these indicators are simply reversed: higher trust, savings over debt, better health, optimism, high birth rates, employment.

### Theory 1: Financialization (Thomas Piketty)

#### Main Idea

The first theory comes from French economist **Thomas Piketty**, author of *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (described as an easy but illuminating read). Piketty argues that capitalism naturally transitions through three phases:

1. **Consumer capitalism** — society focuses on creating goods consumers want to buy. This is an era of rapid wealth generation.
2. **Financial capitalism** — rather than building factories, money goes into the stock market. The focus shifts from generating wealth to generating money.
3. **Monopoly capitalism** — a few companies control everything, because it is more profitable to be a monopoly than to compete. This is the current era.

The lecturer emphasizes a distinction from the previous class: **wealth and money are not the same thing**. Consumer capitalism generates wealth; financial capitalism generates money.

#### Supporting Material: The Factory vs. Stock Market Example

The lecturer illustrates with a concrete scenario. An entrepreneur wants to make money. First, they build a factory—this creates real wealth because it requires hiring workers, building technology, producing goods people want. But once the entrepreneur has money, they put it in the stock market to grow it faster. If too many people do this, no one is creating real wealth.

Piketty's statistical analysis of income tax data found:

- The **real economy** in late-stage capitalism grows at about **2%**
- The **financial economy** grows at about **5%**

Applied practically: if you have a million dollars, opening a restaurant yields roughly $20,000/year. Putting it in the stock market yields $50,000/year. So everyone chooses the stock market, which inflates financial markets while the real economy stagnates—producing unemployment, debt, and disengagement from work.

**Connection**: Financialization explains the economic symptoms of decline (unemployment, debt, declining work ethic) as a natural, inevitable byproduct of capitalism's internal logic rather than moral failure.

> "Wealth and money are not the same thing."

### Theory 2: Elite Overproduction (Peter Turchin)

#### Main Idea

Historian **Peter Turchin** spent decades studying why societies rise and fall—examining the Roman Empire, the French Revolution, and many other cases. His conclusion: societies decline and collapse because of **elite overproduction**—too many powerful people competing for limited positions of power.

#### Supporting Material: Rat Utopia (John B. Calhoun)

To explain elite overproduction, the lecturer introduces the **Rat Utopia** experiments conducted by American scientist **John B. Calhoun** after World War II. Calhoun wanted to understand what living in abundance and security meant for populations.

The experiment: a sealed room with unlimited food, water, and housing for a colony of rats (starting with ~10, growing to 400-500). Initially the rats thrived, but no matter how Calhoun configured the experiment over twenty years, the rats always ended up killing each other.

The lecturer's interpretation: the rats were not competing for food—they were competing for **status**. In nature, alpha males fight for mating rights, and losers can leave to establish their own colonies. In the sealed room, there was nowhere to go. Status is a **zero-sum game**—one winner, one loser—and without an escape valve, the fighting continues until death.

**Connection**: This maps directly onto elite overproduction. Elite families' children fight for positions of power. When there are too many of them and nowhere for them to go, conflict escalates. The lecturer uses a contemporary Chinese example: too many graduates of Peking University (Beida) and Tsinghua University all want to be "the big boss," but there aren't enough positions.

Turchin's conclusion: elite overproduction inevitably leads to war or revolution and the collapse of society, with no way around it.

### Theory 3: Civilizational Life Cycle (Oswald Spengler)

#### Main Idea

German philosopher **Oswald Spengler** argues that civilizations follow the same life cycle as human beings: birth, growth, maturity, death. No civilization can escape this cycle.

#### The Four Stages

The progression of successful civilizations:

1. **Village** → 2. **Town** → 3. **City** → 4. **Mega City**

Examples: the Romans and the Qing dynasty followed this path.

#### What Changes Along the Cycle

The key concept is **increased abstraction**—becoming progressively removed from reality.

- **Village**: life is simple. People understand where their food comes from. People work hard, are united, have a collective mentality, help each other. Mothers have 10-11 children because kids are free labor.
- **Mega City**: people have no idea where their food or drinks come from. Everything is abstract.

The shift in what holds society together:

- **Village**: emotions, tradition, relationships
- **Mega City**: money

> "Money is the greatest abstraction. The problem with money is that it means we don't have to ever trust each other."

In the village, if you get sick, everyone helps. In the city, you go to the hospital and pay the doctor. Neighbors don't care. Family doesn't have to help. People become **individualized** and **atomized**.

In the mega city: people stop working hard, want immigrants to do the work, stop caring about others, and stop having children. The mega city represents the death of civilization.

The lecturer lists current mega cities as examples: Beijing, Shanghai, Washington DC, New York, Paris, London.

Spengler's conclusion: there is nothing anyone can do about this. It is a natural life cycle.

#### Student Question: What About External Threats?

A student asks whether a universal external threat (like an alien invasion) could force people to unite and extend the cycle. The lecturer's answer: **no**. By the mega city stage, people are too selfish and self-absorbed to recognize external threats, and they no longer trust each other enough to cooperate.

> "What would happen is certain factions of humans would try to align with the aliens to conquer everyone else."

### The Unified Model: Structure of Society

The lecturer now combines all three theories into a single working framework, with an explicit caveat:

> "I warn you that this framework, there are a lot of problems with it, but it is a useful tool for us to analyze society."

#### The Three Groups

Society is structured in three layers:

1. **The Elite Families** (the core) — the founding families of a nation. Not many: maybe 10 to 100. The Roman Empire, controlling most of Europe, Anatolia, and Egypt, was run by roughly 200 families.
2. **The Middle Class** (the managers) — also called **scholar officials** (historical China), the **professional managerial class (PMC)**, or the **petit bourgeoisie**. They manage society on behalf of the elite.
3. **The People** (the outer edge) — the massive population that generates the actual wealth of society.

**Metaphor**: Society is like a corporation. The families are the owners. The people are the workers. The middle class are the managers.

#### The Three Pillars of Power

The elite families control society through three mechanisms:

1. **Finance** — central banking (discussed in previous class)
2. **Religion** — controlling what people believe; the modern form is science and technology
3. **Intelligence** — spies

The nexus of these three pillars allows the elite to control all aspects of society: schools, military, government, media, culture, crime/mafia, corporations.

The people, being massive in number, generate the energy (wealth) that powers the system. The system is designed to extract maximum energy from the people.

---

### The Rise, Decline, and Collapse Cycle

#### The Dynamic Between Groups

**During Rise**: The elite families are happy to let the middle class (managers) run society. The managers provide feedback: "treat workers better and they'll work harder." This produces democracy. People feel they have a voice. Everyone is happy.

**During Decline**: Elite overproduction kicks in. Too many elite children want positions of power (want to "spend money from the corporation"). The corporation goes into debt. The managers, rather than advocating for the people, begin to **exploit** them. Why? Because the middle class engages in **rent-seeking behavior**—using their position to extract wealth from others.

Examples of rent-seeking:

- Owning an apartment in Beijing and collecting rent
- Being a lawyer—only lawyers can work in the court system, so anyone with a legal dispute must pay them

The managers don't do much real work but have comfortable lives. When the corporation is in trouble, they fear being fired, so they push workers harder to prove their worth.

**During Collapse**: Conflict within the elite worsens. Factions form. Each faction recruits elements of the middle class, which recruit elements of the people. Society fractures into warring factions → civil war or revolution.

#### Foreign Invasion and Mercenaries

The lecturer addresses why external threats don't prevent collapse. Foreign invasions often happen not because outsiders spot weakness, but because **one internal faction invites mercenaries** as part of its power struggle. The mercenaries then realize they can seize power for themselves. By the collapse phase, society is too insular and racked with infighting to respond to external threats.

---

### Characteristics Across the Three Phases

The lecturer maps several dimensions across rise, decline, and collapse:

| Dimension | Rise | Decline | Collapse |
|-----------|------|---------|----------|
| **System** | Open (democratic, meritocratic) | Bureaucratic | Authoritarian |
| **Mechanism** | Consent | Deception | Coercion |
| **Priority** | Unity | Stability | Survival |
| **Criticism** | Welcomed, rewarded | Discouraged | Punished (critics become enemies) |

#### Supporting Material: The Lunch Example

To illustrate consent → deception → coercion:

- **Consent**: "Where should we go for lunch? Let's vote. Majority wins."
- **Deception**: "Let's go to McDonald's—Jack Ma will be there, or it's free hamburgers today." (Lying to get what you want.)
- **Coercion**: "I'm the teacher and I'll beat you up if you don't listen."

#### Historical Note on Openness

The lecturer makes a striking comparison: in the 1950s, both America (a democracy) and China (communist) were **open societies**. You could criticize leaders and were encouraged to do so. China was "as democratic back then as the United States." The point: openness is not about political systems but about where a society sits in its developmental cycle. Young societies are naturally open, democratic, meritocratic, and receptive to criticism.

---

### The Timeline of Collapse

The lecturer describes the shape of the cycle:

- **Rise**: steep
- **Decline**: slow
- **Collapse**: sudden

People assume decline will continue gradually, but collapse happens fast. The reason: the system in its authoritarian phase cannot survive **external shocks**—a perfect storm of simultaneous crises (plague, drought, war, revolution all at once).

Society might prepare for any single crisis, but not for all of them simultaneously. And by the authoritarian phase, those who point out problems are treated as enemies rather than heroes, so the system loses its capacity for self-correction.

> "In the rise phase, those who criticize society are the heroes. In the collapse phase, those who speak out are the enemies of society."

### Five Predictions for the Western World (5-20 Years)

The lecturer applies the framework to make five predictions (not necessarily sequential, but all expected to occur):

1. **Decline of democracy and freedom** — The US and Europe become more authoritarian. Already visible: Trump using military to resolve domestic issues.
2. **Economic collapse** — As people feel disinvested from the system, they work less; the economy collapses.
3. **Increase in immigration** — Governments respond to declining productivity by bringing in immigrants to replace the unwilling workforce.
4. **Civil conflict** — The native population resists; people fight each other in the streets.
5. **Stupid foreign wars** — Governments, fearing the fighting will turn against them, send people off to pointless overseas wars as a distraction.

#### Student Question: War as Distraction?

A student asks whether governments use war to distract from internal collapse. The lecturer confirms: that is precisely the function of war in this model.

> "If you don't send the people to war, they will revolt against you. So you send them off to war to distract them, to get rid of them."

### The Lecturer's Epistemological Frame

The lecturer repeatedly emphasizes the nature of what he's doing:

- The framework is **simplistic, inaccurate, and imprecise**—but useful as an analytical tool
- The course is about understanding **how power works**, not about morality

> "There's no right and wrong. There's no logic... I'm trying to explain to you how people in power think and behave."

> "Morality doesn't matter here. It's about power. We're trying to understand how the world works. And when we do... then maybe we can work together and build a more just world. But first, we need to figure out how the world really, really works."

The model's validity rests on whether its predictions come true. If they do, the model has some accuracy and can be refined. If leaders suddenly cooperate peacefully, the model is wrong.

---

### Student Q&A: Why Don't Elite Families Just Have Fewer Children?

A student asks whether elite overproduction could be avoided if families had fewer children. The lecturer's response: elite families are specifically incentivized to have many children because **passing on power requires heirs**. Everyone wants to marry into elite families, and marriage alliances between elite families are essential for maintaining power. The middle class might reduce family size, but the elite will not.

### Key Takeaways

- Societies follow a natural life cycle of rise, decline, and collapse—driven by financialization, elite overproduction, and increasing abstraction
- The three-layer model (elite families → middle class → people) shifts from consent-based to deception-based to coercion-based governance as decline progresses
- Collapse is sudden, not gradual, triggered by a perfect storm of simultaneous crises
- External threats (even alien invasions) cannot reverse decline because internal factions will exploit them rather than unite
- The framework is a working analytical tool, not a moral argument—it prioritizes understanding power over judging it

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Financialization**: the transition from productive capitalism (making goods) to financial capitalism (making money from money) to monopoly capitalism (few companies control everything)
- **Elite overproduction**: too many powerful people competing for limited positions of power, leading to factional conflict and eventual collapse
- **Civilizational life cycle**: Spengler's theory that civilizations pass through village → town → city → mega city, with increasing abstraction and inevitable death
- **Abstraction**: being progressively removed from reality; not understanding where goods, services, or systems come from
- **Rent-seeking behavior**: exploiting a position of power or access to extract wealth from others without creating new value
- **Zero-sum game**: a situation where one person's gain is another's loss; no mutual benefit possible
- **Fiscal crisis**: when a government spends more than it takes in through tax revenue
- **Bailan** (摆烂): Chinese term meaning "let it rot"—an attitude of disengagement from effort
- **Professional Managerial Class (PMC)**: the middle layer of society that manages on behalf of the elite; also called petit bourgeoisie or scholar officials
- **External shocks**: a perfect storm of simultaneous crises that a weakened society cannot withstand
