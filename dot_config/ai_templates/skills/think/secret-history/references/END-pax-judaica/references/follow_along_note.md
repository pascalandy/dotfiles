## All Roads Lead to Jerusalem — The Grand Thesis

### Overview

### A Theory of Truth

#### Main Idea

Before presenting the narrative, the professor establishes a framework for evaluating any theory or idea. He calls it a **theory of truth** — a theory approaches truth if it satisfies three tests:

1. **Connects the past** — Does it help put known historical events into a coherent story?
2. **Explains the present** — Does it help us better understand the world we live in?
3. **Predicts the future** — Can we derive predictions from the narrative, and do those predictions come true?

He immediately adds a caveat: even if predictions come true, that does not prove the theory is correct, because **correlation does not necessarily mean causation**. The theory remains a model, not proof.

> "Just because things turn out the way that we predict doesn't necessarily mean that our theory is a hundred percent correct."

The professor frames the entire lecture as speculation — calling it fun, telling students to doubt what he says, and acknowledging some will call it conspiracy theory. But he insists it passes all three tests of truth.
