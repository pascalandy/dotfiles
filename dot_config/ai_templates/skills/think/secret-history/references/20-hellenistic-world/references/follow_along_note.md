## The Greeks and the Patterns of Empire

### Overview

This is a university lecture covering three foundational principles of geopolitics, then applying them to trace the arc from the Warring States period in China through Mesopotamia, the Persian-Greek wars, the rise and fall of Athens as an empire, Alexander the Great's conquests, and the Hellenistic age. The lecture emphasizes recurring patterns in how empires rise, stagnate, and are overtaken by energized borderlands.

---

### Review: Three Principles of Geopolitics

#### Principle 1: Competition Within States > Competition Between States

The primary driver of conflict is not rivalry between nations but rivalry inside them. The historian **Peter Turchin** studied the fall of the Roman Republic, the French Revolution, and other crises, and identified a pattern he calls **elite overproduction**: too many elites competing for limited positions of status and power.

The conflict is not between rich and poor. It is between those who have a lot and those who have some but want more — typically upper nobility versus lower nobility. Julius Caesar is cited as an example: he was of the lower nobility trying to reach the upper nobility.

> "The conflict in states is not between rich and poor, but between the have a lot and have some, but want more."

#### Principle 2: The Elite Has No Loyalty

Elites are not loyal to their people, their state, or even their families. They will do whatever is necessary to accumulate power. The lecturer frames this as the "Game of Thrones" dynamic — people who love power will sacrifice parents, siblings, even children to amass it.

#### Principle 3: War Is About Maintaining the Status Quo (Equilibrium)

War often serves to preserve existing power structures rather than to conquer. World War I is offered as an example: tens of millions died in what appeared to be idiotic military strategy, but it makes more sense if understood as a mechanism for managing excess male population who might otherwise fuel domestic revolution.

**Connection**: These three principles form the analytical lens applied to every historical case in the lecture. The lecturer argues that once you internalize them, the movement of history becomes far more legible.

### Application: China's Warring States Period

#### The Setup

During the Warring States period, three powerful states — the **Zhao**, the **Wei**, and the **Chu** — were the obvious candidates to unite China. They had rivers for transport, large populations, and fertile ground. Meanwhile, a poor, backward, isolated people called the **Qin** were considered barbarians.

#### How the Qin Won

The three major states had reached **equilibrium**. War between them was ritualistic — like a chess game — designed not to destroy each other but to reduce domestic elite conflict. This made them stable, but stability became **stagnation**.

The Qin benefited from this system in three ways:

1. **Military knowledge transfer**: The Qin were hired as mercenaries and learned all the military innovations of the major states
2. **Talent migration**: Ambitious lower nobility from the major states, blocked from advancement at home, migrated to the Qin
3. **Energy**: The Qin received a massive infusion of innovation, talent, and drive

The major states could not imagine the Qin overtaking them because people inside an equilibrium cannot conceive of change.

> "Once you reach an equilibrium, the people inside the equilibrium become lazy, stupid, and arrogant."

The lecturer suggests the lower nobility in all three states probably supported the Qin takeover because it created new opportunities for advancement.

### Application: Mesopotamia

#### The Pattern Repeats

The first city, **Uruk**, expanded by establishing colonies along the Tigris and Euphrates, which eventually became warring states that reached their own equilibrium. A critical rule maintained this equilibrium: you could wage war but could not destroy another city's **temple**, because the temple housed the patron god and — more importantly — all the city's gold and wealth. This prevented any state from truly expanding.

#### The Equilibrium Breaks

Energy flowed into a poor, marginal area called **Umma**. A merchant named **Lugal-zage-si** broke the unimaginable taboo: he destroyed a temple. The gods did not strike him down. Now he had access to wealth and quickly overwhelmed the other city-states.

The other city-states called for help from mercenaries — the **Akkadians** — who came and conquered the entire region under **Sargon**, establishing the Akkadian Empire. But this empire also reached equilibrium, talent flowed elsewhere, and successive powers arose: Babylonians, Assyrians, and eventually from the Zagros Mountains, the **Persians** with their Zoroastrian religion.

The Persians conquered Anatolia and Egypt, creating a new border area: the **Greeks**.

### Empire vs. Borderlands Framework

The lecturer introduces a structural comparison that recurs throughout history:

| Empire | Borderlands |
|--------|-------------|
| **Mass** — large population | **Energy** — people work hard, not afraid to die |
| **Organization** — centralized bureaucracy | **Openness** — eager to learn from others |
| **Death** — can absorb losses, keep sending armies | **Cohesion** — united as a people |

The empire's people are often slaves, its bureaucracy stagnant and insular, and its population divided. The borderlands' advantages — energy, openness, cohesion — explain why throughout history barbarians are able to conquer empires.

### Greece: Geography and Innovation

#### Why Greece Was Special

Greece is poor, mountainous, and hilly — nearly impossible terrain for an empire to develop. This forced the existence of thousands of small, competing **poleis** (city-states), which drove massive innovation. Greece's position by the sea gave access to Persia (the wealthiest region), Egypt, and northern Africa. The Greeks absorbed knowledge from Egypt, Anatolia, Mesopotamia, Persia, and Northern Europe.

Three factors made Greece the center of innovation:

1. **Homer** — considered the greatest poet-prophet in human history; author of the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*, the two founding texts of Greek civilization
2. **The Alphabet** — enabled writing down and transmitting thoughts across time and space
3. **The Polis** — a system of government where all citizens participated in politics; thousands existed, often small (1,000–10,000 people)

### Sparta vs. Athens

#### Sparta: The Conservative Land Power

- Located on the flat **Peloponnese**, enabling agriculture
- Agriculture required slaves — the **Helots**, conquered surrounding peoples turned into serfs
- Became a **war society** centered on producing warriors
- **Conservative** because leaving to conquer elsewhere risked Helot rebellion
- Comparable to China in its structure
- **Oligarchy**: only those born to both a Spartan father and mother were citizens
- Helots outnumbered Spartans ten to one — both the major resource and the major danger

The Spartan training system:

1. Boys taken from parents at age 5–7 to create psychological hardness through anxiety and separation
2. Subjected to daily beatings living among other boys
3. At 14–15, assigned a mentor (~30 years old) who became a lover — building military camaraderie
4. At 18–19, graduation ceremony: hide in fields at night, wait for a Helot to break curfew, kill them

> "This is a brutal, brutal people. But guess what, guys? The Romans were the same thing. The Aztecs were the same thing."

#### Athens: The Expansionist Naval Democracy

- Poor farmland but a good harbor; mountainous territory
- Could grow **olives** but little else
- Had no choice but to be expansionist — strong navy, focused on trade and piracy
- **Democracy** because in a navy, everyone could row — no special equipment or decades of training needed
- Contrast with Sparta: hoplites required expensive equipment and long training, so only a few fought, producing oligarchy
- The Greek rule: if you fight for us, you can vote and participate in politics

**Connection**: Geography determined military form, which determined political structure. Flat land → agriculture → slaves → land army → oligarchy. Harbor → navy → mass participation → democracy.

#### Military Innovations Shaped by Geography

- Greeks developed **hoplites** — heavy infantry forming a wall to overwhelm enemies; suited to hilly terrain
- Persians developed **horse archers** — suited to wide, flat terrain
- Horse archers couldn't function in Greece's hilly terrain
- Hoplites couldn't function in Persia's wide plains
- This geographic mismatch is why the Persians lost when they invaded Greece

### The Persian Wars

#### Battle of Marathon (490 BCE)

The Athenians had been committing piracy and encouraging Greeks under Persian rule not to pay taxes. The first major military confrontation resulted in Athenian victory because the Persians couldn't use horse archers in Greek terrain and were forced into infantry combat — the Greeks' strength.

#### Xerxes' Invasion (The Purple Campaign)

The Persians created a land bridge on the Bosphorus using ships and crossed into Greece.

**Battle of Thermopylae**: 300 Spartans made their famous stand. The Persians eventually crushed them, beheaded King **Leonidas**, and put his head on a pike — a major insult to the Greeks. The Persians then burned Athens.

At this point, the war should have been over. But the Athenians said: "A polis is not a place. It's a people." They got on their ships and sailed away, continuing the war.

The lecturer emphasizes repeatedly: the Persians had won. All they needed to do was land their navy in Sparta, where the Helots would rise up and destroy Sparta from within. Game over.

#### Battle of Salamis

Instead, **Xerxes** — lazy, stupid, and arrogant — wanted a glorious climactic battle so he could build a monument to himself. He sent his navy into the narrow straits of Salamis against the Greek navy led by **Themistocles**.

Themistocles had earlier convinced Athens to use silver from a mine to build a navy in preparation for the Persian invasion.

The Greek **triremes** were fast, good at ramming, with marines on top. The Persians converged head-on against the Greeks — violating the principle of never fighting the Greeks face-on. The Athenians destroyed the Persian navy.

> "Don't fight the Greeks head on."

The lecturer expresses exasperation at the Persian decision-making multiple times: the war was already won, and they threw it away.

### The Clash of Values: Empire vs. Borderlands in Their Own Words

The lecturer reads three primary source passages to show the transformation from borderland virtue to imperial arrogance.

#### Persian Envoy to Athens (After Salamis)

The Persians appeal to mass and inevitability:

> "Why are you so mad as to wage war against the king? For neither can you get the better of him nor can you resist him forever... even if you should get the better of us... another much more numerous will come against you."

#### Athenian Response

The Athenians appeal to liberty and cohesion:

> "We ourselves are aware that the power of the Medes is far greater than ours... But nevertheless, being ardent for liberty, we will defend ourselves... so long as the sun shall continue in the same course as now, we will never make terms with Xerxes."

**Connection**: These two passages crystallize the empire vs. borderlands framework — mass/organization/death versus energy/openness/cohesion.

#### Pausanias After the Battle of Plataea

After the Spartans destroyed the remaining Persian forces under **Mardonius** at the Battle of Plataea, a soldier suggested putting Mardonius's head on a pike to avenge Leonidas. Pausanias refused:

> "You have thrown all down to nothing by advising me to insult a dead body and saying that if I do so I shall increase my fame, which is more fit for barbarians to do than Greeks, and which we abhor even in them."

**Connection**: At this point in history, the Greeks defined themselves by virtue, faith, and loyalty — the opposite of what they would become as an empire. The lecturer flags this explicitly: "Remember this. We're gonna compare and contrast this when they become an empire."

### The Athenian Empire

#### From Alliance to Empire: The Delian League

After defeating the Persians, the Athenians proposed a defensive alliance — the **Delian League** — pooling resources on the island of **Delos** as a defense fund against future Persian invasion. Compared to NATO.

What actually happened:

1. The Athenians moved all the gold from Delos to Athens ("It's probably safer with us")
2. The alliance became an empire where everyone paid tribute to Athens
3. Greek city-states went from paying tribute to Persia to paying tribute to Athens — "probably a worse deal for everyone"
4. Athens spent the money on the **Parthenon** and a pure gold statue of Athena

#### Athens Becomes What It Fought Against

The lecturer corrects a common academic reading of the **Peloponnesian War**. **Thucydides** wrote that Sparta was the hegemon and Athens the rising power, so they were bound to clash. The lecturer argues the opposite: Athens was the hegemon and empire, its allies were rebelling, and Athens dragged Sparta into the war. Sparta did not want this war.

The **Melian Dialogue** shows the transformation complete. Athens tells a subject city:

> "Right, as the world goes, is only in question between equals in power, while the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must."

**Connection**: The Athenians who told the Persians "we fight for liberty" now tell their own subjects "shut up and obey." The borderland that defeated an empire through virtue became an empire that ruled through force.

#### Pericles' Funeral Oration — A Speech About Empire

The lecturer challenges the standard reading that this is a celebration of democracy. He argues it is a speech about empire. To parents of dead soldiers, Pericles says:

> "Fortunate indeed are they who draw for their lot a death so glorious... you who are still of an age to beget children must bear up in the hope of having others in their stead. Not only will they help you to forget those whom you have lost, but will be to the state at once a reinforcement and a security."

The lecturer's reading: your sons were worthless, but dying for empire made them valuable. Now have more children to serve the empire. Orphaned children will be raised as soldiers to fight and die like their fathers.

#### Strategic Stagnation and the Plague

Pericles refused to launch an aggressive war against Sparta because defeating Sparta would destroy the equilibrium. Instead, he forced everyone inside Athens's city walls to avoid facing Sparta's land army. The overcrowding caused a **plague** that killed a third of the Athenian population, including Pericles and his two sons.

#### The Sicilian Expedition and Athens's Fall

As a "mafia state" collecting tribute, Athens needed ever more money as the war dragged on. They invaded **Sicily** — a wealthy target uninvolved in the war — and lost catastrophically. This dragged **Syracuse** (with its navy) into the war alongside Sparta. The Persians also joined against Athens. Athens's allies turned against it. The combined forces blockaded Athens from its wheat supply at the **Black Sea**, forcing surrender.

Sparta chose not to destroy Athens — because destroying Athens would upset the equilibrium and allow Thebes or other powers to rise.

---

### The Peloponnesian War's Strategic Paradox

Both sides fought in ways that preserved the status quo rather than seeking decisive victory:

- **Athens** could easily destroy Sparta by landing forces in the Peloponnese and letting the Helots rise up — but didn't want to destabilize the system
- **Sparta** could massively increase its army by freeing the Helots — but that would destroy its own oligarchy and social order
- Both sides avoided the most effective strategies because winning too completely would destroy the equilibrium they depended on

### The Rise of Macedonia

#### Philip II

The lecturer calls **Philip II** one of the greatest military geniuses in human history, overshadowed only by his more famous son. While Athens, Thebes, and Sparta maintained their equilibrium, Macedonia absorbed all their innovations — the same Warring States pattern.

A critical fact the lecturer emphasizes as under-taught: **Philip II and Aristotle were childhood best friends**. They grew up together. Philip went to Greece to learn military innovations; Aristotle went to learn intellectual innovations (studying under Plato at the Academy).

#### The Macedonian Phalanx

Philip II's genius was combining innovations that other states kept separate:

- Persian horse archers (mobility)
- Greek hoplites (heavy infantry)
- Theban tactical innovations

He combined these into one coordinated military package. The key enabler was **meritocracy** — promoting soldiers based on performance rather than birth, which overturned the aristocratic social hierarchy and maximized morale across all ranks.

#### The Anvil and Hammer

Philip II's signature tactical innovation:

1. **Anvil**: Infantry locks with enemy infantry, fixing them in place
2. **Hammer**: Cavalry sweeps in from behind to smash the enemy

No other army had encountered this combination, and there was no effective counter.

#### Battle of Chaeronea

Philip II defeated a combined Athenian-Theban force and united mainland Greece for the first time. He then prepared to invade Persia — sending spies and diplomats to negotiate with local elites.

#### Philip II's Assassination (336 BCE)

At his daughter's wedding, Philip's bodyguard killed him. The lecturer attributes this to his wife **Olympias** (Alexander's mother, not Macedonian). Philip had married a Macedonian woman and produced a son who would displace Alexander as heir. Olympias — who had taught Alexander from childhood that he was the son of God, not Philip — had Philip killed, then killed the new wife and son.

### Alexander the Great

#### The Conquest

Alexander took Philip's army and conquered the Persian Empire in approximately ten years. He moved rapidly with a small army, which the lecturer says proves he had cooperation from local Persian elites — lower nobility helping overthrow the equilibrium.

#### Alexander's Self-Conception

Two early acts reveal his psychology:

1. **Troy**: He went to Troy first because he saw himself as the new Achilles. He had memorized the *Iliad* and carried it everywhere. He even tied an enemy to his chariot and dragged him, reenacting Achilles' treatment of Hector.
2. **Oracle at Ammon** (Egypt): He detoured deep into the desert to visit an oracle who confirmed: you are not the son of Philip II but the son of **Zeus-Ammon** (king of gods in both Greek and Egyptian traditions). You were born to rule the world.

This explains why Alexander rejected Darius's offer of territory and half the Persian treasury. Philip II would have accepted. Alexander's appetite for conquest was insatiable.

> "All these great conquerors believe that they are divinity, that they are the son of God, and they came on Earth in order to unify the world."

The lecturer lists Genghis Khan, Julius Caesar, and Napoleon as sharing this psychology.

#### The Burning of Thebes and Persepolis

Alexander committed atrocities that appear random but the lecturer argues may have been **ritual sacrifices** to prove his divinity — acts with diplomatic and military purpose, not senseless violence.

#### Alexander's Downfall

Alexander systematically killed his father's loyal generals — **Parmenion** and **Cleitus** — to consolidate his own power. Meanwhile, he was incorporating Persian elites into his army and administration, angering his Macedonian soldiers. The remaining generals conspired and poisoned him.

### Plato vs. Aristotle: The Philosophical Foundation of Empire

#### Plato's Philosophy

For Plato, what matters is the **spirit, the mind, the soul** — the immaterial. The Good (or God) emanates and creates the shadow world we live in. Our goal is to return to the Good by seeking knowledge, truth, and mastering **sacred geometry** — the shapes that underpin reality.

#### Aristotle's Counter-Philosophy

Aristotle — probably building on others' ideas — focused exclusively on the **material world**. The world operates through **cause and effect**. This implies a **Prime Mover** (God) who initiated the first cause, but the Prime Mover is irrelevant after that. What matters is the **telos** (purpose, end goal).

Everyone has a purpose: students learn, teachers teach. Fulfill your purpose = be good. The imperial application: work hard and produce energy for the empire.

#### Why This Matters

This conflict is the underlying tension in all of Western philosophy:

| Plato (Rationalist) | Aristotle (Empiricist) |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| Mind over matter | Mind doesn't matter, focus on matter |
| Spirit, soul, immaterial | Material world, cause and effect |
| Seek truth and return to the Good | Fulfill your purpose (telos) |
| Leads to contemplation | Leads to productivity |
| Descartes inherits this | British Empire adopts this |

The lecturer argues Plato's philosophy is unsuitable for empire because it encourages contemplation over production. Aristotle's philosophy is a **tool of empire** — it directs individuals to maximize their energy and output.

> "If you're an empire, you don't want this crap... If you're just sitting around reading books, that's — we don't want that."

### The Hellenistic Age

#### Division of Alexander's Empire

After Alexander's assassination, his generals divided the empire into four parts:

- **Ptolemy** → Egypt (wealthiest)
- **Macedon** → Greek mainland
- **Anatolia** → Asia Minor
- **Seleucid Empire** → Mesopotamia and east

#### Governing With Too Few Greeks

The Greeks didn't have enough people to govern the empire. Their solution:

1. Built Greek cities everywhere to settle veterans and attract more Greeks — all following the same template (amphitheater, gymnasium, agora)
2. Recruited **Persian bureaucrats** (effective administrators)
3. Recruited **Jews** as a foreign administrative class

#### Cultural Imperialism

The Greeks controlled their empire through culture and religion rather than pure military force. The **Library of Alexandria**, created by Ptolemy I and II, was presented as a center of learning but functioned as a **tool of empire** — systematizing and standardizing Greek knowledge into textbooks taught throughout the world.

The Library anecdote: The Egyptians asked Athens to borrow original manuscripts of Euripides, Aeschylus, and Sophocles. Athens demanded a massive deposit. The Egyptians paid, copied the manuscripts, then told Athens to keep the deposit. The Athenians were furious.

> "A university is not really about education. It's not really about literacy or culture. It's really about a tool of empire to create a cultural understanding of the world that allows the empire to rule over people."

The Greeks needed to explain to Egyptians — who had given Greece much of its science and mathematics — why barbarian Greeks now ruled them. The answer: **center Greek culture, downplay Egyptian culture**.

#### The Septuagint and Jewish Integration

Egypt needed to control the Levant, where Jews were loyal to the former Persian Empire. The Greeks bribed Jewish priests by offering to translate the Hebrew Bible into Greek — the **Septuagint**. This flattered Jewish tradition while integrating Jews into the Greek imperial system. Eventually, a third of Alexandria's population became Jewish.

#### Syncretism and the Birth of Christianity

The Hellenistic age brought together three major post–Bronze Age cultures:

- **Greeks** (rulers)
- **Jews** (administrators and scholars)
- **Persians** (bureaucrats)

Their **syncretism** — the combining of religious and philosophical traditions — created the basis for Western civilization and gave birth to **Christianity**.

### Key Takeaways

- The recurring historical pattern: stable states reach equilibrium → become stagnant → energy/talent/innovation flows to marginal borderlands → borderlands conquer the center
- People inside an equilibrium cannot imagine change — they become "lazy, stupid, and arrogant"
- The virtues that allow a people to defeat an empire (energy, openness, cohesion) are abandoned once they become an empire themselves
- War between roughly equal powers is often fought to maintain equilibrium, not to achieve decisive victory
- Cultural imperialism (libraries, standardized knowledge, language) is as important as military conquest in sustaining empire
- The Plato vs. Aristotle divide — rationalism vs. empiricism, contemplation vs. productivity — is the foundational philosophical tension of Western civilization

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Elite overproduction**: Too many elites competing for limited positions of status and power, driving domestic crisis
- **Equilibrium**: A balance point where things stay the same; people within it cannot imagine change
- **Polis**: Greek city-state where all citizens participate in politics
- **Hoplites**: Greek heavy infantry fighting in tight formation
- **Helots**: Enslaved population of Sparta who farmed the land
- **Delian League**: Defensive alliance that became the Athenian Empire
- **Telos**: Purpose or end goal (Aristotelian concept)
- **Prime Mover**: Aristotle's concept of God as the first cause, irrelevant after initiating motion
- **Syncretism**: Combining of religions and cultural traditions
- **Septuagint**: Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, commissioned by the Ptolemaic Empire
- **Anvil and Hammer**: Macedonian tactic — infantry fixes enemy in place, cavalry smashes from behind
- **Cultural imperialism**: Using culture, education, and language to control subject peoples
