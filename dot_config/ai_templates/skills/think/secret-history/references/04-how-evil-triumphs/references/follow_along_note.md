## How Evil Triumphs — Ritual Sacrifice, Transgression, and the Geist

### Overview

This is a university lecture that builds a speculative theoretical framework for understanding how power structures operate through transgression, ritual sacrifice, and group cohesion. The lecturer repeatedly emphasizes these are tools for thinking, not truths. The lecture moves from current events to a thought experiment, to historical analogies, to game theory, and finally into a deep metaphysical framework drawing on Kant, Hegel, Plato, and Dante.

---

### Opening Frame: This Is Speculation, Not Truth

The lecturer opens with a strong disclaimer: the class deals with disturbing topics, and everything discussed should be treated as theoretical tools rather than established fact.

> "Do not take this as gospel. Do not believe this is the truth. The entire point of this class is to help you... the theories and the ideas to better understand the world. These are tools. This is not truth."

He also notes that certain words cannot be spoken aloud due to YouTube censorship, and will be written on the board instead. This framing device recurs throughout — several key terms (genocide, child sacrifice, specific taboo acts) are gestured at but not spoken.

### Gaza as Ritual Sacrifice

#### Main Idea

The lecturer argues that what is happening in Gaza is not merely the censored word (implied: genocide), but something he considers even more disturbing — a **ritual sacrifice**. He claims this is a recurring pattern in human history where mass violence serves a religious or social-cohesion function.

#### Historical Precedents

- **Aztecs**: Temples containing thousands of human skulls. Mass murder of enemies conducted publicly before wars.
- **Phoenicians/Carthaginians**: Known for child sacrifice. Romans wrote histories condemning this practice.
- **Romans**: After major wars, captured enemy leaders were paraded through the streets in a ceremony called a **triumph**. At the Temple of Jupiter, enemies were strangled to death. The lecturer calls this human sacrifice disguised as military ceremony.

> "They didn't call that [sacrifice]. They disguised it, but we know for a fact that they did do this."

#### The "River Behind Your Back" Analogy

The lecturer draws on a famous Chinese military stratagem: forcing your own army to fight with a river at their backs, eliminating the option of retreat. Soldiers either drown or fight to the death — and most fight. This produces a surge of unified energy.

**Connection**: Israel's actions in Gaza function as the same logic. The "river" is the ultimate taboo — killing children. By crossing this line publicly, there is no exit. The nation must go all the way or face destruction. This forces internal unity.

A student asks: "What is the river for Israel?" The lecturer responds:

> "The river is a taboo. What's the worst thing you can do in modern society? Killing children. So there's no exit for Israel. You either go all the way or the world comes and destroys you anyway."

#### Why Do It Publicly?

The lecturer raises a striking point: Israel could accomplish its goals covertly (poisoning water, inducing cancer through environmental means). Instead, the violence is conducted openly, in front of the world. He argues this is intentional.

- Public atrocity unites the Israeli population through shared transgression
- It turns the entire world against Israel, which aligns with extreme Jewish eschatological belief — that at the end of the world, Israel will fight the entire world and God will ensure triumph
- Extremists within the Israeli government want to accelerate this eschatological timeline

### The Monkey Island Thought Experiment

#### Setup

One hundred men, ages 15–65, are mysteriously placed on an island. They come from different countries, speak different languages, have different ethnicities, and are all poor and uneducated. The island has one safe hilltop, but all resources (food, trees, water) are in territory controlled by infinite flesh-eating monkeys.

#### What Happens: The Emergence of Cohesion

Despite the hopelessness of the situation, the lecturer argues the opposite of despair occurs:

1. **Common language develops** — quickly, organically, out of necessity
2. **Stories are shared** — personal histories become a founding myth
3. **Religion emerges** — they come to believe God chose them to save the world
4. **Rituals develop** — including sexual rituals for bonding and intimacy, and religious ceremonies
5. **A leader is chosen** — through demonstrated sacrifice, not ideas

#### The Leadership Selection

Two candidates are contrasted:

- A 65-year-old wise man delivers an articulate, strategic speech
- A 15-year-old boy stands silently, pulls out a knife, cuts off his own hand without flinching

> "Who does everyone pick to be their leader? The second person, obviously. The man who has shown sacrifice and commitment and devotion to everyone else."

The old man becomes the adviser. The leader is the one most willing to die for the group.

#### Synchronicity and the Hive Mind

Over time, the group develops what the lecturer calls **synchronicity** — the ability to act in unison, to think as one. He compares this to a soccer team's coordination, calling it a **hive mind**.

He illustrates with two scenarios:

- **Telepathic awareness**: If one member wanders off and is attacked by monkeys, the others would sense it — the way a mother feels when her child is in danger thousands of miles away, even without being told
- **Self-sacrifice without hesitation**: The last person across a rope bridge cuts the rope, killing himself to save the nine who crossed. He compares this to soldiers in trench warfare jumping on grenades to save comrades

> "No one thinks about it. You just do it because what matters is saving your comrades."

#### Return to the Real World

After 20 years on Monkey Island, the hundred men are transported back to their original lives with full memories. They reunite, share their rituals and religion with their descendants. Over generations, they become the secret elite — the true controllers of power, regardless of who appears to hold public office.

A student asks: "What if when they return, there's already a leader and they're just peasants?" The lecturer responds:

> "On the surface, there are leaders, but the real power are you guys."

#### Funeral Rituals

Strange rituals develop on the island, including funeral practices where the dead are cut apart and consumed by the group. These reinforce unity and sacred commitment.

### Historical Analogies: Sparta, Thebes, Macedonia

#### Sparta

The Spartan education system (**agoge**) is described in stages:

1. **Hazing phase (ages 5–6)**: Boys are removed from homes and placed under older boys (age 10–11) who beat them relentlessly. This builds solidarity and brotherhood among the young boys — survival requires mutual support.
2. **Mentorship phase (puberty, ~11–13)**: Each boy is paired with an older soldier (~28–30) who becomes their mentor and sexual partner. This builds **hive mind** thinking — integration into a larger group identity.
3. **Graduation mission (age 18)**: Boys hide in fields at night and ambush **helots** (Spartan slaves) who violate curfew, slitting their throats. The lecturer calls this human sacrifice.

**Connection**: Only Sparta among thousands of Greek city-states practiced this system. Everyone else found it disgusting, but it made Sparta the dominant military power. The pattern mirrors the Monkey Island thought experiment — transgression creates cohesion creates synchronicity creates power.

#### Thebes: The Sacred Band

Thebes copied the Spartan system but made it **voluntary** and open to anyone (not just elites). The **Sacred Band of Thebes** consisted of 300 soldier-lovers who served as the vanguard in battle. They became the dominant military power after Sparta.

The lecturer emphasizes that in this historical period, there was no concept of "homosexuality" as an identity category. Sexual relationships between men were unremarkable and considered natural, especially during extended military campaigns.

#### Macedonia: Philip II and Alexander the Great

**Philip II** of Macedonia learned the Theban system and built the greatest army in the world. His son, **Alexander the Great**, used this army to conquer Persia.

At the **Battle of Chaeronea (338 BCE)**, Macedonia defeated the combined forces of Thebes and Athens. When all was lost, the Sacred Band made a final stand in the open field, blocking the Macedonian advance so the rest could retreat to the cities.

> "They did not fear death. They welcomed death. For them, what mattered was honor and sacrifice. And that's how you create the greatest army in the world."

### Game Theory and Transgression

#### Why Cheating Wins

In a game of a million people competing against each other, **game theory** says the best strategy is cheating — specifically, **secret coordination**.

- If two people coordinate, it forces others to form groups
- This creates an arms race of coordination
- The key advantage goes to those who can **coordinate without others knowing**

Methods of coordination include family, religion, shared ethnicity, common language — but these are all **visible** to others, which triggers counter-coordination.

#### Transgression as Secret Coordination

**Transgression** — the breaking of taboos, social norms, and laws — is presented as the ultimate method of secret coordination.

The lecturer proposes a core thesis:

> The greater the transgression, the greater the cohesion, which leads to synchronicity.

**Why it works**:

- Breaking rules together creates a shared secret
- If anyone betrays the group, everyone is destroyed — this enforces loyalty
- Transgression feels empowering and addictive

#### The Escalation Ladder

The lecturer walks through an escalation of transgressive acts:

1. **Pranking** — covering a school in toilet paper (mild transgression, builds group bonding)
2. **Petty theft** — stealing a one-dollar candy (terrifying at first because of internalized moral codes, but empowering once done)
3. **Greater transgressions** — each successful transgression creates desire for more
4. **Ultimate taboos** — the lecturer points to written words on the board (implied: child sacrifice and incest), identifying these as practices of secret societies that seek ultimate power

> "They do it because they believe that when you do this, it releases divine energy."

He claims secret societies perform these acts publicly within the group — creating both group unity and, in their belief system, access to divine power.

### Deep Theory: Kant, Hegel, Plato, Dante

The lecturer shifts to explain the metaphysical framework behind why transgression supposedly "works."

#### Kant: The Problem of Perception

Recap from a previous class:

- There is no direct access to objective reality
- Our brains are **filters** that add space and time to raw reality
- **Noumena** (things in themselves) = objective reality, unknowable
- **Phenomena** (things as we see them) = filtered reality, the world we experience
- Confirmed by neuroscience

This creates three problems:

1. What is the noumena?
2. Who gave us these filters?
3. If everyone has their own lens, how do we know we're seeing the same world?

#### Hegel: The Geist

**Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel** proposes a solution: the noumena is the **Geist**.

**Geist** is a German word usually translated as "spirit," but the lecturer breaks it down through three English derivatives:

- **Ghost** — a parallel, invisible reality alongside ours
- **Geyser** — a force constantly expanding, exploding, becoming
- **Gist** — the essence, the core of things

> "The real truth in the world is the geist. Not what we see, but what is invisible to us."

The Geist answers all three of Kant's problems:

1. The noumena is spiritual, not material — it is the Geist
2. The Geist gives us our filters
3. Because we all perceive through the Geist, we see the same world

#### Plato and Gnosticism: The Structure of the Geist

Drawing from Plato and **Gnosticism** (a religious tradition whose name means "knowledge"), the lecturer outlines a cosmological model:

1. **The Monad** (also called "the One" or "Nous") — the supreme source, like a spiritual sun
2. The Monad creates through breathing/vibration, producing **dyads** (pairs of forces)
3. Dyads create different realities — a multilayered, multidimensional universe
4. **Earth** is the outermost shell — the furthest point from the Monad

The purpose of life: **return to the Monad**.

The lecturer notes this maps directly onto Hinduism and Buddhism — Nirvana is the return to the Monad.

#### Two Paths Back to the Monad

**Plato's path**: The pursuit of knowledge, specifically geometry — the secret language of the universe. The lecturer notes this is elitist: only those who study at Plato's school could access the divine.

**Dante's path**: The pursuit of love. The Monad is love. Because the Monad created everything, a **divine spark** exists in all beings. This spark manifests as love. The more you love, the closer you return to the Monad.

> "Plato is like, you have to do calculus. And Dante's like, that's just ridiculous."

Dante's argument is democratic — everyone can love, but not everyone can do mathematics. A loving God would not create a system accessible only to elites.

#### The Problem of Evil

**Plato's answer**: This material world doesn't matter. It's a fake shadow world. Let the powerful have it. Focus on knowledge.

**Dante's answer**: The Monad is love, and love requires trust, and trust requires **free will**. The Monad gives us free will even though it can lead to evil, because to interfere would be to destroy his greatest gift. Periodically, the Monad sends **messengers** (Plato, Dante, Jesus) to remind us of the divine spark — but it remains our choice whether to listen.

#### How Transgression Fits the Model

In this multilayered Geist, there are evil forces as well as good ones. The lecturer uses the Internet as a metaphor:

> "Think of the Internet. There are millions and millions of websites. Through transgression, by doing the same evil act, what the powerful have done is they've locked on to the same website."

By committing the same transgressive act, a group tunes into the same frequency within the Geist — achieving synchronicity, shared consciousness, and coordinated power.

#### Why the Powerful Maintain the System

A student asks why those in power deny the spiritual model. The lecturer's answer:

- The powerful gained their position through transgression-based cohesion
- They need to rationalize and maintain that power
- They promote **materialism** and **science** as the denial of the spiritual world — making the prison feel like the only reality
- In their own inverted theology, the Monad is evil and their god is the liberator

> "Different people will interpret this model in a different way. In their conception, the Monad's the evil one. Their god's the good one. They're liberating us from the Monad by making us more focused on the material world."

### Key Takeaways

- Ritual sacrifice is framed as a recurring historical mechanism for social cohesion, not merely an act of cruelty
- **Transgression** is proposed as the most effective form of secret coordination — the greater the taboo broken, the greater the group cohesion

- Two paths back to the source: knowledge (Plato) vs. love (Dante)
- The powerful maintain control by promoting materialism and denying spiritual reality
- All of this is presented as speculative theory and tools for thinking — not as established truth
