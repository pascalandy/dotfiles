## The Bronze Age Collapse

### Overview

This is a university history lecture covering the Bronze Age collapse (circa 1200 BCE). The instructor traces how bronze became the first universal currency, how it drove globalization across the ancient world, and why that interconnected system suddenly collapsed. The lecture connects this historical event to a broader argument about the nature of capital and its inevitable cycle of growth and destruction.

---

### The Four Early Civilizations and Their Rivers

#### Main Idea

The four earliest major civilizations—Egypt, Mesopotamia, Indus Valley, and China—all developed around major rivers. These rivers enabled agriculture and overseas trade. Each civilization had a major city with colonies along the river.

- **Egypt** → the Nile
- **Mesopotamia** → the Euphrates and the Tigris; first city is **Uruk**
- **China** → the Yellow River
- **Indus Valley** → the Indus River

The city-states along these rivers went to war to control trade routes, and this competition drove major innovation in human history.

---

### The Rise of Bronze

#### Main Idea

The need for better military equipment led to the development of **bronze**, an alloy of tin and copper. Bronze was the strongest metal available—ideal for shields, armor, and swords. Whoever had more bronze weaponry would win any war.

#### The Tin Problem

**Tin** is hard to mine, discover, and manufacture. Tin deposits are scattered all around the world rather than concentrated in one place. This scarcity forced civilizations to build ever-expanding trade routes to access tin. The major points along these trade routes eventually became cities themselves.

**Connection**: The geographic scarcity of tin is the engine that drives globalization in this period. The world becomes interconnected not by choice but by military necessity.

> "This world is globalized in order to facilitate warfare."

---

### The Indus Valley Exception

#### Main Idea

The Indus Valley civilization stands apart from Egypt, Mesopotamia, and China. Archaeological digs in modern-day Pakistan have uncovered bronze but no weaponry. The instructor characterizes it as a peaceful, egalitarian, and artistic civilization.

Despite its peaceful nature, the Indus Valley was still brought into the global trading system because bronze transitioned from a weapon into a status symbol—used for pottery and jewelry—and eventually became capital itself.

The instructor speculates that the Indus Valley people, through contact with the other civilizations, consciously rejected the militaristic system.

> "We don't want a system where everyone's killing each other."

The Indus Valley cities, when excavated, show advanced housing, irrigation, and hygiene—very similar to **Çatalhöyük** (discussed in a previous class), but on a much larger scale. Religion was a major part of life.

#### Supporting Material: The BMAC Culture

The Indus Valley gave rise to the **BMAC (Bactria-Margiana) culture** in northern Afghanistan, which developed because of trade—specifically tin mining in the mountains. This culture became a gateway for proto-Indo-Europeans from the steppes, who later migrated into modern-day Iran and India, giving rise to **Zoroastrianism** and **Hinduism**.

---

### The Collapse Begins (~1200 BCE)

#### Main Idea

Starting around 1200 BCE, this heavily globalized system collapses suddenly and devastatingly. In China, the **Shang dynasty** collapses. Egypt comes under attack by the **Sea Peoples**. The instructor frames the Sea Peoples as refugees fleeing the destruction of Europe.

#### The Perfect Storm

The collapse involves multiple simultaneous crises:

- **Climate change** → drought → famine → migration crisis
- Earthquakes (confirmed through geological analysis)
- Revolutions and civil wars
- Mass migration

#### Supporting Material: Eric Cline's "1177 B.C."

**Eric Cline**, an anthropologist at the University of Washington, wrote *1177 B.C.*, which catalogs the reasons for the Bronze Age collapse. He describes it as a "perfect storm of crises."

**Connection**: The instructor uses Cline's framework as a starting point but then extends the argument—claiming the root cause is the nature of capital itself, not just the confluence of disasters.

#### Key Civilizations Destroyed

- **Mycenaean Greece** — destroyed by the Sea Peoples
- **Hittite Empire** (in Anatolia / modern Turkey) — destroyed
- **Egypt** — survived the invasions but was left bankrupt and lost its global power status
- **Indus Valley** — overwhelmed by refugees from the steppes

#### Mesopotamia's Resilience

Mesopotamia was the most resilient of the four civilizations. The reason: it had no natural geographic defenses (unlike Egypt with the Sahara and Red Sea, or China with its natural boundaries). Mesopotamia is flat land, easily invaded. Throughout its history, it cycled through three major empires—the **Akkadian**, **Babylonian**, and **Assyrian**—none lasting more than a couple hundred years. This constant competition made it more resilient.

> "Because it's so competitive, it's actually much more resilient than the other areas."

---

### The Sea Peoples

#### Main Idea

The Sea Peoples are a mix of different groups. Despite attempts to identify their origins, scholars still don't know exactly who they were. The assumption is they came from everywhere—refugees led by pirates who were expert raiders.

Egyptian records document at least three major invasions, with forces of around a hundred thousand people attacking Egypt simultaneously. Egypt expended tremendous resources repelling these invasions, which ultimately left it bankrupt.

**Connection**: The instructor draws a parallel to modern European migration crises, with Middle Eastern refugees overwhelming economic systems.

---

### The Nature of Capital

#### Main Idea

The instructor's central argument: the Bronze Age world was a **proto-capitalistic system**. Capital allows for rapid growth but also enables rapid collapse. This is presented as an inherent, unavoidable feature of capital.

#### Three Characteristics of Capital

1. **Universality** — everyone wants it; everyone believes it has value
2. **Store of value** — you can take wealth and store it inside this commodity
3. **Mobility** — you can transport it somewhere else at little or no cost

If a commodity has all three characteristics, it is capital.

#### Supporting Material: Testing Different Commodities

The instructor walks through several candidates:

| Commodity | Universal? | Store of Value? | Mobile? | Became Capital? |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|---------|----------------|
| Grain | Sort of (everyone eats) | No (decomposes) | No (cheap but expensive to move) | No |
| Cattle | Sort of | Better | No (hard to transport) | No |
| Slaves | Sort of | Unstable value | Yes | Basic economy only |
| Bronze | Yes | Yes (hard to make) | Yes | Yes — first real universal currency |
| US Dollar | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes (modern equivalent) |

**Connection**: Bronze became the first real universal currency, enabling rapid globalization—just as the US dollar does today.

---

### How Capital Changes Human Nature

#### Main Idea

Humans have two natures: **altruistic** (caring about relationships, empathy, harmony) and **utilitarian** (caring about goals, profit, power). Capital shifts people from the altruistic state into the utilitarian state.

#### Supporting Material: The Restaurant vs. Mother's House

When you go to a restaurant and get a good meal, you pay and tip. When your mother cooks for you, you say thank you. You don't tip your mother a thousand dollars, and you don't just say "thanks" and walk out of a restaurant. These two systems—transactional and relational—must stay separate.

**Connection**: Capital blurs this boundary, pushing all interactions toward the transactional/utilitarian side.

#### The Growth-Collapse Cycle

1. In a purely altruistic system, nothing changes. Villages remain static because harmony matters more than progress. Outsiders and curious people are unwelcome—in some traditions, outspoken or exploratory individuals were killed as sacrifices.
2. Capital enters → people become motivated to work hard, innovate, explore → rapid growth
3. Growth hits a maximum point → the scale tips
4. People become too utilitarian → cheating, exploitation, indifference
5. System collapses

#### Supporting Material: The Village Leader Thought Experiment

- **Without capital**: A leader cares about status, reputation, how people perceive them
- **With capital**: The leader sees people as commodities to exploit. Leaders align with other leaders to form cartels/elites. They expand to conquer new areas. Eventually, only a few people hold all the capital. Those below are enslaved and exploited. They become indifferent or revolt.

> "The system must collapse."

---

### Capital as Monetization of Power

#### Main Idea

Capital exploits, alienates, and consolidates. The only results of capitalism are: massive inequality, corruption, immorality, alienation, anger, and indifference.

The instructor frames this as inevitable—not a moral judgment but a structural observation.

> "It's just the nature of capital. There's no way around it."

#### Altruistic vs. Utilitarian Traits

- **Altruistic people** tend to be more creative
- **Utilitarian/money-obsessed people** tend to work harder
- This explains why globalization happens fast—everyone rushes to make money
- Eventually, wealth concentrates → everyone else gives up ("lying flat," "quiet quitting")

### The Origin of Money: David Graeber's "Debt"

#### Main Idea

**David Graeber**, an anthropologist, argues in his book *Debt* that money was created to symbolize debts that could not be repaid. Most human interactions are reciprocal (you help me, I help you), but three situations create unpayable debts:

1. **Blood debt** — If I kill your brother, it creates a cycle of vengeance. Instead, I give something valuable to acknowledge the unpayable debt.
2. **Marriage debt** — A woman marrying into another family creates a debt, compensated with gifts (gold, cattle, etc.)
3. **Death of a great person** — When a respected leader or priest dies, the community buries them with gold and gifts as a monument to their legacy. They know the person can't take it to the afterworld—it's an acknowledgment of contribution.

**Connection**: This explains why ancient graves contain gold. It's not about the afterlife—it's about community recognition of unpayable debt.

> "The community is acknowledging this person was great. We will forever remember him, and so the money is a way to acknowledge this fact."

### Historical Parallels to Modern Globalization

#### Main Idea

The instructor draws explicit parallels between the Bronze Age collapse and the modern world:

- **Pre-WWI world**: Heavily globalized, people believed trade made war impossible → then tens of millions died in WWI "for no reason"
- **Today**: Heavily globalized, heavily interconnected, but because of the nature of capital, collapse is likely

> "If you look at the Bronze Age collapse and World War One, we are in a very similar situation."

### Troy and the Control of Trade Routes

#### Main Idea

The most profitable activity in the Bronze Age was not mining, processing, manufacturing, or transporting—it was **controlling trade routes**. Troy sat at the center of global trade in the Mediterranean/Aegean, which is why it was fought over.

The instructor ranks money-making activities in the Bronze Age:

1. Mining — hard work, low return
2. Processing into ingots — moderate
3. Manufacturing (jewelry, pottery, weapons) — better
4. Transporting — hard but profitable
5. Piracy — stealing goods
6. **Controlling trade routes** — the real money

> "Empires really are trade routes. Empires are just control of trade routes."

**Connection**: This is why the Middle East remains strategically important today—whoever controls it controls global trade. The Trojan War was fundamentally a trade war.

### Mycenaean Civilization

#### Main Idea

The **Mycenaean civilization** is important because it gives rise to Greek civilization, considered the birthplace of Western civilization. Key characteristics:

- A **warrior aristocracy** (warriors in charge, not priests as in Sumer)
- Descendants of the **proto-Indo-Europeans**
- Located at the center of Mediterranean trade
- Originally pirates—the people who attacked Troy were pirates
- Operated a **palace economy**: all farmers gave food to the palace, which redistributed it to artisans who made textiles and pottery for trade (command-and-control economy)

#### Supporting Material: Archaeological Evidence of Wealth

- A gold death mask (sometimes speculated to be **Agamemnon**, a major character in the Iliad—though this is unconfirmed)
- Swords with gold hilts—even warriors had gold
- The **Lion's Gate** — entrance to Mycenae, heavily fortified due to constant wars and piracy
- Elaborate tombs that grew more ornate over time, indicating increasing wealth
- Tomb-building practices learned from Egypt

### Capital and Brain Damage

#### Main Idea

The instructor argues that excessive capital fundamentally changes brain chemistry. He references an article from *The Atlantic Monthly* reporting on neuroscientists who conducted brain scans showing that **power causes brain damage**. The brains of psychopaths and wealthy people look similar—both lose the capacity for empathy.

Examples cited: Elon Musk and Mark Zuckerberg as people with no concept of empathy, unable to relate to others' feelings.

#### Supporting Material: Brian Johnson

**Brian Johnson** is a millionaire who spends approximately two million dollars per year on health and longevity. He practices blood transfusions, extreme exercise regimens, and constant health monitoring. The instructor uses him as an example of how wealth drives irrational behavior—specifically, the obsession with living forever rather than enjoying life.

#### Supporting Material: The Satan Thought Experiment

Two scenarios:

1. Satan offers you a billion dollars, but you must live forever as his slave → you refuse
2. You already have ten billion dollars. Satan offers to let you live forever and keep your money, but you must be his slave → you accept

**Connection**: The difference is what money does to decision-making. Once you have extreme wealth, losing it (through death) becomes unbearable—**sunk cost fallacy** at the ultimate scale.

#### The Wealthy and Desperation

The instructor argues wealthy people, desperate to preserve their status and avoid death, resort to:

- Investing in longevity research
- Building bunkers (example: Mark Zuckerberg's $26 million bunker in Hawaii)
- Joining secret societies (seeking secrets of immortality/reincarnation)
- Worshipping Satan — framed as a logical endpoint of desperation

#### Why Satan Specifically?

A student asks this question directly. The instructor's answer:

- Traditional religion teaches wealth is bad
- God will judge you for how you obtained your wealth
- The wealthy know exactly what they did to accumulate their fortune
- They don't want to die, don't want to give up money, and don't want to face God's judgment
- Satan offers refuge from that judgment
- They prefer hell to accountability

> "You and your heart know exactly what you did to get that ten billion dollars."

### The Transition to Greek Civilization

#### Main Idea

The Bronze Age collapse, while devastating, created the conditions for Greek civilization to emerge. Three major transitions occurred:

1. **Political**: From the Mycenaean palace economy to the **polis** (city-state) system — the origin of the word "politics." Centralized economy → competing city-states.
2. **Writing**: From **Linear B** to the **alphabet**
3. **Expression**: From censorship under bureaucratic palace systems (propaganda) to free expression — enabling the emergence of **Homer**

These three changes together became the basis for Greek civilization.

> "Collapse is bad, but collapse also gives opportunity for a new civilization to arise, for a new humanity to emerge."

The next class will cover the Iliad and the birth of Greek civilization.

### Key Takeaways

- Bronze became the first universal currency because it met all three criteria of capital: universality, store of value, and mobility
- The scarcity of tin forced civilizations to build trade networks that globalized the ancient world
- Capital drives a predictable cycle: altruism → innovation and growth → exploitation and consolidation → collapse
- The Bronze Age collapse resulted from a perfect storm of crises, but the instructor argues the deeper cause is the inherent instability of proto-capitalistic systems
- Mesopotamia's lack of natural defenses made it paradoxically the most resilient civilization
- The destruction of the Bronze Age world created the conditions for Greek civilization—arguably the most influential civilization in Western history

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Bronze**: An alloy of tin and copper; the strongest metal of the era, used for weapons and eventually became the first universal currency
- **Capital**: A commodity that is universal, stores value, and is mobile
- **Proto-capitalistic system**: An early economic system with the structural characteristics of capitalism—rapid growth followed by inevitable collapse
- **Palace economy**: A centralized economic system where all agricultural output flows to a palace, which redistributes it to artisans
- **Polis**: Greek city-state; the political system that replaced the Mycenaean palace economy
- **Sea Peoples**: Unidentified groups of refugees and pirates who attacked Mediterranean civilizations around 1200 BCE
- **BMAC culture**: Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex in northern Afghanistan, a trade/mining community connected to the Indus Valley
- **Sunk cost fallacy**: Referenced in context of wealthy people unable to accept losing accumulated wealth through death
- **Linear B**: The writing system of Mycenaean Greece, replaced by the alphabet after the collapse
- **Ingots**: Standardized blocks used to transport tin and copper for trade
