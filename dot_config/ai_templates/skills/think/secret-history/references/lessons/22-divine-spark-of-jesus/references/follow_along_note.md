## The Most Famous Person Who Has Ever Lived: Jesus and His True Teachings

### Overview

This is a university lecture exploring three central questions about Jesus: What did he actually believe and teach? Why did he become so popular? And why did the Romans kill him? The lecturer argues that Jesus' true teachings—preserved in the Gospel of Thomas rather than the canonical Bible—align with a universal tradition of spiritual enlightenment found in Homer, Plato, Zoroaster, Dante, and Dostoevsky. The lecture also examines how Christianity as an organized religion diverges from what Jesus himself likely taught.

---

### The Major Ideas of Christianity

#### Main Idea

Before examining the historical Jesus, the lecturer outlines what most Christian denominations agree on—the baseline beliefs that define Christianity as a religion.

The core beliefs:

- Jesus is the son of God and God himself—and he is king. The lecturer notes this is already confusing on its face.
- Jesus died for our sins. Humanity was expelled from the Garden of Eden for disobedience (eating from the tree of knowledge), and Jesus' death redeemed us.
- Jesus will return—the **second coming**. He died, was resurrected, ascended to heaven, and will eventually return for the **last judgment**, where all individuals are judged for heaven or hell.

#### The Controversial Core

The most controversial element: if you believe in Jesus, you go to heaven. If you do not, you burn in hell. The lecturer contrasts this with other religious traditions, which generally say "be a good person" without demanding allegiance to a specific figure.

This makes Christianity a **missionary religion**—believers must actively convert others, because non-believers face damnation. The lecturer calls this what makes Christianity "one of the most aggressive religions that we have today."

When questions arise about the logic of these beliefs, the standard Christian explanation is **miracles**—don't question it, just believe.

> "How did Jesus die for our sins? Why is Jesus' son coming? It's all a miracle. Meaning, don't question it. Just believe in it."

### What We Actually Know About Jesus

#### Main Idea

The lecturer shifts from religious belief to historical scholarship—what mainstream academics agree on based on evidence, archaeology, and textual analysis. Only four things are established with reasonable certainty.

**The four known facts:**

1. **Born around 4 BCE in Galilee**—not year zero as the Bible states
2. **His teacher was John the Baptist**—an **apocalyptic preacher** who taught that God is coming to destroy evil, and people must redeem themselves now. Jesus was one of his disciples but eventually broke away and developed his own following with a different message.
3. **The Romans crucified him**—we don't know exactly why. **Crucifixion** involved being nailed to a wooden cross and dying over roughly three days from suffocation as the body lost energy and the head drooped. Only two categories of people received this punishment: thieves/bandits (the lowest class) and rebels against the Roman state. Jesus was likely considered a rebel.
4. **He had a brother named James the Just**—who inherited Jesus' movement after his death. This movement was called **the Poor** or **Ebionites**.

**Connection**: The lecturer emphasizes the enormous gap between these four sparse facts and the elaborate narrative found in the Bible.

### The Biblical Story of Jesus

#### Main Idea

The lecturer presents the official Biblical narrative, noting that there are at least four different biographies of Jesus in the Bible—**Mark, Matthew, Luke, and John**—which say different things. He presents the main synthesized story.

The narrative arc:

- Jesus is the son of God. Humanity has disobeyed God, and God is angry. Jesus decides to take human form and sacrifice himself as a **scapegoat** to redeem humanity.
- He begins a **ministry**: healing people, dispelling demons, and preaching the **gospel** (good news).
- His teachings conflict with **Judaic authority**—Jewish priests enforce the law of Moses, Sabbath observance, and tradition. Jesus says what matters is following the spirit of God.
- The Jewish priests plot against him. He is betrayed by **Judas Iscariot**, detained by the priests, and handed to the Roman governor **Pontius Pilate**.
- Pilate doesn't want to kill him but yields to public pressure. Jesus is crucified and dies.
- After three days, he is **resurrected**, appears before his disciples, and tells them to spread the good news—he has redeemed humanity from sin, and salvation is available to all, Jewish or not.

> "Tell them the good news that I will one day return, and that I've redeemed people from their sins."

### Problems with the Biblical Story

#### Main Idea

The lecturer identifies several logical and historical problems with the Biblical account, treating them as legitimate scholarly questions rather than matters of faith.

**Problem 1: The nature of Jesus as God's son.** How does God produce a son? Aren't we all God's children? In Greek mythology, gods physically impregnate women—but that's not what happens here. And the Bible also says Jesus *is* God, not just his son. The confusion is unresolved.

**Problem 2: The logic of self-sacrifice.** Why does killing himself resolve humanity's disobedience? The causal chain is unclear.

**Problem 3: Jewish culpability—the most historically damaging claim.** The Bible explicitly blames Jewish priests for Jesus' death. For two thousand years, Christians used this to persecute Jews. The lecturer argues this likely did not happen, offering three pieces of evidence:

1. **Disagreement is built into Jewish tradition.** At the time, there were at least three competing Jewish groups—the **Sadducees** (priestly nobility maintaining ritual), the **Pharisees** (reformers emphasizing law over sacrifice), and the **Essenes** (monastic apocalyptic communities, likely including John the Baptist). These groups hated each other. Disagreement alone was never grounds for execution.

2. **Unwritten Jewish law against betraying Jews to non-Jewish authorities.** The Jewish people saw themselves as distinct and separate. There is no historical precedent for Jews handing over a fellow Jew to Roman authorities.

3. **James the Just remained in Jerusalem, protected by Jewish authorities.** If Jesus was killed for opposing Jewish law, why was his brother—teaching the same things—allowed to stay and even protected?

The lecturer concludes the Romans killed Jesus for their own reasons. The Biblical narrative conveniently exonerates the Romans and blames the Jews—which is exactly what you'd expect if the Romans wrote or influenced the story.

**Additional problems:** If Jesus is divine, how does death have meaning for him? Why resurrect? Why promise a second coming if humanity is already redeemed? Why require worship?

> "Northern religion has this many questions. And so what the explanation is—miracle, mystery, magic."

The lecturer frames the rest of the lecture around three questions:

3. Why did the Romans kill him?

The central argument: Jesus taught the same thing every great prophet studied in the course has taught.

### Jesus' Core Teaching: The Universal Doctrine of the Divine Spark

#### Main Idea

The lecturer argues Jesus taught a doctrine consistent with Homer, Plato, Zoroaster, Buddhism, Hinduism, and Nietzsche. The teaching is this:

- There is a **source**—the divine, the good, God, the universe, the **monad**
- The source emanates and creates the entire universe
- Our bodies are material (products of evolution), but our **consciousness/soul** is the **divine spark** from the source
- After death, the spark returns to the source
- There is a constant cycle until we ultimately merge with the source

> "Jesus was just teaching it for his times."

#### Why Jesus Was Special

Jesus wasn't special because his message was unique—it was the same message every prophet delivered. He was special because the **Roman Empire was particularly evil**.

The lecturer connects this to the Iliad: Achilles couldn't forgive himself; Priam forgave him. During the Iliad's era, everyone did both good and evil, and forgiveness was the path forward. But by Roman times, the Romans represented **pure, demonic evil**. The question became: how do you explain a world where evil triumphs and the good are enslaved?

#### Jesus' Answer

The world we live in is a **false world**—a **corpse**, a dead world. Those who embrace death, evil, and hatred win in this false world. But this world is not the true one. What matters is the spiritual reality above.

The Romans have won by being evil—which means, in the grand scheme, they've lost. Like Achilles after killing Hector, the powerful are tormented, haunted by demons, unable to sleep or find peace.

> "Use our imagination, use our heart, use our empathy, and look into their minds, and you can see how tormented they are."

The radical instruction: **forgive your enemy. Love your enemy.** Only by loving your enemy can you release them from their demons. This is what saves the world.

**Connection**: Jesus' message maps directly onto the Priam-Achilles dynamic from the Iliad. The oppressed must "kiss Achilles' hand"—submit with love so the oppressor can be freed from his own darkness.

### Why the Romans Killed Jesus

#### Main Idea

Jesus was a **rebel** who subverted the natural order. The Roman order placed Romans at the top and slaves at the bottom. Jesus inverted this: slaves are at the top (free in spirit), Romans at the bottom (trapped by the evil in their hearts). Slaves can liberate everyone through compassion, love, and forgiveness.

The Romans saw this as a direct threat.

After killing him, the Romans wrote (or shaped) the Biblical narrative to justify what they did—blaming the Jews and exonerating themselves. This is what the lecturer says Romans always do: commit evil, then get others to write history explaining why it was good.

> "Christianity is not an invention of Jesus. Christianity is an invention of the Romans."

### Jesus as the Democratization of the Divine

#### Main Idea

The lecturer introduces the concept of the **divine spark of Jesus**—not in a theological sense but as a cultural force.

Homer's Iliad, Dante's Divine Comedy—these are portals to the divine. But most people cannot read them well. They require extensive education.

Jesus is a portal anyone can access. Anyone can know his story, recite his sayings, and through that, access the divine. Jesus represents the **democratization of the divine**—making spiritual truth available to all people, not just the educated elite.

### The Gospel of Thomas: Jesus' True Teachings

#### Main Idea

In **1945**, at a place called **Nag Hammadi** in Egypt, a collection of lost texts was discovered. Among them was the **Gospel of Thomas**, which the lecturer believes records Jesus' true teachings.

After Jesus' death, many different sects of Christianity existed and argued with each other. The Catholic Church won—because it was backed by the Roman Empire. The Gospel of Thomas was suppressed.

The lecturer presents several passages with commentary:

#### "Whoever finds interpretation of these sayings will not experience death"

Death doesn't truly exist. The soul ascends to the astral plane, to the divine. Once you recognize you are the divine spark, you become immortal in the meaningful sense. If you don't recognize it, you fear death.

#### "Let him who seeks continue seeking until he finds"

> "When he finds, he will become troubled. When he becomes troubled, he will be astonished, and he will rule over the all."

This parallels **Plato's Allegory of the Cave**. The prisoner escapes, is blinded by sunlight, experiences pain, but eventually sees truth and "rules over all" because everyone else remains chained in darkness.

#### "The kingdom is inside of you and it is outside of you"

> "When you come to know yourself, then you will become known, and you will realize that it is you who are the sons of the living father."

Key implications the lecturer draws:

- **Organized religion does not work.** Jesus would never say "follow me and you're good"—he knew you can't follow someone else to truth. The divine spark is in you.
- **"We're all sons of the living father"**—Jesus is not claiming superiority. Everyone shares the same divine nature.
- Throughout the Gospel of Thomas, **Jesus does not reference the Bible**—no Adam and Eve, no Yahweh, no Moses, no David. For him, the Bible is irrelevant. What's true is the divine spark in you.
- Jesus was not a Christian.

#### "These infants being suckled are like those who enter the kingdom"

> "When you make the two one... when you make the inside like the outside and the outside like the inside, and above like the below... then you enter the kingdom."

The world we see is a hallucination—a projection of our imagination. Once we recognize this through imagination, we can access the divine. All separations—male/female, nationality, race—are fabricated. Reality is interconnected. This maps to the Zoroastrian concept of **Asha** (harmony, interconnectedness) versus **Druj** (division, falsehood).

#### "There is light within the man of light, and he lights up the whole world"

When the disciples ask Jesus to show them how to be like him, his answer: be yourself. There's a light inside of you. Shine it.

#### "I found all of them intoxicated. I found none of them thirsty."

> "For empty they came into the world, and empty too they seek to leave the world."

We arrive with nothing and leave with nothing. The pursuit of materialism, wealth, and power is senseless—we are drunk on false pursuits and not thirsty for truth.

The lecturer connects this to **Rumi**, the Persian poet, who wrote that the world is "a prison for drunks"—likely without having read the Gospel of Thomas. They think the same thought because they are inspired by the same source.

#### "The Pharisees and the scribes have taken the keys of knowledge and hidden them"

> "You, however, be as wise as serpents and as innocent as doves."

Jesus opposes organized religion. The Pharisees create hierarchy—"only I know the truth, listen to me." But knowledge cannot be accessed through hierarchy or oppression. Only through looking inward.

The serpent reference is deliberate: it was a serpent who told Eve to eat the fruit. Jesus is saying it is **good and natural to seek knowledge even if it means disobeying God**. If a god forbids knowledge-seeking, that god is a false god. The true God—the monad—is pure love, compassion, and forgiveness.

#### "Whoever has come to understand the world has found only a corpse"

> "Whoever has found a corpse is superior to the world."

The Roman Empire, for all its power, is a corpse—dying, dead, nothing. Once you recognize this and refuse to comply, the empire has no power over you. This is what made Jesus a threat.

#### The Parable of the Lost Sheep

God does not want obedience. He wants creativity, exploration, knowledge-seeking. The one sheep that goes astray is cared for more than the ninety-nine who stay. Those who merely obey contribute nothing—they're like **NPCs** (non-playable characters). When we love, imagine, and create, we make the universe brighter.

#### The Parable of the Dinner Party

A man prepares a feast and invites guests. Every invitee declines—too busy with merchants, a house purchase, a wedding, collecting rent.

> "Businessmen and merchants will not enter the places of my father."

The divine has prepared a great feast for us (union with the monad after death). But the wealthy and busy are too consumed with worldly affairs to accept. They will lose out in the end, even though they appear to have won today. The lecturer draws a contemporary parallel to Elon Musk.

#### Student Q&A: How Do We Know What We Want?

A student asks: if socialization brainwashes us toward materialism, how do we find our true path?

The lecturer's answer: **be brave and true to yourself.** Two billion people believe in Jesus because they recognize he spoke truth. The divine spark is the guide—you know when it glows. When you love and are loved back, when you teach and students learn, the spark glows. Try different things until you find what makes it glow.

Life is infinite. Death is a game reset. **Free will** is the essential principle—it's your choice what to do with each life. After death, you return to the astral plane, reflect, and start over. It may take a million years. But eventually, everyone figures it out.

> "What's really important to understand is you have to figure it out by yourself. No one can teach you."

### Dostoevsky's Grand Inquisitor: The Problem Jesus Left Behind

#### Main Idea

The lecturer introduces **The Brothers Karamazov** by Dostoevsky as a direct engagement with the problem Jesus' teachings create: most people don't want freedom. They want to be told what to do.

#### The Problem

Jesus came and told humanity the truth—there's a spark inside you, you're free, you don't have to be slaves. But this creates **anxiety**. People don't want individual responsibility. They want structure, obedience, and someone else making decisions.

#### The Story

In Dostoevsky's parable, the Catholic Church has become the evil empire—worshipping Satan rather than Jesus, enforcing law and order through oppression. One day, Jesus returns to Earth. People recognize him, he heals them, they celebrate. But the Church must arrest him because his presence threatens the order they've built.

The **Grand Inquisitor** confronts Jesus and makes his case:

> "Thou didst not love them at all... Instead of taking possession of man's freedom, thou didst increase it and burden the spiritual kingdom of mankind with its suffering forever."

The argument: Jesus' gift of freedom is actually cruelty. People can't handle it. Before Jesus, teachers told you what was good and evil. Now Jesus says "you decide"—and the result is chaos and anxiety.

> "They will cry aloud at last that the truth is not in thee, for they cannot have been left in greater confusion and suffering than thou hast caused."

The Inquisitor's justification for the Church: freedom only serves the tiny minority who can think for themselves. What about everyone else? The weak? The Church created **miracle, mystery, and authority** so people could follow blindly—and they were relieved.

> "Man's joy that they were again led like sheep and that the terrible gift that had brought them such suffering was at last lifted from their hearts."

#### Jesus' Response

After the Inquisitor's lengthy argument, Jesus says nothing. He simply approaches the old man and **softly kisses him on his bloodless aged lips**.

The Inquisitor shudders. He lets Jesus go:

> "Go and come no more. Come not at all. Never. Never."

> "The kiss glows in his heart, but the old man adheres to his idea."

**Connection**: This mirrors Priam kissing Achilles' hand. Jesus doesn't argue—he forgives. The kiss represents the power of love to ignite the spark even in the most evil person. The Inquisitor knows he's lost, but he can't change. Yet the spark glows.

#### The Solution to the Inquisitor's Problem

The soul is eternal. People need time—millions of years, countless lifetimes. God is patient, loving, forgiving, compassionate. Eventually, people learn for themselves that being a slave to comfort doesn't work. They'll figure it out.

> "Eventually, you'll figure out being a slave sucks."

### Dante's Divine Comedy: Why Jesus Had to Die

#### Main Idea

Dante addresses the question no one can satisfactorily answer: why did Jesus have to kill himself? What's the logic?

#### The Ransom Theory (What the Church Taught)

The traditional explanation in Dante's time: when we do evil, we become slaves of Satan. Jesus made a deal—he dies, Satan takes his body, and in exchange, Satan frees humanity. But since Jesus is divine, he can never truly die, so he wins the bargain.

Dante rejects this. God did not free us to make us slaves to God. God freed us to make us **free**.

#### Beatrice's Explanation: Freedom of Will

In Paradise, Beatrice explains to Dante the foundational principle of the universe:

> "The greatest gift, the magnanimity of God as he created, gave the gift most suited to his goodness... was the freedom of the will."

If God is love, he must trust people. True love means letting go—even letting your child make mistakes. The parent analogy: if you truly love your child, you let them go, even if it frightens you. You don't impose your fear on your child's freedom.

#### The Logic of Self-Sacrifice

The problem: humanity disobeyed God (ate the fruit, tried to become God). This created evil. Evil compounds—we do more evil to avoid confronting the evil we've already done. We're stuck.

Two possible solutions:
1. **Humanity compensates God**—impossible, because we tried to kill God. No act of humility can match that transgression.
2. **God simply forgives us**—but then we learn nothing.

#### The Analogy That Makes It Click

The lecturer offers an analogy: A father has a daughter (Eve) and a dog. He tells Eve he loves them both equally. Eve, wanting to test this, kills the dog. Now the father is trapped:

- If he punishes Eve, it proves he loved the dog more
- If he doesn't punish Eve, she'll do it again

The solution: **the father punishes himself.** Eve sees this and understands two things—her father truly loves her (he's suffering for her sake), and she won't do it again (she can't bear watching him suffer).

This is why Jesus had to die. God punished himself so humanity would be humbled by the sacrifice, feel remorse, and never disobey again. Through remembering Jesus, we stay on the path toward the light.

#### The Purpose of the Universe

Beatrice explains that God created the laws of the universe—the "game"—and those laws are perfect. Within the game, things enter cycles of life and death. That's necessary for the game to function.

The purpose of life: to learn, make mistakes, be creative, imagine new possibilities. **Death is a reset**—it allows rejuvenation and growth. Don't think about one lifetime; think about all possible lifetimes. Make as many mistakes as possible, because only through mistakes does the soul grow.

> "The rays and motion of the holy light draw forth the soul of every animal and plant from matter able to take form."

### The Divine Spark After Death

#### Main Idea

A student asks: if death clears memory, what's the point of learning across lifetimes?

The lecturer clarifies: what carries over is not memory—it's the **spark itself**. The spark either glows brightly or dims based on how you lived. A bright spark ascends to a higher plane, closer to the monad, experiencing more pleasure. A dim spark descends to a lower plane.

What you leave behind is not wealth or memory but the **amount of goodness or evil you did.** Every religion teaches this.

### Evidence from the Canonical Bible: The Sermon on the Mount

#### Main Idea

The lecturer argues that some of Jesus' true teachings survive even in the canonical Bible—specifically the **Sermon on the Mount** from the Gospel of Matthew.

**The Beatitudes:**

> "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn... Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth."

What matters is the spark, not wealth or power. The slaves and the suffering are more virtuous and will inherit the kingdom.

**Salt and Light:**

> "You are the light of the world. A town built on a hill cannot be hidden."

Everyone has a light inside them. Everyone has the power to change the universe and do good.

**Love Your Enemy:**

> "Love your enemy and pray for those who persecute you... If you love those who love you, what reward will you get? Are not even the tax collectors doing that?"

**Do Not Store Treasures on Earth:**

> "For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also."

Focus on spiritual enrichment. Material possessions can't follow you after death, but your heart always will.

**The Eye as Lamp:**

> "If your eyes are healthy, your whole body will be full of light."

We imagine reality. How you see the world reflects the spark inside you. This connects to the **law of attraction**—see goodness, and goodness manifests.

**God and Money:**

> "You cannot serve both God and money."

Satan is money and power. This world belongs to Satan. The spiritual world belongs to God. You must choose.

**The Final Warning—Against Using Jesus' Name:**

> "Not everyone who says to me, Lord, Lord, will enter the kingdom of heaven... Then I will tell them plainly, I never knew you. Away from me, you evildoers."

Christianity as organized religion tells people: believe in Jesus and you'll be saved. Jesus himself says the opposite—don't use his name. Follow your heart. Those who invoke his name are doing evil. This is the gap between Jesus and the Catholic Church—and the subject of the next lecture.

### The Question of Forgiveness

#### Main Idea

A student raises the question: can someone who has committed extreme evil be forgiven?

The lecturer's answer: **yes, because forgiveness is the only way the world functions.** Without forgiveness, the only alternative is vengeance—an endless cycle of retribution that destroys everything.

Previously, humanity lacked the tools for forgiveness. Jesus provides those tools: if Jesus forgives us, we can forgive ourselves. This doesn't mean escaping consequences—you still go to prison. But after serving justice, you must be able to forgive yourself and move on, because society must move on.

The lecturer acknowledges this is hard for young people to accept—at a young age, the mind dominates. As the heart matures through life experience, forgiveness becomes intuitive rather than logical.

> "In a world with no forgiveness, this world would break down really quickly because everyone would just kill each other."

### Why the Gospel of Thomas Reflects Jesus' True Teachings

#### Main Idea

The lecturer offers three reasons for his conviction:

1. **The Gospel of Thomas has a unique poetic voice.** True poetry—the divine spark itself—cannot be replicated. When you read it, you can feel its authenticity.
2. **Its teachings align with every other prophet studied in the course**—Homer, Plato, Zoroaster, Buddha, the Hindu traditions.
3. **Later prophets like Dante and Dostoevsky** independently arrived at the same conclusions, confirming what Jesus originally taught.

### Key Takeaways

- Jesus' core teaching was **love, forgiveness, and the divine spark within every person**—not institutional worship or obedience
- The divine spark connects everyone to the source/monad; it glows through love and dims through evil
- **Organized religion contradicts Jesus' actual message**—he explicitly taught that truth must be discovered individually, not followed blindly
- The Romans likely killed Jesus because he was a political rebel who inverted the social hierarchy, placing slaves above emperors
- Christianity as an institution was shaped by the Roman Empire, not by Jesus himself
- **Forgiveness is the foundational principle** that allows the universe to function—without it, only vengeance remains
- Death is a reset, not an end; the soul has infinite lifetimes to grow toward the divine
- Freedom of will is God's greatest gift, even though it creates anxiety and chaos—this tension drives the creation of organized religion

- **Ebionites (the Poor)**: The movement inherited by James the Just after Jesus' death
- **Sadducees**: Jewish priestly nobility maintaining temple ritual
- **Pharisees**: Jewish reformers emphasizing law over sacrifice
- **Essenes**: Jewish monastic groups preparing for God's apocalyptic return
- **Gospel of Thomas**: Text discovered at Nag Hammadi in 1945, containing sayings attributed to Jesus
- **Nag Hammadi**: Location in Egypt where lost Christian texts were found in 1945
- **Gnosis**: Knowledge—specifically spiritual self-knowledge
- **The Monad**: The source, the divine, from which all consciousness emanates
- **Divine spark**: The piece of the source/monad within each person's consciousness
- **Ransom theory**: The idea that Jesus traded his life to Satan in exchange for humanity's freedom
- **Asha and Druj**: Zoroastrian concepts of cosmic harmony/truth versus division/falsehood
- **Law of attraction**: The idea that how you see the world shapes what manifests in it
- **The Grand Inquisitor**: Dostoevsky's character in The Brothers Karamazov who argues that the Catholic Church serves humanity by replacing Jesus' freedom with obedience
