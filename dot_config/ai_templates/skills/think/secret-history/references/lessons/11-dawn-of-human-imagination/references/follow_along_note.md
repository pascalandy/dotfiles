## The Dawn of Humanity — Rethinking Human History

### Overview

This is the opening lecture of a series covering the entirety of human history. The instructor challenges Darwin's theory of evolution and the materialistic worldview it introduced, arguing instead that humans are fundamentally imaginative, spiritual, and empathic beings. The lecture draws on cave paintings, ice age societies, and modern examples to build an alternative framework for understanding human nature and early civilization.

---

### Darwin and the Theory of Evolution

#### Main Idea

The lecture begins by introducing **Charles Darwin** as arguably the most influential thinker of the past two hundred years. His book *The Origin of Species* (1859) presents the **theory of evolution**, which the instructor breaks into three core claims:

1. **Accidental** — Genetic mutations are random; whichever mutation is best fitted to survive in the environment wins out.
2. **Materialistic** — Everything that you can see is all that exists. There is no spiritual or immaterial dimension.
3. **Emergent** — Complex things build on top of simpler things. It is a bottom-up process.

#### The Pre-Darwinian Christian Worldview

Before evolution, European countries operated under a Christian framework with its own three core ideas:

1. **One omniscient and omnipresent God** — The Holy Spirit is what infuses life with meaning and purpose.
2. **The world is a mystery** — Humans must hold faith; God has a plan, but it is mysterious. Humility and service are required.
3. **Equality before God** — All humans are equal in God's eyes, which meant you could not justify enslaving or killing other people.

**Connection**: The instructor sets up a direct contrast — evolution's materialistic framework dismantled the Christian conception of the universe and replaced it with one that had profound political consequences.

#### Why Evolution Took Hold

The instructor argues evolution succeeded for political, not purely scientific, reasons:

- In the nineteenth century, Europeans were engaged in **imperialism** — conquering, colonizing, and committing genocide around the world.
- They needed a theory to justify this. Darwin's concept of **survival of the fittest** provided legitimacy: the world is divided into races, the white race is superior, therefore colonization is justified.
- Evolution gave rise to **racism** (the concept of race as a hierarchical category) and **eugenics** (the idea that "inferior" people should be sterilized to protect the genetic pool).

> "Before Darwinism, we didn't really have a concept of race because everyone was equal in the eyes of God."

The instructor emphasizes that evolution is itself a **theology** — a theory about human progress and what is "right." Its implicit theology is that **might makes right**: whatever survives is what deserves to survive.

Despite being a rejection of Christianity, the theory of evolution dominated the entire Western world within twenty years of the book's publication. It became the **dominant paradigm** in schools, universities, and sciences — one that cannot be questioned without social penalty.

### Problems with Evolutionary Theory

#### Main Idea

The instructor raises several objections to evolutionary theory, not as formal scientific rebuttals but as provocative questions meant to open alternative ways of thinking.

- **Lack of species diversity**: If evolution produces tremendous diversity, why is there only one human species today? The standard answer — other species existed but we won out — is acknowledged but treated as insufficient.
- **Human migration**: All human species like to migrate, but primates don't. Why? Standard explanations (climate change, war, chasing game) don't explain people who populated the **Pacific Islands and Micronesia** — sailing into open ocean for months with no guarantee of finding land.
- **Religious behavior in early humans**: **Neanderthals** built religious ritual sites. This is puzzling from a survival standpoint — religion consumes time and resources that could be spent hunting.

> "What does religion have to do with survival? In fact, you can argue that religion is a waste of time."

**Connection**: These anomalies are used to argue that the standard evolutionary framework — humans as survival machines — cannot explain the full range of human behavior, especially our drive toward creativity, spirituality, and exploration.

### Cave Paintings and the Ice Age World

#### Main Idea

The lecture devotes significant time to **ice age cave paintings** (dating back 30,000–40,000 years) as evidence that early humans were profoundly creative, spiritual, and intentional — not primitive survival machines.

#### The Paintings Themselves

- Found deep inside caves, in cold environments with limited oxygen.
- Extremely elaborate and detailed — clearly the work of adults spending significant time and care.
- Used **red ochre** for red/yellow pigment and **charcoal** for black.
- Depicted animals (horses, bisons, lions) in lifelike and sometimes surreal, mythological compositions.

> "Whoever painted these paintings put a lot of care and devotion into them. It's not just kids playing."

#### Three Questions About Cave Paintings

The instructor organizes analysis around three questions:

1. **How are they painted?** — Collectively, using red ochre and charcoal. Because the caves lack oxygen, painters entered a **trance-like state**, hovering on the edge of consciousness. This was interpreted as actually communicating with the spirit world — channeling divine inspiration rather than exercising personal skill.

2. **What are they painting?** — The story of life itself: cycles of life and death, destruction and renewal. Animals are central because they provided food and clothing. Predators and prey are shown together, depicting the balance of nature.

3. **Why are they in caves?** — Caves were understood as **portals** connecting the material and spirit worlds. Rivers, caves, and mountaintops were all considered sites where the two worlds met. Painting animals in caves was a way of **summoning them back** — completing the cycle by thanking killed animals and calling for their return.

#### The Mythology Behind the Art

- **Bird figures** appear frequently and signify **mother earth**, shown herding animals.
- **Shamans** (like the **Löwenmensch** or "lion-man" figure, dating back 40,000 years in Germany) were believed to be leaders because of their connection to the divine. They would dress as animals to communicate with the spirit world.
- The entire system formed a **complete mythology**: balance and harmony, commemoration of the fallen, unity of all living things.

> "If you kill these animals and you eat their food, you must thank them for their sacrifice by commemorating them."

**Connection**: The instructor draws a direct parallel to modern practice — building war monuments to commemorate fallen soldiers serves the same impulse toward balance and commemoration.

### Ice Age Survival and Human Intuition

#### Main Idea

During the ice age, when the world was largely frozen, humans survived by using resources around them — for example, killing **mammoths** and turning their remains into houses. The instructor's central claim is that this knowledge was **intuitive**, not taught or planned.

#### Supporting Material: The Cooperative Painting Thought Experiment

The instructor proposes a thought experiment: put ten people in front of a blank wall, forbid communication, and ask them to draw. The prediction is that the result would be coherent and even beautiful — one person draws a sun, another a tree, another a mountain. It functions almost like telepathy.

**Connection**: This experiment illustrates the instructor's core thesis — humans possess an innate capacity for cooperation, shared imagination, and empathy that doesn't require language or planning. Early humans didn't need bosses or hierarchies; everyone intuitively knew their role.

> "If you put humans in a group, they would know instantly how to work together. They would know how to share ideas together."

#### Diversity of Organization

Early human societies were remarkably **flexible** in their social organization:

- Small groups of 5–10 for daily food gathering.
- Large gatherings of hundreds or thousands for religious festivals.
- People shifted between organizational forms based on season and location.
- If you didn't like a situation, you could leave and start your own group.

The instructor characterizes early human history as "a very diverse, very dynamic, very creative period."

### Symbols, Writing, and the Conscious Rejection of Literacy

#### Main Idea

**Genevieve von Petzinger**, a Canadian archaeologist, cataloged geometric symbols found alongside cave paintings across Europe. These symbols constitute what could be a **writing system** — yet early humans chose not to develop writing.

#### Why Humans Rejected Writing

The instructor offers three reasons:

1. **Writing as corruption** — Speaking is channeling the divine. Writing something down is counterfeiting the divine, fixing what should be living and flowing.
2. **Music cannot be captured** — For early humans, talking was closer to singing. Written text cannot capture the musical, vibrational quality of speech.
3. **Writing is solitary** — Everything in this world was meant to be **communal** — singing, painting, storytelling. Writing is an individual act that breaks the communal bond.

> "Writing was a conscious decision. It was not a technology that was invented later on. We just chose not to do it because we thought it was a corruption of the natural world."

#### The Purpose of Symbols

- **Abstraction**: Some concepts (energy, life force, cycles) cannot be drawn realistically — symbols express these ideas.
- **Visualizing spirit language**: The spirit world communicates in images; symbols represent that communication.
- **Sacralizing objects**: Drawing a symbol on a stone makes it divine, storing a memory (a birth, an event) within it.

#### Why Humans Create

The instructor identifies three fundamental reasons for artistic and symbolic creation:

1. **Bringing beauty to the world** — Celebrating the divine, showing the spirit world our gratitude.
2. **Understanding the spirit world** — Mapping how spiritual forces operate within material reality.
3. **Telling a story of identity** — Combining imagination to create society, belonging, and community.

#### Clarification: Coexistence, Not Connection

A student asks about "connecting with the divine spirits." The instructor corrects this:

> "We're not trying to connect with the divine spirit. This divine spirit is all around us. We coexist with the divine."

There is no separation between material and spiritual in this worldview. What humans do through ritual, painting, and song is **reconcile** their existence with the spirit world — for example, commemorating a killed animal through art.

### Pre-Literate People and Empathy

#### Main Idea

The instructor introduces a key claim that will recur throughout the course: **pre-literate people** — those who could not read and write — were more intuitive, more imaginative, and more empathic than modern literate humans.

- They could sense others' emotions immediately, without asking.
- Modern indigenous communities that maintain these traditions perceive the world as unified — gods are present everywhere, the home is a temple.
- These communities cannot express their worldview in modern academic language, so they are dismissed as unintelligent.

> "They're not stupid. It's just that they're focusing their energies on empathy, on imagination, on intuition."

#### Supporting Material: Inuit Art

**Inuit art** is shown as an example — depicting a unified world where gods coexist with humans, where the house is the temple.

### Empathy and Intuition in Action

#### Example 1: The Horse That Could "Do Math"

A horse appeared to solve arithmetic problems on stage. In reality, the horse was **reading the room** — watching the audience's pupils dilate, breathing quicken, and body language shift as the correct answer approached. The horse detected the right moment to stop counting.

**Connection**: This demonstrates empathy as a form of perception — reading emotional states without language. Animals can do it; humans can do it even better. The instructor calls it "our superpower."

#### Example 2: The "Telepathic" Autistic Boy in China

A mother claimed her autistic son could read her mind. A CCTV documentary crew investigated. Eventually, **artificial intelligence** analysis revealed the mother was encoding numbers in the **pitch of her voice** — differences so subtle only her son could detect them. When confronted, the mother admitted the scheme: she was poor, her son couldn't go to school, and she wanted help.

**Connection**: The instructor reframes this as remarkable rather than fraudulent:

- The mother, uneducated, identified her son's unique ability (pitch sensitivity).
- Together, they created a **"human emotional language"** so sophisticated it fooled everyone except AI.
- This demonstrates the power of human imagination driven by love.

> "That tells us the power of the human imagination if we truly love someone."

#### Example 3: Cooperative Painting (Revisited)

The thought experiment is repeated with emphasis on **why** the result would be good: not because people want to draw a great picture, but because they don't want to let each other down. Empathy and cooperation unlock maximum creative potential.

> "When you're able to find a purpose and meaning in other people, your creative potential will be fully released."

#### Example 4: Horseback Warriors

In historical nomadic societies, warriors born on horseback developed such deep emotional bonds with their horses that the horse anticipated the rider's intentions before they were consciously formed. This made mounted warriors nearly invincible — the man effectively had four legs.

#### Example 5: Dogs and Emotional Connection

Dogs demonstrate emotional connection across distance — knowing when their owner is returning home before any physical signals are present. The instructor calls this "telepathy" but defines it as an emotional connection, not literal mind-reading.

> "Telepathy exists, but it's an emotional connection."

### The Resilience of Human Imagination

#### Main Idea

Humans are fundamentally creative beings, and the imagination is **resilient** — it will always find a way to express itself, even when senses are lost or faculties diminish.

#### Supporting Material: Beethoven

**Beethoven** was deaf but composed some of the greatest music in human history. The instructor's explanation: he **saw** the music. Music is vibrations — waves that can be visualized as patterns of movement. Because Beethoven perceived music visually rather than aurally, his compositions were unique.

> "Others hear the music, but he saw the music and it turned into a movie."

#### Supporting Material: John Milton

**John Milton** wrote *Paradise Lost* while blind. He **heard** the text as music, dictating to secretaries who transcribed what he sang. This is why *Paradise Lost* reads like music — "probably the most beautiful poetry ever composed in the English language."

#### Supporting Material: Deaf Communities in China

The instructor observes that groups of deaf people communicating in restaurants in China are consistently the happiest people in the room. Sign language is part of it, but the deeper explanation is that deaf people develop **stronger emotional intelligence**, communicating empathically in ways that bring tremendous joy.

**Connection**: All these examples support the thesis that when one channel of expression is blocked, the imagination reroutes — hearing becomes sight, sight becomes sound, spoken language becomes emotional connection.

> "If you wanna express yourself, you'll find a way. It's all about will."

### Socialization into Mundanity

#### Main Idea

The instructor introduces a thesis that will recur throughout the course:

> "We are socialized into mundanity. Our true selves are divine."

Society — schools, companies, organizations — needs obedient workers, not creative individuals. The education system functions to **separate people from the divine** by removing children from parents, teaching abstract knowledge disconnected from nature, and suppressing creativity, courage, and resilience.

#### Supporting Material: The Alzheimer's Self-Portraits

An artist with Alzheimer's documented his decline through self-portraits. As the disease progressed, the portraits became increasingly abstract and fragmented. The standard reading: this shows the horror of losing one's mind.

The instructor offers an alternative reading: the artist is **shedding his artifice**, revealing his true self. Alzheimer's strips away socialization — the ability to speak, verbalize, and communicate conventionally. What remains are three things:

1. **Spiritual desire** — Many Alzheimer's patients, even lifelong atheists, suddenly want to go to church and pray.
2. **Desire to sing** — They stop talking but sing.
3. **Desire to create art** — One patient, never having painted before, began creating paintings at age eighty after developing Alzheimer's.

The instructor notes that the "hallucinations" of Alzheimer's — hearing voices, seeing things that aren't there — mirror what was normal throughout most of human history, when humans believed they coexisted with spirits and ghosts.

> "The voices were always there. We just forgot about it because we're socialized to forget."

#### Supporting Material: Psychedelic Art

Art created under the influence of psychedelics is shown as another window into the "unsocialized" mind:

- A painting of a cat reveals the **energy field**, memory, aspirations, and imagination of the animal — not just its physical form.
- Other psychedelic paintings collapse **time and space** — helicopters and cats appearing together, incomprehensible to the rational mind but reflecting how consciousness actually works.
- Colors become more vibrant — a "heightened reality."

#### Supporting Material: Vincent van Gogh

**Van Gogh** is presented as the greatest painter who ever lived, someone who shed his socialization entirely. Paintings like *Starry Night* and *Sunflowers* glow with life because van Gogh didn't care how his work communicated to others — he cared only about the picture itself.

### Five Myths About Human Nature

#### Main Idea

The instructor presents five myths that modern people are "brainwashed" into believing, and offers counter-narratives based on the framework developed in the lecture.

#### Myth 1: Humans Are Driven by Material Desires

**The myth**: We want money, genetic reproduction (marrying young beautiful women for babies), and power/status.

**The counter-narrative**: Humans are fundamentally driven by:

1. **Religious expression** through art, music, and rituals — wanting to know where we came from, why we're here, where we're going.
2. **Differentiation** — wanting to stand out, be creative, be unique.
3. **Curiosity and exploration** — which explains why humans have migrated everywhere throughout history.

> "We are fundamentally religious spiritual beings that want to create and to love and to connect."

#### Myth 2: The Family Is the Most Natural Unit, and Men Own Women

**The myth**: Men are in charge; women exist to breed.

**The counter-narrative**: For most of human history, **women controlled their own bodies** and often chose to sleep with multiple partners. This served three purposes:

1. Women enjoy it.
2. It creates **social cohesion**.
3. It's a child-rearing strategy — if no one knows who the father is, **everyone** takes responsibility for raising every child, ensuring all children thrive.

Women were the **core and center** of early societies.

#### Myth 3: Survival of the Fittest Is the Iron Rule

**The myth**: Darwin's survival of the fittest governs human behavior.

**The counter-narrative**: Throughout most of human history, humans were **compassionate**. Every creature was believed to have divinity, and those who were weakest had the most divinity. This is why killed animals were thanked and commemorated.

#### Myth 4: Civilization Is Superior to Primitive Cultures

**The myth**: Humans have gotten smarter over the centuries.

**The counter-narrative**: With civilization, **human imagination has decreased**. Pre-literate humans had:

- Better memories and information storage.
- More sensitive perception.
- Greater empathy — knowing exactly how others feel and how to help.
- Greater emotional resilience and maturity.

> "Our brains can be supercomputers if we allow them to be."

#### Myth 5: We Evolved from Apes

**The myth**: Humans are just sophisticated apes.

**The counter-narrative**: Humans are **uniquely imaginative** and can choose their own evolution.

> "If you believe you're an ape, then you're an ape."

### Romito 2: Care for the Different

#### Main Idea

The lecture concludes with the story of **Romito 2**, a dwarf discovered in a 10,000-year-old ice age burial site. Despite his deformity, he lived to old age, was buried with jewelry, and received an elaborate funeral — treatment reserved for the most valued community members.

#### Supporting Material: *The Dawn of Everything*

The instructor quotes from **David Graeber and David Wengrow's** book *The Dawn of Everything*:

> "When archaeologists undertake balanced appraisals of hunter-gatherer burials from the Paleolithic, they find high frequencies of health-related disabilities, but also surprisingly high levels of care until the time of death and beyond, since some of these funerals were remarkably lavish."

#### Three Explanations

1. **Cosmic balance and harmony** — Everyone is part of God. If someone suffers, the community must help. Concepts: unity of all, "as above, so below," setting a good example.
2. **Stronger empathy in pre-literate societies** — People simply couldn't bear to see others suffer.
3. **Appreciation of diversity as divine gift** — Difference was seen as a blessing. A dwarf might have better vision, better stories, more wisdom, stronger intuition, deeper connection to the divine. The best storytellers and musicians are often the marginalized and the outsiders.

> "If you were different, if you were deformed, if you were ill, they saw you as blessed rather than cursed."

### Student Q&A

#### Are People with Cognitive Disabilities Closer to the Divine?

A student asks whether people with cognitive disabilities in the modern age are "returning to the divine" by losing cognitive function.

The instructor's response: Everyone is connected to the divine — society just makes us forget. Schools, from an early age, remove children from parents and teach abstract knowledge that severs the connection. People who are discarded by society (autistic children, people with disabilities) sometimes **return to the divine** precisely because society stops trying to socialize them.

#### Where Does Empathy Come From?

A student suggests empathy is shaped by socialization. The instructor corrects this:

> "We are not socialized into empathy. We are socialized out of empathy. We're born into empathy."

Empathy is a **natural emotional connection** we're born with — visible in the mother-child bond. Over time, it can become almost telepathic. But it can be broken by excessive schooling, screen time, and television, leading to a zombie-like state without direction, purpose, or meaning.

#### The Mental Health Crisis

The instructor connects this framework to the modern mental health crisis:

- We're seeing an explosion of depression, anxiety, eating disorders, and suicidal tendencies, especially in the past twenty years with mobile phones and social media.
- The root cause: loss of connection to the divine. When you're born, you have an automatic connection. As you lose it, confusion leads to anxiety, then depression.
- Social media creates a **prison for the imagination** — people pursue likes and approval instead of meaning.

> "Before we cared about what makes us happy, and now we care about what we think would make other people happy."

### Key Takeaways

- Pre-literate humans were more creative, empathic, and imaginative than modern humans — not less intelligent
- Cave paintings were religious acts of commemoration and communication with the spirit world, not idle decoration
- Early humans consciously rejected writing because it corrupted the communal, musical, divine nature of speech
- Human cooperation and creativity emerge from empathy, not from hierarchy or planning
- The imagination is resilient — it will find expression even when senses are lost (Beethoven, Milton)
- Modern society socializes people out of their innate connection to the divine, empathy, and creativity
- Early societies treated the different and disabled as blessed, not cursed — diversity was a divine gift
- The modern mental health crisis stems from severed emotional connections and imprisoned imagination

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Theory of Evolution**: Darwin's framework — accidental, materialistic, emergent — explaining life through natural selection and survival of the fittest
- **Eugenics**: The belief that genetically "inferior" people should be sterilized to protect the gene pool
- **Pre-literate**: People who could not (or chose not to) read and write; characterized here as more intuitive and empathic
- **Löwenmensch (Lion-man)**: A 40,000-year-old figurine from Germany depicting a shaman as half-human, half-animal
- **Red Ochre**: Natural pigment used to create red/yellow color in cave paintings
- **Mundanity**: The state of ordinariness that society socializes people into, suppressing their divine creative nature
- **Romito 2**: A dwarf from a 10,000-year-old burial site, evidence that early societies cared for and honored the disabled
