## The Rise of the Catholic Church: Paul's Construction of Christianity

### Overview

This lecture examines how Paul transformed Jesus' teachings into an institutional religion by syncretizing Roman, Greek, and Jewish cultures. The lecturer argues that Paul was a sophisticated rhetorician—possibly a Roman agent or double agent—who replaced Jesus' message of inner divine spark and personal freedom with a system of obedience, hierarchy, and spiritual enslavement modeled on the Roman paterfamilias. The lecture includes close readings of Paul's letters and Augustine's *City of God* to demonstrate how sophistry was used to invert Jesus' original teachings.

---

### Review of Last Class: The Mission of Jesus

#### Main Idea

Jesus was a messenger from the **monad** who reminded people of a **divine spark** within them. Activating that spark required focusing on Jesus' words—his poetry, his sayings—not on Jesus as a person. Jesus stood in a lineage of "poor prophets" including Zarathustra, Plato, Homer, and the Yahwehs.

#### Why the Romans Killed Jesus

The Roman Empire's economy ran on slavery. Most people were slaves or poor. Jesus told slaves they were free, that a power higher than Rome existed—the divine, the universe. People began defying their masters and losing their fear of death. Jesus was therefore a direct economic threat.

> "Jesus was killed because he was a thief. He was stealing from the Romans."

Jesus was crucified alongside two thieves—the lecturer frames this as deliberate symbolism. Slaves were property; freeing them destroyed Roman wealth.

#### The Central Contradiction: Jesus vs. the Catholic Church

- **Jesus**: The divine spark is within you. Listen to your heart. You are free. You can reconnect with the monad yourself.
- **Catholic Church**: Believe in Jesus and you'll be saved. Jesus died for your sins. You are now a slave to Jesus.

These are presented as opposite ideas. The lecturer argues the Romans created the Catholic church as a **prison** to control and hide the divine spark, turning it into an economic commodity.

### Paul: Background and the Three Mysteries

#### Main Idea

**Paul** is identified as the true founder of Christianity. A third of the New Testament is either written by Paul or about Paul—more content than about Jesus himself.

> "Christianity is a religion about Jesus, but not by Jesus. It's by Paul."

#### Paul's Background

- A **Hellenized Jew**: Jewish by heritage but raised in Greek culture
- Very well educated, very wealthy
- Trained primarily in **rhetoric** (sophistry)
- Likely working for the Roman Empire as a spy or agent

#### The Three Scholarly Mysteries of Paul

1. Why would a literate, wealthy Hellenized Jew join a movement of illiterate Jewish peasants?
2. He was a fanatical Pharisee hunting Christians—then converts to become a fanatical follower of Jesus. Why?
3. He spent his entire life traveling the Roman Empire promoting Christianity with secretaries. Where did the money come from?

The lecturer's conclusion from basic game theory: Paul was clearly a spy. The question is for whom—Rome, the Jewish leadership, or both.

### Philosophers vs. Sophists

#### Main Idea

Both terms derive from **Sophia** (Greek for knowledge), but they represent fundamentally opposed worldviews. This distinction is essential for understanding Paul's method.

- **Philosophers**: Love knowledge. Believe in eternal truth. Goal is liberation into the light (Plato's allegory of the cave).
- **Sophists**: Believe eternal truth does not exist. Words are reality itself. What matters is mastering language to **construct** reality.

**Sophistry** gives us the word for trickery. Paul was trained in this tradition.

#### Paul's Great Trick

> Jesus says: "Listen to my words and you will find the divine spark in you."
> Paul says: "Believe in Jesus and you'll be saved."

**Connection**: Paul shifts focus from the *words* of Jesus to the *person* of Jesus. This is the foundational act of sophistry that enables everything that follows.

### Paul's Syncretism: Three Cultures Combined

#### Main Idea

Paul constructs Christianity by syncretizing three major cultures of his time: Jewish, Greek, and Roman. Each contributes a structural element to the new religion.

#### Culture 1: Jewish — The Messiah Redefined

- The Bible had been heavily influenced by Persian monotheism (Ahura Mazda vs. the Druze, good vs. evil)
- Jews adopted Persian **eschatology** (end of the world)
- Jews awaited a **messiah of war** who would lead them against Rome and restore the Davidic kingdom
- Paul's move: Jesus is not the messiah of war but the **messiah of peace**. Jews should not resist the Romans.

#### Culture 2: Greek — The Mystery Schools

- Greek **mystery schools** were cults that become the basis of secret societies
- Paul chose the cult of **Dionysus** (god of creativity, wine)
- The Dionysus story: born of a virgin mother, father is Zeus, killed by enemies of Zeus, then resurrected by Zeus
- This becomes the story of Jesus
- Dionysus worshippers held a **ritual meal** where they believed they ate the body of Dionysus, absorbing his energy, achieving communion with him
- Christians adopted the same ritual: the **Eucharist** (thanksgiving/communion)

#### Culture 3: Roman — The Paterfamilias

- Roman social structure: **paterfamilias** — one patriarch at top, sons below, slaves at bottom
- The patriarch is always right; he is god within his household
- Promising slaves are freed and become **clients** (business people conducting commerce on the patriarch's behalf)
- This creates a business empire; the Roman Empire is a platform where dozens of families control provinces — a **franchise model**

**Connection**: Paul copies the paterfamilias structure exactly to create the church. Bishop at the top, cardinals below, then followers/disciples. This made conversion easy — you could switch from the Roman system to the church system seamlessly.

### Why Romans Adopted Paul's System

#### Main Idea

Paul's formulation solved the "Jesus problem" for Roman patriarchs in three ways:

1. **Better slave exploitation**: Before Jesus, lazy slaves got whipped. With Paul's Christianity, lazy slaves burn in hell for eternity. Slaves now work not just to stay alive but to earn eternal paradise. A few decades of slavery for an eternity of joy — "pretty good deal."

2. **From king to god**: Private wealth transforms into spiritual authority. You can't be robbed or killed as easily because attacking a church leader means incurring God's wrath. Gold stored in the Catholic church is untouchable.

3. **Faster expansion**: Before, acquiring slaves required buying them. Now, you just convert people. Religion spreads faster than commerce.

> "Jesus was talking to slaves, but Paul was talking to patriarchs."

When patriarchs converted, they brought their entire system with them. This explains the rapid growth of Christianity.

#### The Two-Tier Religion

- **Slaves** practice Christianity
- **Patriarchs** practice the Greek mystery cults in secret

The patriarchs use mystery cults to bond with each other and coordinate. This becomes the basis for **secret societies** — a system the lecturer claims persists today.

### Jesus vs. Paul: Three Core Oppositions

#### Opposition 1: Self-Salvation vs. Belief-Based Redemption

- **Jesus**: The divine spark is within you. Save yourself. Seek truth yourself.
- **Paul**: Jesus came to redeem us from original sin. Believe in Jesus and you'll be saved. It doesn't matter what you do — without belief in Jesus, you burn in hell.

#### Opposition 2: Independent Thinking vs. Conformity

- **Jesus**: He can guide you, give you poetry to decipher, but you must seek truth yourself.
- **Paul**: Don't think for yourself. Do what everyone else does. Church cohesion is paramount. Obey the word of Jesus (which is really the word of Paul).

#### Opposition 3: Wealth and the Kingdom

- **Jesus**: The rich cannot enter heaven. Spiritual rejuvenation requires focus beyond material acquisition. The parable of the great feast — the rich won't attend because they're too busy making money.
- **Paul**: The rich will lead us to the kingdom because they finance the spreading of Christianity. What matters is metrics — how many souls saved, how many converted, how cohesive the church is.

> "Jesus is a prophet, but Paul is a business person."

### Early Christianities and the Suppression of Diversity

#### Main Idea

Around year 40-50 CE, many different Christianities existed because Jesus taught that the kingdom of heaven is within you — leading to diverse, democratic communities. Many early leaders were **women** (e.g., Mary Magdalene), often wealthy and charismatic.

Paul's Christianity was the opposite: anti-woman, strict hierarchy, orthodoxy.

> "You may have heard that early Christians were persecuted. Guess what? They're being persecuted by Paul."

Paul's Christianity was a small minority initially. Jesus' Christianity was far more appealing — it taught freedom, empowerment, liberation. Paul taught obedience, discipline, sacrifice. But Paul won because he had Roman Empire backing.

### The Bible's Account: Jews Killing Jesus

#### Main Idea

The Bible claims Jews killed Jesus, but the lecturer argues this is unreliable. Key reasons:

- Jews don't turn each other over to Roman authorities. If someone commits blasphemy, they stone them — they don't involve Rome.
- The Romans killed Jesus because he threatened their social structure.

#### Close Reading: Gospel of John

The passage shows Pontius Pilate interrogating Jesus, finding no fault, whipping him to appease the Jewish priests, then trying to release him. The chief priests and police shout "crucify him." Pilate repeatedly says he finds no case against Jesus.

**Connection**: The lecturer uses this to demonstrate the Bible's anti-Jewish bias and its unreliability as historical record.

### Paul's Conversion Story: Acts of the Apostles

#### The Road to Damascus

Paul (then Saul) is persecuting Christians as a Pharisee. On the road to Damascus, a light from heaven blinds him. Jesus says: "Why do you persecute me?" Paul is blind for three days, neither eating nor drinking.

The lecturer notes:
- The story mirrors **Plato's allegory of the cave** — darkness to blinding light to truth. Paul, trained in Greek culture, would know this model.
- Despite Jesus supposedly favoring Paul, Paul's letters rarely quote Jesus, rarely discuss Jesus' actual teachings, and rarely reference Jesus' story directly.
- Jesus doesn't talk much about good and evil, the Bible, or himself as son of God — but Paul talks about these things constantly.

### Paul vs. James the Just

#### Main Idea

**James the Just**, brother of Jesus, inherited the movement and was based in Jerusalem. As Paul spread his version of Christianity, conflict arose because Paul was teaching things James knew were wrong — particularly that Jesus was God.

> James' position: "Of course he's not God. He's my brother, man."

#### The Circumcision Conflict

The Bible frames the conflict as ideological: both agreed Jesus was the son of God, but James wanted to keep the faith within Jewish tradition while Paul wanted to expand to Gentiles (Greeks and Romans).

The practical point of conflict: **circumcision**. In Greek cities, men exercised naked in the gymnasium — circumcision was immediately visible and marked Jews as different. Paul said converts didn't need circumcision. Jewish leaders complained to James.

#### Paul in Jerusalem — Close Reading from Acts

When Paul goes to Jerusalem, a mob wants to kill him. Roman soldiers intervene. Paul reveals he is a **Roman citizen** — this saves him.

Key observations from the lecturer:
- The text is **anti-Jewish**: Jews are portrayed as an irrational mob wanting to kill Paul for no reason
- The text is **pro-Roman**: Roman soldiers save Paul
- The pattern mirrors Jesus' story — but while God couldn't save Jesus, Rome saves Paul
- This is **propaganda** aimed at Hellenized Jews wavering between loyalty to Rome and Jewish tradition. Paul's message: become Christian and you can be both.

### Paul in Rome — Close Reading from Acts

#### Main Idea

Paul arrives in Rome, claims Roman citizenship, demands an audience with the emperor. He then meets with Jewish leaders in Rome, essentially threatening them — if they oppose him, he can unleash the empire on them. He preaches to them, converts some, then gets angry at those who refuse.

> Paul quotes Isaiah to the unconverted Jews: "You will indeed listen but never understand... For this people's heart has grown dull."

Then he declares: salvation has been sent to the Gentiles — they will listen.

#### The Double Agent Question

The lecturer notes: Paul appears blatantly to be a Roman agent. But why would a spy be so obvious? This leads to the possibility that Paul was a **double agent** — pretending to work for Rome while actually advancing Jewish interests. By spreading Christianity, he spreads the Bible, which says Jews are God's chosen people, elevating Jewish status throughout the empire.

> "The thing that you have to remember with spies is they have no loyalties. They are double agents. They are triple agents."

### Paul's Rhetoric: Close Readings from His Letters

#### On Circumcision (Romans)

Paul argues: what matters is not physical circumcision but circumcision of the heart — spiritual, not literal. A true Jew is one who is Jewish inwardly.

> "A person is a Jew who is sworn inwardly, and real circumcision is a matter of the heart."

**Connection**: The lecturer identifies this as classic sophistry — exchanging the definition of a word. Circumcision is a covenant between Jews and God; Paul redefines it as internal faith.

#### On Faith vs. Law (Romans)

Paul teaches: righteousness comes through faith in Jesus Christ, not through following Jewish law. Jesus was put forward as a sacrifice of atonement. Only belief in Jesus saves — not good works, not following laws.

> "The righteousness of God through faith in Jesus Christ for all who believe."

#### On Internal Disagreements (1 Corinthians)

Other Christians challenge Paul: this isn't what Jesus taught. Paul's response is to position himself above the debate. He doesn't engage with what Jesus actually taught. Instead:

> "I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the growth."

His argument: don't argue about whose interpretation is right. What matters is spreading the message. Whoever builds the biggest church wins — business success validates theological correctness.

**Connection**: The lecturer identifies this as using metrics of material acquisition to justify faith — the opposite of what Jesus taught.

#### On Slavery (1 Corinthians)

> "When you were a slave when called, do not be concerned about it... For whoever was called in the Lord as a slave is a free person belonging to the Lord."

Paul's move: you are free — but you're a slave to God. No human can master you because you're already God's slave. The lecturer calls this deliberate confusion.

#### On Becoming All Things (1 Corinthians)

> "To the Jews, I became as a Jew... To those outside the law, I became as one outside the law... I become all things to all people that I might by all means save some."

The lecturer compares this to a business pep rally:

> "Go out and sell those damn hamburgers, man. I don't care what you do. I don't care how many lies you tell. Go sell those hamburgers."

### The Eucharist: Destroying Individuality

#### Main Idea

The **Eucharist** (communion/thanksgiving) is presented as the most important Christian ritual — eating bread and drinking wine believed to be the flesh and blood of Jesus.

The lecturer's interpretation: this negates the divine spark. The divine spark is free, individual, independent. When you partake in the body of Jesus, you become one with everyone else — your individuality is destroyed. You become the church itself. It is **possession by Jesus**.

#### Greek Mystery Cult Origins

The Eucharist derives from Dionysian mystery cult rituals where participants ate food believing they consumed the body of Dionysus, absorbing his energy. The lecturer describes these cults as "demonic" — involving ritual sacrifices where participants would tear bodies apart and consume flesh, replicating the death of Dionysus.

#### Historical Context: Cannibalism Through History

The lecturer provides a broader context for ritual consumption:

- **Magdalenian age** (15,000 years ago): cannibalism was common for religious purposes — consuming a loved one's body after death to maintain eternal communion with their spirit
- **Aztecs**: cannibalism for war preparation and sacrifice
- **Victorian Britain** (19th century): the British elite stole Egyptian mummies and ate them, believing they had magical properties
- **Albert Einstein's brain**: Einstein explicitly asked that his brain not be dissected. A doctor stole it and cut it into pieces anyway. The lecturer asks: could someone sell pieces of Einstein's brain to rich people for a million dollars each? Answer: yes.

#### The Body of Christ Metaphor (1 Corinthians)

Paul uses the metaphor of the body — foot, hand, eye, ear — to argue that all members of the church must work together as one body. Each person has consumed a part of Christ; they must come together to form the whole body of Jesus.

**Connection**: This requires complete obedience. The church has a hierarchy ordained by Jesus: apostles, prophets, teachers, then gifts of healing, forms of leadership, etc.

### Paul's Redefinition of Love (1 Corinthians 13)

#### Main Idea

The famous "love is patient, love is kind" passage. The lecturer argues that Paul uses the word **love** but actually means **obedience**.

> "He switches his words. He's saying that slavery is now love."

The logic: when a human master enslaves you, that's evil. When Jesus enslaves you, it's love — because Jesus loves you. And because he loves you, you must obey him completely. Never question the authority of the church.

### Augustine: Systematizing Paul's Ideas

#### Main Idea

**Augustine** writes *City of God* after Christianity becomes the official religion of the Roman Empire (~300 CE). He places the Catholic church above Rome — Rome is temporal, but the Catholic church is the divine city, the new Jerusalem.

#### Original Sin and the Evil of Self-Trust

Augustine takes Jesus' teaching of the divine spark and inverts it: yes, there's something in you — but it's from **Satan**, not from God. You cannot trust it.

> "Pride is the start of every kind of sin."

Jesus says follow your own heart. Augustine says that leads to pride, and pride means wanting to be better than God, wanting to kill God. Therefore the heart's impulse comes from Satan.

#### Why Humans Are Flawed

The Bible says humans are created from dust — created from nothing. Augustine concludes: we are a "failed science experiment." That's why we can't trust ourselves and must believe in the church completely.

> "To abandon God and to exist in oneself, that is to please oneself, is not immediately to lose all being, but it is to come near to nothingness."

#### Obedience as the Only Path

Augustine defines freedom as complete obedience — when you're with God, you won't even think about disobeying. The church hierarchy: priests represent God on earth, the pope has supreme authority, whatever the pope says is the word of God.

A student asks about making mistakes. The lecturer contrasts:
- **Jesus**: It's okay to make mistakes. You'll be forgiven.
- **Catholic Church**: It's not okay to screw up.

#### Main Idea

The lecturer draws an analogy between Paul and **Ray Kroc**, founder of the McDonald's empire. Both took an existing idea and built a franchise system around it.

- McDonald's restaurants look the same everywhere — same building, same menu
- Paul's churches follow the same pattern — same ritual, same hierarchy
- Ray Kroc didn't need to convince people to eat hamburgers; he needed to convince small business owners to invest in the franchise
- Paul didn't need to convince slaves; he needed to convince patriarchs to adopt the system

**To Jewish Leaders:**
1. Spread our faith to the Gentiles and elevate our status as God's chosen people

**To Greeks and Common People:**
