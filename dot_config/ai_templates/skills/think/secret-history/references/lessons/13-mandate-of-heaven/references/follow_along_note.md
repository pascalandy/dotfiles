## The Origins of Civilization, Mythology, and Writing

### Overview

This lecture presents an alternative framework for understanding how civilization emerged. It challenges the traditional Marxist model—where agriculture leads to surplus, which leads to elites, which leads to culture—and argues instead that humans were always capable of religion, art, and science. Civilization, in this view, is a device created by elites to legitimize hierarchical power structures through mythology, writing, and propaganda.

---

### The Traditional (Marxist) Framework

#### Main Idea

The dominant understanding taught in schools follows a linear progression proposed by Karl Marx:

1. Humans begin as hunter-gatherers, struggling to find food
2. Discovery of agriculture creates **surplus** (more food than needed)
3. Surplus frees up an elite class from labor
4. This elite develops religion, arts, and science/technology
5. These developments enable cities, irrigation, hereditary elites, writing, money, and property

These four elements—cities, hereditary elite, writing, and money/property—are collectively called **civilization**.

The lecture notes that civilization brings both positive outcomes (religion, arts, science) and negative ones (war, slavery, debt).

---

### The Alternative Framework

#### Main Idea

The lecturer's counter-argument: humans did not need an elite class to produce religion, art, or science. From the very beginning, humans were religious, artistic, and scientifically capable.

#### Supporting Material: Cave Paintings and Göbekli Tepe

Cave paintings and religious settlements like **Göbekli Tepe** and **Karahan Tepe** demonstrate that pre-civilizational humans were already producing sophisticated art and building monumental structures for religious purposes.

**Connection**: If humans could do all this before agriculture and surplus, the Marxist model's assumption—that we needed elites to unlock creativity—falls apart.

#### The Alternative Sequence

1. People come together to practice religion, building temples and monuments
2. Farming develops around these temples to sustain religious practice
3. Temple leaders become corrupt over time—shifting from elected to hereditary
4. People leave and build temples elsewhere when leaders engage in **rent-seeking**
5. Some temple locations sit at major trade crossroads, making their real estate too valuable to abandon
6. These locations grow into large cities

#### The Temple Economy

As cities grow, leaders create a **temple economy**—a primal form of taxation. Everyone brings food to the temple; priests redistribute it. This enables public works (mainly irrigation) and more temples.

The temple economy creates the need for:

- **Writing**: to record food stores, rations, and trade
- **Money**: to facilitate exchange

#### Writing as Propaganda

A strict hierarchy emerges that goes against what the lecturer calls the "natural order." Since people can always leave, elites must create **mythology**—written down to appear as if it comes from the gods themselves—to convince people the hierarchy is legitimate.

> "Civilization did not give us religion, art, and science. We already had these things."

> "Civilization is a device meant to gaslight or fool people into believing that a hierarchy is legitimate when it is not legitimate."

### The Four Earliest Civilizations

#### Main Idea

The four earliest major civilizations are **Egypt**, **Mesopotamia** (modern Iraq), the **Indus Valley** (also called **Harappan**, after its capital Harappa), and **China**.

#### Three Shared Characteristics

1. **Latitude**: All sit at roughly the same latitude—not too hot, not too cold—ideal for agriculture
2. **Major rivers**: Egypt has the Nile; Mesopotamia has the Tigris and Euphrates; Indus Valley has the Indus; China has the Yellow River. Rivers solve water supply, transportation, and agricultural needs
3. **Access to the sea/ocean**: Egypt has the Mediterranean and Red Sea; Mesopotamia and Indus Valley access the Arabian Sea and Indian Ocean; China accesses the Pacific. Sea access enables trade, new goods, people, and ideas

#### Growth Pattern

Large cities develop at trade locations, then establish **colonies upstream and downstream** of the river to expand trade reach. Trade is the priority driving expansion.

#### Western Civilization as an Integrated System

The lecturer argues that Egypt, Mesopotamia, and the Indus Valley collectively form the foundations of **Western civilization**—not just Europe and America as commonly assumed. These three regions were always in contact through trade and communication, influencing each other.

China is treated as a special case because the Himalayas limited its trade connections with the other three.

> "You cannot understand development of Egypt in isolation from Mesopotamia and the Indus Valley."

### Sumer: The Cradle of Civilization

#### Main Idea

**Sumer** (Sumeria), located at the center of global trade routes, is considered the cradle of civilization. It sits in the **Fertile Crescent**, making it both strategically important and extremely fertile.

#### Why Sumer Was First

Looking at the map, Sumer connects everyone to everyone else. If the Indus Valley wanted to reach Egypt, goods had to pass through Sumer first. This centrality drove the invention of writing, irrigation, and many technologies.

#### The Language Mystery

Sumerian language is unrelated to surrounding languages, leading scholars to theorize Sumerians came from elsewhere. The lecturer suggests an alternative: different peoples converged at this trade hub and organically formed their own language.

#### Debunking the Anunnaki Alien Theory

Some internet theories claim aliens called the **Anunnaki** created humans in Sumer. The lecturer dismisses this firmly.

> "Necessity is the mother of creativity."

**Connection**: When diverse peoples are forced together by trade, they rapidly develop shared language, writing, and civilization—no aliens required.

#### Cuneiform

**Cuneiform** is the first writing system ever invented. The process: take clay from riverbeds, inscribe marks with a reed before it hardens, then dry it in the sun. Because clay tablets don't decay, we know more about Sumer than many other ancient civilizations.

### History as a Process of Inversion

#### Main Idea

A key principle of human history: it is a **constant process of inversion**, where the old order is dethroned by the new order.

#### The Sequence of Inversions

1. **Animistic era**: Egalitarian, fluid, dynamic society. Everything contains God
2. **Mother goddess era**: Agriculture requires fertility. The goddess serves humans—she is kind and compassionate. Balance and harmony
3. **Sky god era**: As societies grow and go to war, males overtake females. Instead of gods serving humans, humans must serve the gods. Values shift to struggle, exploitation, and toil
4. **Prince kills king**: Hereditary succession breeds civil wars—the son overthrows the father
5. **Bureaucrats overthrow the king**: As populations grow, bureaucracies form, and bureaucrats collude to steal power from the king

> "The servant rules the king."

#### Sky Gods Across Civilizations

Different societies have different sky gods representing the same concept:

- Egypt: **Ra**
- Babylon: **Marduk**
- Greece: **Zeus**
- Rome: **Jupiter**

The sky god demands struggle, toil, conquest, exploitation of nature (the mother goddess), and enslavement of others.

### The Enuma Elish

#### Main Idea

The **Enuma Elish** ("from up high") is the most famous epic from Mesopotamia—a creation myth that functioned as propaganda to justify the existing power structure.

#### The Story

1. In the beginning: two gods exist—**Apsu** (fresh water/rivers) and **Tiamat** (salt water/ocean)
2. They come together and create all life, including their children, the new gods
3. Apsu wants to kill the noisy children; Tiamat warns the children, who rebel and kill Apsu
4. Tiamat, furious about her husband's death, raises an army to kill her children
5. The children elect **Marduk** (the Thunder God, Sky God) as champion
6. Marduk kills Tiamat and splits her body "like a dried fish"—one half becomes the sky, the other becomes Earth
7. Marduk creates a calendar and bureaucratic order
8. Marduk builds his temple (Babylon) and creates humans from the blood of his enemy **Kingu** to serve as slaves to the gods
9. Marduk establishes a divine hierarchy among humans—some are "better slaves" than others

> "He split her into two like a dried fish."

#### Why It Was Written Down

The lecturer emphasizes that oral tradition already preserved these stories perfectly—everyone could memorize them line for line. Writing them in stone served **propaganda purposes**. Seeing something written creates an aura of divine authority, similar to how modern film mesmerizes audiences into accepting its message uncritically.

#### Key Interpretive Points

- Marduk killing Tiamat represents the inversion from mother goddess values (balance, harmony) to sky god values (destruction, exploitation, control)
- The bureaucratic calendar and order are presented as divinely ordained rather than human inventions
- Humans are defined as slaves created to serve gods—justifying the social hierarchy
- Babylon is declared a divine city built by gods for gods, making it impossible to leave without defying divine order
- Marduk has multiple names (Bel, El) because the epic consolidates different traditions into one composite story

**Connection**: The Enuma Elish demonstrates exactly how mythology functions as a tool of social control—encoding the power structure as cosmic truth.

### Different Writing Technologies

The lecture briefly notes that different civilizations developed different writing media based on available materials:

- **Mesopotamia**: Clay tablets (highly durable—why we know so much about Sumer)
- **Egypt**: **Papyrus** (plant-based scrolls—less durable, so we know less about Egypt)
- **China**: Paper

### The Epic of Gilgamesh

#### Main Idea

The **Epic of Gilgamesh** is the second major literary achievement of Mesopotamia. It tells the story of Gilgamesh, the first king of Uruk, and his quest for immortality.

#### The Story

1. Gilgamesh is a tyrant king—takes men to war, sleeps with all women
2. People cry to the gods for relief
3. Gods create **Enkidu** from clay (parallel to Adam in the Bible)—a wild, animal-like being
4. Gilgamesh sends a prostitute to civilize Enkidu
5. Gilgamesh and Enkidu fight, realize neither can win, become best friends
6. They go on adventures together—killing divine beings, the protector of the forest, a divine bull
7. Gods decide one must die; they kill Enkidu
8. Heartbroken and afraid of death, Gilgamesh quests for immortality
9. He fails—but returns home to find happiness in his city and people

#### The Moral

> Immortality is not about living forever. Immortality is about doing great things for your people so that you'll be remembered forever.

The irony: Gilgamesh failed his quest but succeeded in his mission—we still celebrate him today through his epic.

#### Theory of Kingship

One interpretation: the epic defines what kingship should be. Being a king means serving the people, not doing whatever you want. Good service leads to immortalization in writing.

### How Stories Evolve: The Process of Mythological Construction

#### Main Idea

The lecturer explains a multi-stage process by which local legends become consolidated epics that serve bureaucratic purposes. The Epic of Gilgamesh is a product of this process.

#### Stage 1: Local Legends

Each region has local heroes celebrated through stories of extraordinary deeds. Local kings claim descent from these heroes to justify their rule. The stories also serve as tests—if your ancestor fought a lion, you should prove yourself similarly.

#### Stage 2: Consolidation

As regions merge under one ruler, their separate hero stories are combined into a single, larger narrative. Different heroes become one composite figure.

#### Stage 3: Bureaucratic Editing

Bureaucratic priests gain power and add morality and messaging to control the people and the king. The major message: the king must serve the people, and the priests control the king.

Different bureaucratic factions produce different versions as power shifts.

#### Supporting Material: The University Legends Analogy

The lecturer illustrates this with a vivid modern example:

**Stage 1 — Local legends emerge:**
- Ohio State: "Michael James gets drunk the night before an exam and still passes"
- Connecticut: "A football player scores a winning touchdown"
- Middlebury: "Pat Jack takes a piss in a random place"

**Stage 2 — Oral tradition exaggerates:**
- Michael James now gets drunk before every exam and scores 100, then throws up on the professor
- The football player scores touchdowns every game, then also hits a home run to win a championship
- Pat Jack drives to Canada and pisses on a sleeping bear, then has to be rescued from a tree

**Stage 3 — Consolidation:**
All three legends merge: "Harvard's most legendary student was Pitbull James. He once wrote an exam no professor could pass. He hit a home run with one arm. He pissed on a sleeping bear just so he could knock it out with one punch."

**Stage 4 — Bureaucratic editing:**
A single line is added to change the story's purpose:
- Version 1: "When he became a billionaire, he gave it all to Harvard" — the institution demands tribute
- Version 2: "He found his true love and settled down to write" — intellectuals over athletes
- Version 3: "The bear ate him" — don't be reckless

**Connection**: This mirrors exactly how the Epic of Gilgamesh was constructed—and how Chinese classics like *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*, *Journey to the West*, and *Water Margin* were likely more colorful before bureaucrats edited them into didactic texts for schools.

> "The bureaucrats took them and changed them into boring stories that they can now teach school children to brainwash them."

### Hesiod's Theogony and the Pattern of Succession

#### Main Idea

Greek mythology follows the same pattern of inversion seen across civilizations.

#### The Succession Myth

1. **Gaea** (mother goddess) and **Chaos** give birth to the gods
2. Gaea marries **Uranus**; they produce twelve Titans
3. Uranus beats his children; the youngest son **Cronus** kills Uranus with Gaea's help
4. Cronus, fearing his children will rebel like he did, eats them all
5. **Rhea** (the mother) gives birth to **Zeus** in secret on an island
6. Zeus grows up, becomes Cronus's cup bearer, and poisons him
7. Zeus becomes the ultimate king

#### Historical Pattern Behind the Myth

The myth encodes three layers:

1. **Animistic layer**: Gods come together and create humans
2. **Kingship layer**: Son rebels against father (Cronus vs. Uranus)
3. **Mercenary layer**: A foreign general of low birth overthrows the king

#### The Foreign Mercenary Pattern

Kings often hire foreign mercenaries as generals because they have no legitimacy and no local faction. But talented mercenaries build their own factions and overthrow the king. Historical examples:

- **Sargon of Akkad** (founded the Akkadian Empire)
- **King David** (founded Israel)
- **Genghis Khan** (Mongol Empire)
- **Romulus and Remus** (founded Rome)
- The founder of China's Song dynasty

The problem: after seizing power, the mercenary-king has a **legitimacy crisis** (low birth, foreigner). The myth of Zeus solves this—he was of low birth and raised in secret, but was actually the secret son of a king.

**Connection**: Mythology serves as a retroactive legitimization tool. The succession myths aren't just stories—they're templates that real historical figures use to justify their rule.

### The Debate Between Sheep and Grain

#### Main Idea

This Sumerian text illustrates how mythology was used to convince pastoralists to abandon their free lifestyle and become sedentary farmers—because farmers are easier to control.

#### The Context

Mesopotamia had two forms of agriculture:

- **Sedentary**: Stay in one place, grow crops
- **Pastoral**: Move around, raise sheep and goats

Kings and bureaucrats prefer sedentary farmers because they're easier to tax and control.

#### The Sheep's Argument

The sheep claims superiority: it provides clothing, sandals, oil, food for soldiers, and wool worn by the king. Priests sacrifice sheep, not grain. The sheep mocks grain's tools as destructible.

#### The Grain's Argument

Grain counters: it doesn't need protection from predators, it's independent, requires less labor. It mocks the pastoral life—herdsmen are chased away, live in fear of snakes and desert creatures, and are constantly counted and tallied.

#### The Verdict

The gods declare grain superior—even though pastoralists are stronger, freer, and more independent. This is precisely the point: the myth is designed to brainwash people out of freedom and into a controllable agricultural life.

> "They create these mythologies and want to brainwash people out of their freedom, out of their independence. And that's why we have writing."

### Q&A: Cross-Cultural Mythological Influence

#### Question: Did proximity between civilizations affect their mythology?

The lecturer's answer: It's almost impossible to determine exactly how much these civilizations influenced each other's myths. Even without contact, they would have developed similar myths to justify their hierarchies—myth-making is a natural human process. However, elites also needed **differentiation**:

- Egypt builds pyramids to prove superiority
- Mesopotamia creates ziggurats and the Epic of Gilgamesh

The analogy offered: How much are you influenced by American pop culture? Probably a lot—but how much exactly? And whatever influence you absorb, you still refract it for your own needs.

#### Question: Why is the Chinese creator god Pangu male while other traditions have female creators?

The lecturer's response: Originally, all creation myths were likely **asexual**—either non-gendered or both male and female (as in yin and yang). Over time, characteristics were changed to reflect the ruling hierarchy. Pangu may have originally been female, changed to male as male-dominated power structures took hold. It's nearly impossible to reconstruct the original versions.

### Key Takeaways

- Humans have always been capable of religion, art, and science—civilization didn't create these capacities
- Civilization is fundamentally a system for legitimizing hierarchy through mythology, writing, and propaganda
- Trade location (latitude, rivers, sea access) determined which settlements grew into civilizations
- Sumer became the first major civilization because of its position at the center of global trade
- History operates through a recurring pattern of **inversion**—old orders overthrown by new ones
- Myths are layered constructions: local legends get exaggerated, consolidated, then edited by bureaucrats for social control
- Writing was invented primarily to record economic transactions and encode propaganda, not for creative expression
- Study mythology carefully and you can decode the hidden history of a civilization

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Surplus**: More food than can be consumed, theorized to enable elite classes
- **Temple economy**: Early taxation system where food is brought to the temple and redistributed by priests
- **Cuneiform**: First writing system, inscribed on clay tablets
- **Inversion**: The recurring historical pattern where the subordinate overthrows the dominant order
- **Enuma Elish**: Babylonian creation myth; means "from up high"
- **Ziggurat**: Mesopotamian temple considered the home of the gods; center of civilization
- **Anunnaki**: Gods in Sumerian mythology (not aliens)
- **Pastoralist**: Someone who raises livestock and moves with them, as opposed to sedentary farmers
- **Fertile Crescent**: The highly productive agricultural region encompassing Mesopotamia
- **Harappan**: Another name for the Indus Valley civilization, after its capital Harappa
- **Rent-seeking**: When temple leaders exploit their position for personal gain rather than serving the people
