## Homer, the Iliad, and the Greek Mind

### Overview

This is a university lecture on Greek civilization, centering on Homer's Iliad as the "big bang" of Greek culture. The lecture moves through three major arcs: the political conditions that made Homer possible, the plot and psychological depth of the Iliad, and a theory about how the ancient mind differed from the modern one—specifically through Julian Jaynes' bicameral mind hypothesis. The throughline is that forgiveness is the deepest problem in human society, and Homer understood this three thousand years ago.

---

### Review: From Open Competition to Empire to Greece

#### Main Idea

The lecture opens with a recap of a recurring framework: civilizations begin with city-states along trade routes, which compete openly. This **open cooperative competition** drives innovation. Over time, an empire emerges and creates a **bureaucracy** with three characteristics:

- **Centralization** — monopoly replaces competition, innovation declines
- **Censorship** — information is controlled, free expression disappears
- **Writing as propaganda** — writing serves control rather than knowledge or self-expression

This pattern holds for China, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Mycenaean Greece.

#### The Greek Exception

When the Bronze Age collapsed and Mycenaean Greece fell, the result was a return to decentralization. The **polis** (city-state) system re-emerged, restoring open warfare and competition. The word "politics" comes from polis—because these city-states were perpetually at war.

**Connection**: Because every citizen had to risk his life in war, every citizen earned the right to speak. Before any decision, all citizens could debate. This requirement created a culture of education: even farmers had to learn **rhetoric** (the art of speaking) and gain knowledge. The polis drove mass education and innovation.

---

### The Alphabet as a Democratic Writing System

#### Main Idea

The shift from empire to polis required a new writing system. Mycenaean Greece used **Linear B**, a syllabary deliberately difficult to learn—difficulty was the point, as it kept literacy restricted to elites.

The Greeks needed something more efficient, so they created the **alphabet**—the same system used today.

#### Supporting Material: The Evolution of Writing

The lecturer traces writing through four stages:

1. **Pictograms** — drawings of things (sun, moon)
2. **Symbols** — abstract marks representing ideas (ideograms, like Chinese characters)
3. **Syllabary** — symbols representing sounds (Linear B)
4. **Alphabet** — symbols representing parts of sounds, with vowels added

> "For it to be most efficient, to be most flexible, to be most versatile, we can just have the symbol represent a part of a sound."

**Connection**: The alphabet increased literacy across the Greek world, which enabled the oral culture that produced Homer.

---

### Homer: The Big Bang of Greek Civilization

#### Main Idea

With widespread literacy and a decentralized culture hungry for entertainment and knowledge, communities invited **bards** (poets) to recite legends of the past. Homer emerged as the greatest of these poets. He is considered the father of Greek civilization.

Key facts about Homer:

- He composed two works: the **Iliad** and the **Odyssey**
- He did not write them—he recited them, and his students transcribed them
- He was likely illiterate and possibly blind
- In a centralized system, Homer would have been a propagandist; decentralization made him a truth-teller

> "Homer is, I believe, the big bang of Greek civilization."

### The Trojan War: Legend Behind the Iliad

#### Main Idea

Troy was the center of global trade, which made it a target for pirates. Over time, facts became stories, and stories became legend—producing the Trojan War myth.

#### Supporting Material: The Judgment of Paris

The origin myth: three goddesses—**Hera** (queen), **Athena** (wisdom), and **Aphrodite** (love)—fight over a golden apple inscribed "to the most beautiful." Zeus finds **Paris**, a prince of Troy, to judge.

Each goddess bribes Paris:

- **Hera**: a kingdom
- **Athena**: all the wisdom in the world
- **Aphrodite**: the most beautiful woman in the world (Helen)

Paris chooses Helen.

The lecturer's wife explains the logic: kings are common, wisdom is invisible, but walking around with the most beautiful woman in the world is visible status.

**Consequences**: Hera and Athena want revenge—not just on Paris, but on all of Troy. Helen is already married to **Menelaus** (king of Sparta), whose brother **Agamemnon** (king of Mycenaean Greece) organizes a massive army. The war lasts ten years.

Two key heroes:

- **Achilles** — the bravest warrior
- **Odysseus** — the wisest strategist (inventor of the Trojan Horse)

### The Plot of the Iliad

#### Main Idea

Homer does not tell the whole Trojan War. He tells only a slice: the conflict between **Achilles** and **Agamemnon**.

#### Act 1: The Quarrel

Agamemnon has a captive girl who is the daughter of a powerful priest. The priest demands her back; Agamemnon refuses. The priest prays to Apollo, who sends a plague. Achilles confronts Agamemnon publicly: give her back or everyone dies.

Agamemnon agrees—but only if he takes Achilles' girl instead. Achilles is furious and withdraws from battle entirely, vowing to let the Trojans destroy the Greeks.

#### Act 2: Achilles' Scheme

Achilles asks his mother, the goddess **Thetis**, to persuade Zeus to help the Trojans. His reasoning: let the Greeks lose so badly they beg him to return, making his eventual victory more glorious.

**Hector**, prince of Troy and Paris' brother, exploits Achilles' absence and pushes the Greeks back to their ships.

#### Act 3: The Embassy and Refusal

The Greeks beg Agamemnon to reconcile. Agamemnon offers Achilles his daughter in marriage and limitless treasure. Achilles refuses—he doesn't want gifts, he wants Agamemnon to come in person and apologize.

> "I don't need his daughter. I don't need all this money in the world. I'm gonna die anyway in battle, so screw off."

#### Act 4: Patroclus

Achilles sends **Patroclus**, his lieutenant, to check on the Greeks. The Greeks convince Patroclus to fight in Achilles' place. Achilles gives permission but with a condition: save the ships, but do not push the Trojans back to Troy.

> "You must not burn for war against these Trojans... you will only make my glory that much less."

The lecturer explains this as reverse psychology: Achilles knows that telling Patroclus not to seek glory will make Patroclus seek glory. Patroclus challenges Hector to single combat. Hector kills Patroclus.

#### Supporting Material: The John and Jill Analogy

A modern analogy to illustrate the reverse psychology:

- John and Jill break up but want each other back
- Jill sends her friend Jane to get John to apologize
- John flirts with Jane; Jane reports back excited
- Jill says: "Go date him, but don't kiss him"
- Jill knows Jane will kiss John

**Connection**: Achilles, like Jill, engineers the outcome he claims to forbid. He creates the conditions for Patroclus' death, then uses that death as justification to re-enter the war with maximum glory.

#### Act 5: Achilles' Madness

Achilles kills Hector in single combat. He should be triumphant. Instead, he falls into deep depression. He ties Hector's body to his chariot and drags it around the walls of Troy—a war crime that horrifies Trojans, Greeks, and gods alike.

> "He cannot sleep. He's depressed. He is wrapped with self-guilt. He cannot forgive himself for what has happened."

The lecturer identifies three moments where Achilles could have prevented Patroclus' death:

1. He could have resolved his dispute with Agamemnon
2. When the Greeks came to beg, he could have said yes
3. He did not have to send Patroclus into battle

Achilles knows in his heart that he is guilty.

#### Act 6: Priam and Achilles

The gods arrange a meeting. **Priam**, king of Troy and Hector's father, is smuggled into Achilles' tent by Hermes. Priam has the chance to kill Achilles—instead, he kneels and kisses Achilles' hands.

> "The majestic king of Troy slipped past the rest and, kneeling down beside Achilles, clasped his knees and kissed his hands, those terrible man-killing hands that had slaughtered Priam's many sons in battle."

Priam says: remember your own father.

> "Overpowered by memory, both men gave way to grief. Priam wept freely for man-killing Hector, throbbing, crouching before Achilles' feet, as Achilles wept for himself... now for his father, now for Patroclus once again, and the sobbing rose and fell throughout the house."

This is the first time Achilles can cry. Priam saw Hector in Achilles' face. Achilles saw his father in Priam's face. Through love for others, they found forgiveness for each other.

**Connection**: Priam's submission destroys Achilles' pride. By forgiving Achilles, Priam allows Achilles to forgive himself. Achilles returns Hector's body and guarantees a truce for the funeral.

> "When we forgive each other, we make the world anew. We rejuvenate the world. We change the world from one of destruction into one of vitality."

### The Central Thesis: Forgiveness as the Deepest Human Problem

#### Main Idea

The Iliad's core insight: the real battlefield is not external but internal. The real struggle is within the human heart. Forgiveness—of self and others—is the hardest problem in human society.

#### Supporting Material: The Drunk Driver Thought Experiment

Three scenarios:

1. You're drunk driving. You hit a car. Your wife and child die. **Your fault.**
2. A drunk driver hits you. Your wife and child die. **His fault.**
3. You're arguing with your wife, distracted. A drunk driver hits you. Your wife and child die. **Shared fault.**

The lecturer asks: in which scenario are you least likely to forgive the drunk driver?

The answer is scenario three—because you cannot forgive yourself first, and that blocks you from forgiving others.

> "If you cannot forgive yourself, you can't forgive others. And guess what, guys? In real life, it's always number three."

**Connection**: This maps directly onto Achilles. He knows he shares blame for Patroclus' death. His inability to forgive himself manifests as the mutilation of Hector's body. Priam's act of forgiveness breaks the cycle.

### Julian Jaynes and the Bicameral Mind

#### Main Idea

The lecturer introduces **Julian Jaynes** and his book *The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind*. The theory:

- The **right hemisphere** receives information/vibrations from the universe
- The **left hemisphere** interprets that information into everyday reality
- For most of human history, people interpreted these signals as gods, spirits, and voices

This is connected to **Kant's** distinction between **noumena** (things in themselves) and **phenomena** (things as we perceive them), and **Hegel's** concept of the **Geist** (universal spirit/consciousness).

> "Even though it's not factual, it is truthful in that it allows us to better understand the universe, and it allows us to understand ourselves."

#### Supporting Material: Ancient Burial Practices

Jaynes asks: why did ancient cultures bury kings with gifts, as if they were still alive?

> "The burial of the important dead as if they still lived is common to almost all these ancient cultures... This practice has no clear explanation except that their voices were still being heard by the living."

Examples: Viking ship burials, Neolithic skull cults (ancestor worship).

#### Supporting Material: Jeremy Narby's *The Cosmic Serpent*

The lecturer references Jeremy Narby's argument that the entire universe is consciousness, and that hallucinations can be sources of verifiable information.

> "I went to accept the idea that hallucinations could be a source of verifiable information."

Scientists whose breakthroughs came from non-rational sources:

- **Descartes** dreamed of an angel explaining rationalism
- **Einstein** daydreamed in a tram and conceived relativity
- **James Watson** scribbled on a newspaper and intuited DNA's double helix

#### The Modern Shutdown

The lecturer argues that modern society has shut off the right hemisphere by insisting that only science, logic, and materialism matter. This makes us less creative and less wise than the Greeks.

### Truthful vs. Factual: Homer's Method

#### Main Idea

The lecturer draws a key distinction: **factual** (what literally happened) vs. **truthful** (what gives genuine insight into the human condition). Homer's writing is truthful but not factual. Modern prose is factual but not truthful.

#### Supporting Material: Athena Restraining Achilles

In the Iliad, when Achilles is about to kill Agamemnon, Athena physically appears and stops him. Only Achilles can see her.

> "Rearing behind him... only Achilles saw her, none of the other fighters."

The lecturer rewrites this scene in modern prose: Achilles reaches for his sword, feels his legs go wobbly, turns and walks out. Factually accurate—but it tells us nothing about why.

**Connection**: The gods in Homer externalize internal psychological states. Athena is not literally real, but her appearance gives the audience insight into how rage, restraint, and wisdom operate inside a human being.

#### Supporting Material: Anna Karenina Rewritten as Homer

The lecturer takes **Tolstoy's** *Anna Karenina*—considered one of the greatest modern novels—and rewrites the suicide scene in Homeric style. Instead of stream-of-consciousness internal monologue, Anna hallucinates an older version of herself at the train station who follows her and asks questions.

> "She was older with graying hair and drooping eyes, and Anna looked as though she had seen a ghost."

**Connection**: The Homeric version gives more insight into Anna's psychology than the original modern prose, because externalizing her fear as a vision makes the emotional stakes visible and dramatic.

### Greek Oral Culture as the Source of Wisdom

#### Main Idea

The Greeks' greatness came from an oral culture in which citizens constantly spoke, debated, and performed for each other. This culture produced institutions that reinforced collective inquiry into the human condition:

- **Pericles' funeral oration** — public speaking as civic duty
- **Theater** (amphitheaters seating ~10,000) — plays by Euripides, Aeschylus, Sophocles exploring the human heart
- **The Agora** — marketplace where Socrates debated strangers
- **The Symposium** — gatherings where wealthy people drank watered wine and discussed philosophy, love, death, and meaning
- **Trials** — defendants argued before 500 jurors

> "Today, we just write by ourselves, and that's why our writing is crap."

**Connection**: Writing in Greek culture emerged from community and oral exchange. Homer's works were the shared text that everyone discussed—the source material for an entire civilization's self-understanding.

### The Death of God and Modern Consciousness

#### Main Idea

When society stops believing in the spiritual—when the right hemisphere is shut down—the result is minds cluttered with insignificant concerns.

> "When we kill God, anything can be God."

Anna Karenina's obsessive love for Count Vronsky is reframed as a search for God—for meaning, significance, transcendence—in a world that no longer provides it.

#### Supporting Material: Virginia Woolf's *To the Lighthouse*

The lecturer reads a passage from Woolf as an example of **stream of consciousness**—a mind wandering between grocery bills, suffering, death, and the future with no discipline or focus.

> "There were the eternal problems, suffering, death, the poor... and yet she had said to all these children, you shall go through with it."

**Connection**: Stream of consciousness is presented as what the modern mind actually looks like: fragmented, schizophrenic, disconnected from larger meaning. This is contrasted with Homer's externalized, god-mediated psychology, which gave structure and insight to inner experience.

### Key Takeaways

- The Iliad's central insight: the real battlefield is the human heart, not the shores of Troy
- Forgiveness of self must precede forgiveness of others—this is the deepest problem in human society
- Homer's method of externalizing psychology through gods is "truthful but not factual"—it reveals more about human nature than modern realistic prose
- Greek civilization's greatness came from decentralization, oral culture, and collective inquiry into what it means to be human
- The modern suppression of the right hemisphere (spiritual, creative, receptive) in favor of pure rationalism has made us less wise and less creative
- The alphabet was a democratic technology—it made literacy accessible to all citizens, enabling the culture that produced Homer

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Polis**: Greek city-state; the root of "politics"; a decentralized unit where citizens debated and fought
- **Linear B**: Mycenaean syllabary writing system, deliberately difficult to restrict literacy to elites
- **Bicameral mind**: Julian Jaynes' theory that the right hemisphere receives universal vibrations and the left interprets them into reality
- **Noumena / Phenomena**: Kant's distinction between things as they are and things as we perceive them
- **Geist**: Hegel's concept of universal spirit or consciousness
- **Rhetoric**: The art of public speaking; a required skill for Greek citizens
- **Symposium**: A gathering for philosophical discussion over watered wine
- **Agora**: The Greek marketplace and center of public debate
- **Stream of consciousness**: Modern literary technique reflecting the undisciplined wandering of the godless mind
