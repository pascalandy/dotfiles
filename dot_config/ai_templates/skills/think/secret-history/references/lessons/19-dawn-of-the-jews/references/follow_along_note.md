## Predictive History: The Persian Origins of Jewish Identity

### Overview

This is a university lecture that introduces a framework called "predictive history" and applies it to explain the origins of Jewish identity as a construct of Persian imperial strategy. The lecturer argues that the Jews (distinct from the earlier Israelites) were effectively created by the Persian Empire to control the strategically vital Levant region, and that this pattern of imperial manipulation repeats throughout history, including in the British creation of modern Israel. The lecture draws heavily from the biblical books of Ezra and Nehemiah to support this thesis.

---

### Predictive History: A New Model for Understanding History

#### Main Idea

The lecturer proposes a three-part framework he calls **predictive history** for analyzing historical events while acknowledging that total objectivity is impossible. Rather than relying solely on documents (which are often one-sided or missing), this model offers structural tools for interpretation.

The three components are:

1. **Historical patterns** — Does this event fit into a larger recurring pattern? If yes, it's probably accurate. If not, something is off.
2. **Game theory** — All historical actors are motivated by interests or worldview. Analyze what each actor's best strategy would be.
3. **Religious eschatology** — Every culture has a religious worldview, often encoded in sacred texts (Bible, Quran). These texts reveal how groups perceive history and how they want it to develop, which lets you anticipate their strategies.

> "If you adopt this model, I guarantee you that your understanding of history will improve, also your understanding of how the world works will improve as well."

### Three Geopolitical Patterns

#### Main Idea

The lecturer identifies three recurring patterns in geopolitics that he asks students to remember throughout the course:

1. **Competition within nation-states matters more than competition between them** — The driving force is **elite overproduction**: too many elites competing for too few status positions. This internal competition drives most conflict.

2. **Elites have shifting loyalties** — Like Game of Thrones, elites are loyal only to themselves. They will betray family and allies to gain power.

3. **War as equilibrium mechanism** — Sometimes war is used not to defeat enemies but to cull excess elites and maintain the status quo. War becomes a pressure-release valve for internal instability.

#### Supporting Material: The Warring States Period of China

The lecturer uses China's Warring States period before the Qin Empire as an illustration. Despite being the poorest, most isolated, and least advanced state, Qin ultimately united China.

Why? Because the other states (Chu, Zhao, Wei) weren't truly at war. Their elites were intermarried and used ritualized warfare to maintain equilibrium. Two pieces of evidence:

- **Chinese chess** emerged in this period — warfare as a regulated, rule-bound game
- **Sun Tzu's _The Art of War_** — the lecturer calls it "one of the stupidest books ever written" because it teaches how to cheat within a heavily regulated game, not how to actually win wars

**Connection**: The Qin won because they committed to **total warfare** and ignored the rules everyone else followed. This pattern — an outsider who doesn't respect the established rules conquering a system in equilibrium — recurs throughout history.

#### Supporting Material: How to Actually Win a War

The lecturer contrasts ritualized warfare with what actually wins wars:

1. Total commitment to winning
2. Meritocracy — promote talented generals even if they threaten your family's position
3. Keep your soldiers happy
4. Destroy enemy civilian centers (siege warfare)
5. Destroy supply lines
6. Overstretch your enemy
7. Divide and conquer
8. Strike fear
9. Kill their gods

> "We know because this is what the Romans did. This is what the Prussians did. This is what Aztecs did. This is what the conquistadors did. This is what the Mongols did."

**Connection**: When states don't do these things, it's evidence they aren't truly at war — they're maintaining equilibrium through ritualized conflict.

### Mesopotamian Pattern: Ritualized Warfare and the Rise of Empires

#### Main Idea

Mesopotamia — the cradle of civilization and the wealthiest region for most of early history — followed the same pattern. City-states descended from Uruk established intermarried elites who conducted ritualized warfare with a key rule: **don't attack each other's temples** (where the gods and all the gold were kept).

#### Supporting Material: Lugal-Zagesi and Sargon of Akkad

**Lugal-Zagesi**, a member of the lower nobility from Umma, broke the rules. He ransacked temples, seizing wealth and building an empire. This threatened the other city-states, who invited mercenaries from Akkad.

**Sargon of Akkad** then established the first world empire. His name means "legitimate ruler" — which the lecturer notes probably means he wasn't one. He was likely a mercenary who seized an opportunity, similar to how David took the throne.

**Connection**: This reinforces the pattern — when internal equilibrium can't resolve elite competition, an outsider who ignores the rules unites everyone through force and innovation.

### The Levant: Center of the World

#### Main Idea

The **Levant** (where Jerusalem sits) is described as historically the most strategic location in the world because it provides access to three historically wealthy regions: Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Anatolia. Whoever controls the Levant can threaten Egypt, and whoever controls Egypt controls the world.

This strategic importance means the Levant is perpetually contested. For most of history, it was an Egyptian colony, but after the Bronze Age Collapse, various empires fought over it.

> "Jerusalem is literally the center of the world… both for religious reasons, but also mainly for geopolitical and trade reasons."

### Mesopotamian Empires: A Pattern of Vulnerability

#### Main Idea

Mesopotamia was wealthy but hard to defend because mountain tribes from the borderlands constantly threatened it. This explains why empires in the region changed frequently:

- **Akkadian Empire** → **Babylonian Empire** (Hammurabi) → **Assyrian Empire** (known for brutal reprisals) → **Neo-Babylonian Empire**

The lecturer notes that empire at this time didn't mean total territorial control. It was more like controlling trade choke points, with everyone else going along because it benefited them — a **loose confederation** rather than a nation-state.

### The Persian Achaemenid Empire: Innovation in Imperial Management

#### Main Idea

**Cyrus the Great** founded the Persian Achaemenid Empire, which became the first true world empire. Its success rested on Zoroastrianism and administrative innovation.

**Zoroastrianism** provided a simple, powerful worldview: seek **Asha** (truth/righteousness). Practically, this meant don't lie. For empire-building, this was transformative — honest bureaucrats report real facts, enabling better decisions. Honest, moral administrators allowed rapid expansion.

Cyrus was known for mercy and generosity. When conquering, he would identify the best people — sometimes even the defeated king — and make them advisers. The Babylonian Empire essentially surrendered to him without a fight because of elite overproduction: the upper and lower nobility invited Cyrus to resolve their internal conflicts.

#### How the Persians Ruled

The lecturer contrasts Persian methods with previous approaches:

| Traditional Methods | Persian Innovations |
|---|---|
| Control trade routes and tax them | Effective administration (honest bureaucrats via Zoroastrianism) |
| Fear and conquest (Assyrian model) | Elite competition channeled into meritocratic bureaucracy |
| Taking hostages | Communication networks (roads, first global postal system) |
| Intermarriage | **Divide and rule** — multiple power centers within each region (satraps, bureaucrats, royal representatives) |

> "Divide and rule is probably the most effective way to control an empire."

### Israel as a Construct of Imperial Imagination

#### Main Idea

This is the lecture's central thesis. The lecturer argues that **Jewish identity was constructed by the Persian Empire** as a tool of imperial control over the Levant. The key claim: the Jews and the Israelites are not the same people.

The sequence:

1. After the Bronze Age Collapse, David created a kingdom in the Levant during a power vacuum
2. After Solomon's death, the kingdom split — northern kingdom conquered by Assyria (the "ten lost tribes"), Judah persisted briefly
3. The Babylonians tried hostage-taking and other techniques to control Jerusalem but failed — the Israelites kept rebelling
4. In 587 BCE, the Babylonians destroyed the temple and Jerusalem — **the Israelites were now gone** as an identity
5. In 539 BCE, Cyrus the Great conquered Babylon and issued the **Edict of Cyrus**, ordering the Israelites to return and rebuild

#### The Key Question: Why Would Cyrus Do This?

The lecturer presents multiple explanations:

- **Biblical explanation**: God told Cyrus to do it _(lecturer dismisses this)_
- **Tolerance display**: Showing the empire is religiously tolerant
- **Personal righteousness**: Cyrus saw himself as merciful
- **Quid pro quo**: Israelites in Babylon may have supported his takeover
- **Administrative value**: Israelites were highly literate intellectuals who rose high in Babylonian administration due to their Bible-study tradition
- **Divide and rule** _(lecturer's preferred explanation)_: Setting up a group in the Levant that is only loyal to the empire and exists only because the empire allows it

> "The Jews were invented by the Persians to control the Levant. The Jews is actually a Persian term."

**Connection**: The term "Jews" comes from the Persian province of **Yahu** (Judah), part of the province called "Beyond the River." The returned exiles occupied a small area surrounded by hostile neighbors — Israelites who had stayed and adapted to local circumstances, married foreign women, and practiced different religions. The conflict between returnees and locals was exactly what the Persian Empire wanted.

### Reading Ezra: The Reconstruction of Jewish Identity

#### Main Idea

The lecturer reads passages from the Book of Ezra with students to demonstrate how the Persian-backed reconstruction played out.

**Key passages and their significance:**

**The Edict of Cyrus (Ezra 1)**:
- Cyrus frames his action as divinely commanded
- He finances the rebuilding of the temple
- The Jews consider Cyrus the messiah for this

**The Foundation of the Second Temple (Ezra 3)**:
- Described as the happiest moment in Jewish history
- Old people who remembered the first temple wept; others shouted for joy
- The lecturer connects this to the modern aspiration to build the **Third Temple** — which would require destroying the Al-Aqsa Mosque, the third holiest site in Islam

**The Conflict with Local Israelites (Ezra 4)**:
- Locals say: "We worship your God as you do — let us build with you"
- The returned exiles refuse: "You shall have no part with us… as King Cyrus of Persia had commanded us"
- The lecturer finds this strange — they're all Israelites — and argues the conflict is intentional imperial strategy

> "It seems as though these Jews in Babylon, they're actually imperial agents who are trying to sow discontent within the region."

**The Letter of Artaxerxes to Ezra (Ezra 7)**:
- King Artaxerxes authorizes Ezra to return, provides financing, grants authority

#### Supporting Material: The Balfour Declaration Parallel

The lecturer draws a direct parallel between Artaxerxes' letter to Ezra and the **Balfour Declaration of 1917**, in which the British government (Lord Balfour) wrote to Lord Rothschild expressing support for a Jewish homeland in Palestine.

> "This is almost like the Artaxerxes declaration… they're using the Bible as a script in order to facilitate their geopolitical ambitions."

**Connection**: The pattern repeats — an empire authorizes and finances the creation/recreation of a Jewish state in the Levant as part of its geopolitical strategy of divide and rule.

### Ezra's Speech: Religious Purity and Blood Purity

#### Main Idea

Ezra's speech (Ezra 9) establishes two foundational principles of the new Jewish identity:

1. **The exile was punishment for worshiping false gods** — God exiled them to Babylon so they could "reflect and learn and grow as a people"
2. **Return requires absolute purity** — no intermarriage with foreign peoples, no worship of other gods

> "Do not give your daughters to their sons, neither take their daughters from your sons."

The lecturer emphasizes this is **new**. The earlier Israelites were polytheistic. The Jews now practice strict monotheism heavily influenced by Zoroastrianism. This is a different religion and a different identity.

> "The Jews of Persia are not the same as the Israelites of David."

**Herodotus** noted two distinctive features of the Jews: they don't work on Saturday (Sabbath), and Jewish women refuse to marry foreign men. The concept of **blood purity** was new and functional for the imperial strategy — it kept the Jews separate from their neighbors and therefore permanently in conflict, permanently dependent on the empire.

### Reading Nehemiah: Building the State

#### Main Idea

**Nehemiah** was the governor sent by the Persians to physically reestablish the Jewish nation. He was the king's **cup-bearer** — a position of extreme trust (you could poison the king).

The biblical narrative presents Nehemiah as spontaneously asking the king for permission to rebuild Jerusalem. The lecturer rejects this framing:

> "This is not how empires work. You do not ask the king or the emperor for anything because he will just cut your head off… You have to guess what he wants."

**Connection to modern Israel**: The lecturer draws a parallel — most people believe modern Israel started with Theodor Herzl's _Der Judenstaat_ and the Zionist movement. The lecturer argues it was actually British imperial policy, but the British couldn't be seen as the ones promoting it, so they created conditions where Jews themselves appeared to want it. Same pattern as the Persian period.

### Nehemiah's Reforms: Economic Justice and Purity Laws

#### Main Idea

When Nehemiah arrives, he discovers massive corruption and inequality among the Jews. He forces reforms:

**Economic reforms:**
- Nobles and officials were charging interest to fellow Jews
- Nehemiah orders all interest-taking to stop and debts forgiven
- Fields, vineyards, and houses restored to their owners

The lecturer connects this to a modern practice: **Jews can always get interest-free loans** from Jewish organizations for homes or businesses. Jews are not allowed to charge each other interest. This internal economic solidarity is a key feature of Jewish resilience.

**Purity reforms:**
- No working on the Sabbath
- If you married a foreign woman, you must divorce her
- Nehemiah physically beat violators and pulled out their hair

> "He's bringing up families. Why? Because he believes that religious purity requires blood purity."

### The Creation of the Bible as National Memory

#### Main Idea

The final step in creating Jewish identity was **Ezra's public reading of the Torah**. He read the law before the entire assembly — men, women, and all who could understand — from early morning until midday.

The significance: the Bible becomes the Jews' **historical memory**. Wherever they are, however dispersed, their identity is stored in a single text.

> "This makes them the world's most resilient, flexible, and open people."

### Three Predictions for Modern Israel

#### Main Idea

Based on his biblical analysis, the lecturer makes three predictions:

1. **Rebuilding the Third Temple** — Israel will destroy the Al-Aqsa Mosque and build the Third Temple (Temple of Solomon). The biblical passages about the joy of rebuilding the Second Temple show how central this is to Jewish identity.

2. **Theocracy** — Israel will become increasingly theocratic, with the Bible as law. The lecturer notes that Tel Aviv is currently liberal and open (one of the highest concentrations of gay populations globally), but predicts this will change as religious law becomes dominant. The biblical logic: Jews lost Israel because they disobeyed God, so maintaining it requires strict obedience.

3. **Expansion and Jewish return** — As the **Pax Judaica** expands, more Jews worldwide will return to Israel. The lecturer states that no force in the Middle East can currently prevent Israeli expansion.

### Key Takeaways

- The three tools of predictive history (patterns, game theory, religious eschatology) offer a framework for analyzing historical events and anticipating future developments
- Internal elite competition drives more conflict than inter-state competition; war often serves as an equilibrium mechanism
- Outsiders who ignore established rules tend to be the ones who build empires (Qin, Sargon, Cyrus)
- The Levant's strategic position makes it perpetually contested — whoever controls it can threaten Egypt and dominate trade
- Jewish identity as we know it was forged during the Persian period, not during the Israelite kingdom — it involved strict monotheism, blood purity, economic solidarity, and the Bible as collective memory
- The pattern of empires creating/supporting Israel in the Levant as a divide-and-rule strategy recurs: Persians → British → Americans
- Reading sacred texts through a geopolitical lens can reveal strategic intentions and help predict future behavior

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Predictive history**: The lecturer's framework combining historical patterns, game theory, and religious eschatology to analyze and predict historical developments
- **Elite overproduction**: Too many elites competing for too few positions of status and power, driving internal conflict
- **Divide and rule**: Imperial strategy of creating competing factions within a region so all parties depend on the empire to manage conflict
- **Asha**: Zoroastrian concept of truth/righteousness; practically meant "don't lie," which enabled effective imperial bureaucracy
- **Pax Judaica**: The lecturer's term for a predicted future era of Israeli/Jewish dominance, replacing American imperial hegemony
- **Edict of Cyrus**: The decree by Cyrus the Great allowing exiled Jews to return to Jerusalem and rebuild the temple (539 BCE)
- **Satraps**: Local governors in the Persian provincial system
