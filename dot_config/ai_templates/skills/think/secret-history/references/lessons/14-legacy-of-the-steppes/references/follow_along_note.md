## Civilization versus the Steppes

### Overview

This is a university lecture challenging the traditional understanding of "civilization vs. barbarians." The lecturer argues that the conventional framing—civilized people as free, innovative, and prosperous versus steppe people as violent, static, and poor—is fundamentally wrong. The lecture traces the development of both civilizational and pastoral cultures, showing how geography shaped radically different economies, mythologies, and social structures, and why steppe peoples consistently conquered empires throughout history.

---

### The Conventional View (and Why It's Wrong)

#### Main Idea

The lecture opens by laying out the standard textbook distinction between civilization and the steppes (grasslands). In Chinese, steppe people are called **yamanian** (barbarians). The traditional view holds three contrasts:

- Civilization grants **intellectual freedom** through education and literacy; steppe people are "emotional slaves"—unpredictable and violent
- Civilization is **open and curious**, therefore innovative; steppe people are static and insular
- Civilization leads to **wealth and prosperity** (and happiness); steppe people are poor and unhappy

#### The Problem

This framework cannot explain a glaring pattern in human history: steppe peoples have been the greatest conquerors. Genghis Khan conquered both China and Baghdad—two of the world's greatest civilizations. This pattern repeats consistently.

> "How is it that if in civilization, they're so free, they're so smart, they're so open, they're so curious, innovative, and so prosperous—why is it that they keep on losing to the steppe people?"

The lecturer's thesis: the traditional understanding is "completely wrong." It is actually the steppe people who are open, curious, and innovative, and civilized people who become close-minded, static, and unhappy.

---

### How Civilization Develops

#### Main Idea

The lecture traces how civilizations form, consolidate, and eventually decay—following a predictable lifecycle.

**Stage 1: City Formation.** People gather around major rivers for religious purposes—to celebrate nature, God, and belief systems. These settlements become unequal and hierarchical over time, causing people to leave. But some locations are too strategically valuable for trade, so they persist and grow into major cities. The first city in world civilization is **Uruk**.

**Stage 2: Colonization and City-States.** As cities grow, they colonize other places along trade routes—upstream and downstream. These city-states enter into a system the lecturer calls **open cooperative competition**:

- **Open**: willingness to learn and grow
- **Cooperation**: contact with others, learning best practices
- **Competition**: drive to outperform rivals

This system produces massive innovation. Examples:

- China's **Chunqiu** (Spring and Autumn period)—the era of Confucius and Zhuangzi. All the great ideas came from this period of competing states.
- Same pattern in Mesopotamia and Egypt

**Stage 3: Empire Formation.** One city-state eventually conquers the others. The lecturer notes an ironic pattern: it is often the _most disadvantaged_ city that wins, because disadvantage forces greater innovation. In China, the **Qin** dynasty—isolated in the mountains, lower population, poorer—conquered the Zhao, Chu, and Wei.

Early empires are innovative for three reasons:

1. **Scale** — more resources to draw on
2. **Standardization** — same monetary system, laws, communications
3. **Centralization** — one controlling authority enables massive public works (canals, temples)

**Stage 4: Bureaucratic Decay.** Over time, the empire becomes the _opposite_ of open cooperative competition. It becomes:

- **Insular** (closed off)
- **Secretive**
- **A monopoly** (bureaucracy)

> "Civilization leads ultimately to corruption."

**Connection**: The lifecycle model shows that innovation is a feature of early, competitive city-state systems—not of mature empires. Civilization's supposed advantage (innovation) is temporary.

#### Exception: The Indus Valley Civilization

The lecturer flags that this pattern is not universal. The **Indus Valley civilization** (modern-day Pakistan) had a city-state system but remained peaceful, egalitarian, and artistic. They did not go to war, create hierarchies, or build bureaucracies. This will be discussed later in the course.

### Why Empires Collapse

#### Main Idea

Life at the bottom of an empire is terrible. The lecturer identifies three reasons:

1. **War** — the empire conscripts people to fight and die in order to protect its advantages
2. **Debt** — peasants easily fall into debt, owe rent to landlords, and may have to sell their children
3. **Immobility** — people are stuck where they are

The bureaucracy develops mythology to justify this arrangement.

#### Elite Overproduction

At the top, the critical problem is **elite overproduction**: there are limited elite positions, but elites have too many children. Factions form and compete, producing three typical outcomes:

1. **Revolution** — one elite faction mobilizes the people to overthrow the others. Critically: _the people do not create revolutions on their own_. It is always one faction of the elite working with the people against other factions.
2. **Civil war** — factions with different armies fight directly
3. **External war** — factions direct aggression outward against other empires

#### The Steppe Peoples Enter

As empires weaken, **pastoralists** (steppe people who raise sheep, goats, and cattle rather than farming) take over. Why?

- They are the best fighters in the world—they have horses and archery
- One faction often invites them as **mercenaries**, and the mercenaries eventually realize they can take power themselves
- Sometimes the faction that hired them runs out of money to pay, so the mercenaries seize control

Pastoralists maintain constant contact with empires through three channels: **trade**, **pillaging** (raiding and stealing), and **mercenary work**.

### Why Steppe People Are Different: Geography

#### Main Idea

The fundamental driver of difference is **geography**. People in the steppes grew up in a radically different environment than people of civilization, producing a different economy, mythology, and culture.

#### The Agricultural Foundation

Agriculture originated because people built temples, then needed farms to sustain them. Due to climate change and overpopulation, agriculture spread—from the Middle East to Europe and beyond. Europe could maintain agricultural practices because its geography was conducive. Early agricultural societies had three key characteristics:

- **Women were in control** — the "most natural thing" was for women to be part of the political class. Conflicts were resolved through discussion, not war.
- **No private property** — agriculture was communal; no concept of ownership
- **No war** — people traded and discussed to reach harmony

Europe specifically lacked large rivers (unlike China, Egypt, Mesopotamia), so it could not grow massive cities requiring bureaucracies. This allowed Europeans to maintain a relatively good quality of life longer.

#### The Shift to Pastoralism

When people moved to the steppes, they could not farm—it was all grassland. So they adapted:

- Agricultural people traded with steppe people
- Migrants brought cows and sheep to the steppes
- Cows and sheep can eat grass that humans cannot, so the entire economy shifted to being **animal-based**

This created three interconnected changes that the lecturer calls a fundamental principle of human history:

- **Private property and money** — animals are expensive and countable, creating ownership
- **War and conflict** — if someone has a cow and you don't, you want to steal it
- **Patriarchy** — war and property require male-dominated systems

> "These three things always go together. Patriarchy, money, and war."

### Major Innovations of the Steppe Peoples

#### Lactose Tolerance

Most humans cannot naturally digest milk. Steppe peoples developed the enzymes for **lactose tolerance** because they depended on cattle. The result: a diet of milk and meat made them physically stronger and taller than agricultural peoples.

> "For most of human history, the people of the steppes were the tallest people in the world. They were the strongest in the world."

#### Horse Riding

The steppes are vast and flat. Cows and sheep exhaust pastures and must move. Protecting herds from raiders requires mobility. So steppe peoples learned to **domesticate horses**—a difficult, long process since horses naturally flee from humans.

#### The Wheel and Wagon

With horses came the **wheel and wagon**, enabling people to load belongings and move from camp to camp across the entire steppe.

**Connection**: These three innovations—lactose tolerance, horse riding, and the wheel—created a culture radically different from agricultural societies. Each innovation cascaded into the next, producing a mobile, powerful, aggressive people.

### Steppe Culture and Social Organization

#### Patriarchy and Population Growth

Men controlled steppe society because war was constant. The culture rewarded **aggression** and **courage**. Under patriarchy, women were pushed to have as many children as possible (unlike matriarchal societies where women practiced birth control to protect maternal and child health). More boys meant more fighters.

#### Primogeniture

Many children created an **inheritance problem**. If a man with 100 cows divides equally among 10 sons, each gets only 10—and bad weather could wipe out the family in two or three generations. The solution: **primogeniture**, where the eldest son inherits everything, keeping the family wealthy.

A student asks whether it must always be the eldest son. The lecturer confirms: always the eldest, to avoid conflict. Opening inheritance to merit ("most brave," "most noble") would cause the sons to fight each other—which is exactly what happened after Genghis Khan's death.

#### Secret Societies (Männerbund)

If the eldest inherits everything, what do the other sons do? They form **secret societies**—essentially gangs of young men who steal cows, sheep, and women. This creates a culture of perpetual war and conflict.

> "What's the most valuable commodity in the steppes? Cows and women. So that's what you steal."

The institution of the **Männerbund** (warrior brotherhood) has been reconstructed as central to Proto-Indo-European initiation rituals. The **Vikings** are identified as direct descendants of this tradition.

#### Patron-Client Relationships

In civilization, bureaucracy maintains control through debt (slavery). In the steppes, there is no slavery. Instead, society is organized through **patron-client relationships**—like a mafia structure. The "big brother" lends cows to the "little brother," who now owes loyalty. This creates **tribes**.

Key difference from empire: steppe people remain free and independent. They fight _for themselves_, not because they're forced to. This is why they are superior fighters.

> "In civilization, bureaucracy maintains control over people over debt. And so you become a slave. But in the steppes, there's no concept of slavery."

The system is held together by belief in the gods, who oversee oath-bound contracts between patrons and clients. It is also **open-ended**—easy to bring outsiders into the system without shaming them or assigning them permanent submissive roles. This openness and flexibility is why steppe societies were innovative and cohesive.

### Mythology: Mother Goddess vs. Sky God

#### Main Idea

Agricultural societies worshipped the **Mother Goddess**—a religion emphasizing harmony, balance, kindness, and compassion. In this mythology, grain and nature are sacred.

Steppe societies worshipped the **Sky God**—a religion emphasizing conquest, exploitation, destruction, and killing. Horses and cows are central to this mythology.

#### The Proto-Indo-European Creation Myth

The foundational myth involves twin brothers: **Man** and **Twin**. Accompanied by a great cow, they travel the world. To create the world, Man must sacrifice Twin—kill the person he loves most. The gods bless him for it.

Then: the sky gods gave cattle to "third man." A three-headed, six-eyed serpent stole the cattle. Third man and the storm god killed the serpent and freed the cattle.

**Connection**: This mythology of sacrifice and violent struggle echoes throughout Indo-European civilizations:
- **Genghis Khan** kills his best friend to become leader of the Mongols
- **Romulus** kills his twin brother Remus to found Rome

> "That shows you that the Mongols and the Romans—they come from the same culture. A culture based on violence and exploitation."

### Old Europe Before the Yamnaya

#### Main Idea

The lecturer introduces **Marija Gimbutas**, a Lithuanian-American anthropologist who first hypothesized that "Old Europe" was conquered by steppe peoples. Her archaeological work revealed that Old European culture was peaceful, honored women, and espoused egalitarianism.

#### Gimbutas on the Mother Goddess Civilization

> "The goddess in all her manifestations was a symbol of the unity of all life in nature. Her power was in water and stone, in tomb and cave, in animals, in birds, snakes and fish, hills, trees, and flowers."

> "This culture took deep delight in the natural wonders of this world. Its people did not produce lethal weapons or build forts in inaccessible places as their successors did... Instead, they built magnificent tombs, shrines, and temples, comfortable houses in moderately sized villages, and created superb pottery and sculptures. This was a long lasting period of remarkable creativity and stability, an age free of strife."

Critically, these people _had_ writing systems and technology—they chose not to use them for war because it was against their religion.

#### Cultural Inversions by the Yamnaya

When the Yamnaya arrived, they inverted Old European symbolism:

- **Snake**: Old Europeans saw it as a symbol of life energy and regeneration, "the most benevolent, non-evil creature." The Yamnaya introduced the idea that the snake is a devil—the origin of the biblical concept.
- **Black**: Originally the color of fertility, damp caves, rich soil, the womb of the goddess. The Yamnaya made it the color of evil.
- **White**: Originally the color of death and bones. The Yamnaya made it the color of purity.

### Women, Sex, and Power in Pre-Patriarchal Societies

#### Main Idea

The lecturer references **_Sex at Dawn_** by Christopher Ryan to argue that for most of human history, women had **sexual agency** and multiple partners.

Evidence cited: human males have the largest penises of all primates (despite being smaller than gorillas), which the book attributes to sperm competition from women having multiple sexual partners.

In some societies, women had many husbands (usually brothers)—a practice called **polyandry**, which maintained peace and harmony.

#### The Malaise Example

Among the Malaise people, semen was considered essential to human growth. When a bride married, all men would participate to "give her as much life as possible."

A French missionary objected that a man could never know if a child was truly his. The native response:

> "You French people love only your own children, but we all love all the children of our tribe."

**Connection**: In patriarchal systems, love is exclusive—only your biological children. In pre-patriarchal systems, love was communal—all children were everyone's children. The lecturer argues this system was better for children and would prevent depression.

#### Why Women Were Better Leaders

The lecturer states plainly: women are better politicians because they are more willing to cooperate, possess greater emotional intelligence, and can use sex and gossip as mechanisms of social control.

### Why Agricultural Societies Collapsed

#### Main Idea

Three vulnerabilities destroyed the farming communities of Old Europe:

1. **Static** — they did not innovate
2. **Climate change** — weather shifts devastated agriculture
3. **Disease** — living close together and near animals made them vulnerable to plague

#### The Plague (c. 3000 BCE)

Carbon dioxide measurements show a massive population collapse around 3000 BCE across Europe. The cause: plague (described as a form of Black Death carried by rats). Trade networks spread it everywhere, even as far as China. Steppe peoples were less affected because they lived far apart from each other.

#### Climate Change

Periodic massive climate shifts destroyed agriculture and forced migration, further weakening farming communities.

**Connection**: The combination of being static, plague-ravaged, and climate-disrupted left European farming populations devastated—creating the opening for Yamnaya invasion.

### The Yamnaya Conquest

#### Main Idea

The **Yamnaya** (also called Proto-Indo-Europeans or steppe peoples) migrated globally. They went to Europe, but also to Iran and India. We know this because Persian, Hindi, and European languages share a common mother language.

#### How They Came

- When agricultural peoples migrated to Europe, they came as **families** (men, women, children) and integrated peacefully
- When the Yamnaya came, they came primarily as **young men** who killed local men and took their wives

> "From DNA research, we know that it was a genocide."

The Yamnaya were physically dominant: stronger, taller, and more aggressive due to their meat-and-dairy diet.

#### Regional Variations

The result of Yamnaya conquest varied by location:

- **Britain**: Total genocide. The people who built Stonehenge—farming, Mother Goddess worshippers—were completely eliminated. Men and women killed together (possibly because they chose to fight together).
- **Spain**: All men were replaced (Y-chromosome evidence shows indigenous male lineages disappear after ~2000 BCE), but not full genocide
- **India**: More peaceful settlement. Yamnaya conquerors positioned themselves at the top of society, locals at the bottom—creating the **caste system**. Upper castes spoke Indo-European languages; lower castes spoke **Dravidian** (local language).

---

### Linguistic Evidence for Common Origins

#### Main Idea

Linguists identified common words across Indo-European languages, proving a shared ancestral language:

| English | Latin | Greek | Persian | Hindi |
|---------|-------|-------|---------|-------|
| Father | Patar | Patris | Padar | Pitta |
| Mother | Mitar | Mithra | Madar | Madar |

The Proto-Indo-European word for "two" is **dua**, traceable through the entire language family down to English "two."

#### Proto-Indo-European Vocabulary

Linguists identified words unique to Proto-Indo-European culture: bo, cow, ox, ram, eel, dog, grain, cauldron, wrestling, wealth, households, families, clans. Four characteristics emerge:

- Many words for **wheel**
- Words for **dairy**
- No farming terms
- Words for **horse**

**Connection**: This vocabulary profile confirms they were nomadic pastoralists from the steppes. Archaeological and DNA studies then filled in the rest.

### New Religions Born from Yamnaya Mixing

By blending with local customs and religions, the Yamnaya created:

- **Zoroastrianism** in Iran (scripture: the Avesta)
- **Hinduism** in India (scripture: the Vedas)

### David Anthony's _The Horse, the Wheel, and Language_

#### Main Idea

The lecturer references this book by Harvard anthropologist **David Anthony** to explain Yamnaya culture and dominance.

#### The Pastoral Economy

> "Domesticated cattle and sheep started a revolutionary change in how humans explored the Pontic-Caspian Steppe environment."

Cattle and sheep converted useless grassland into wool, clothing, tents, milk, yogurt, cheese, meat, marrow, and bone—"the foundation of both life and wealth." But herding was a **volatile boom-bust economy**: herds can grow rapidly with luck, but collapse from bad weather or theft. This required flexible, opportunistic social organization.

**Connection**: This volatility made steppe peoples fundamentally different from farmers, who could "sit back and relax because nature will grow the food for them." Steppe peoples were aggressive and opportunistic by necessity.

#### Oath-Bound Obligations

Proto-Indo-European vocabulary includes terms for verbal contracts bound by oaths. Those who loan animals gain power over borrowers. Those who sponsor feasts obligate their guests. But critically, this is _not_ slavery—it is mutual obligation.

> "Even though there are wealthy people and poor people, they are still treated with respect."

### The Steppe as a Fighter-Training Ground

#### Main Idea

Because the steppe system maintains open cooperative competition (never collapsing into bureaucracy), and because it forces aggression, independence, and hard work, steppe peoples are history's greatest warriors.

The ironic pattern: the _strongest_ fighters stay in the steppes. Those _forced out_ go conquer empires.

#### The Cascade Pattern

When China's Han dynasty moved armies into the steppes to eliminate the threat, it triggered a cascade:

- China pushes the **Xiongnu** (Huns) westward
- The Huns push other groups further west
- Migration reaches the Roman Empire

This pattern repeated with successive steppe powers: **Huns** (Attila) → **Turks** → **Mongols** (Genghis Khan, whose empire covered most of the known world) → **Timur** (Tamerlane, the last great steppe conqueror).

The Mongol unification of most of the world also facilitated the **Black Death**, which wiped out roughly a third of Europe.

#### The End of Steppe Dominance

The invention of **gunpowder** was the turning point. For the first time, civilization could defeat steppe warriors. From that point forward, civilizations attacked steppe peoples and reduced their culture.

### On Religion (Student Q&A)

A student asks whether steppe peoples could develop religions without temples. The lecturer responds:

> "Religions don't require temples. Religion is just collective belief."

Religion is defined as a worldview answering three questions: Where do we come from? Why are we here? Where are we going? Everyone has a religion—even modern atheists worship money, materialism, and science. Steppe peoples worshipped the horse, the sky god, the cow, war, courage, and bravery.

> "It's impossible for you to understand the world and operate in the world without a religion."

### Key Takeaways

- The civilization-vs-barbarian framework is misleading; steppe peoples consistently out-innovated and out-fought empires
- Civilizations follow a lifecycle: city-states → open cooperative competition → empire → bureaucracy → corruption → collapse
- Geography determines economy, which determines culture, mythology, and social structure
- Three things always go together: **patriarchy, money, and war**
- The steppe system maintained open cooperative competition indefinitely (no bureaucratic decay), producing perpetually strong warriors
- Steppe peoples conquered empires not despite being "barbarians" but _because_ their system preserved freedom, motivation, and innovation
- Gunpowder ended steppe dominance—the only technological shift that could overcome their military superiority

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Yamnaya (Proto-Indo-Europeans)**: Steppe pastoral peoples who migrated to Europe, Iran, and India, transforming all three regions
- **Open cooperative competition**: System of city-states that are open to learning, cooperate through contact, and compete for dominance—the engine of innovation
- **Primogeniture**: Inheritance system where the eldest son receives everything, preserving family wealth
- **Männerbund**: Warrior brotherhood of young men bound by oath, central to Proto-Indo-European initiation
- **Patron-client relationship (patronage)**: Mafia-like loyalty system where those who lend resources gain loyalty from borrowers, forming the basis of tribal organization
- **Elite overproduction**: When too many elite children compete for limited positions of power, causing factional conflict
- **Pastoralists**: People who raise animals (sheep, goats, cattle) rather than farming
- **Mother Goddess**: Agricultural religion emphasizing harmony, balance, fertility, and the sacredness of all life
- **Sky God**: Steppe religion emphasizing conquest, dominance, violence, and the centrality of cattle and horses
