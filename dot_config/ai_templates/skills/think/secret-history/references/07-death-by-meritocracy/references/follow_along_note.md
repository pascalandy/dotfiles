## Death by Meritocracy

### Overview

A university lecture examining how the American meritocracy—the idea that people succeed based on talent, ability, and hard work—was constructed, how it actually operates, and why the lecturer argues it is destroying American society. The lecture traces the historical origins of the Ivy League, explains how elite admissions really work, and connects the system to rising inequality, political dysfunction, and psychological trauma.

---

### The Meritocracy Defined

#### Main Idea

**Meritocracy** means people should succeed based on their talent, ability, and hard work. The school system is built around this principle: good students go to the best universities, then get the best jobs. The lecturer's central thesis is that this idea has serious problems and is, in fact, destroying American society.

---

### Why America Has the World's Most Complicated Admissions System

#### Main Idea

Most countries use a single-exam system. China has the **Gaokao**—one test score determines university placement. America is unique in requiring transcripts, standardized test scores (SAT, TOEFL), extracurriculars, teacher recommendation letters, and personal essays where applicants must demonstrate they are "a good person."

The lecturer poses the question: why does it matter if you're a good person? The answer unfolds through the historical narrative that follows.

---

### Historical Origins of the Ivy League

#### Phase 1: Religious Foundations (1600s)

In 1600s England, a conflict existed between the King (head of the Anglican Church) and **Protestants** (also called Puritans or dissenters) who believed individuals should access God directly by reading the Bible—no pope, no king as intermediary. This conflict led to wars. The king's solution: send the dissenters to America.

The dissenters went as pilgrims to build a **theocracy**—what they believed would be the new Jerusalem, paradise on earth. A core Protestant belief: you must read the Bible to understand the mind of God. Therefore, education and literacy are a **divine imperative**.

This is why they established **Harvard**—a school designed to train ministers to study and spread the Bible. Harvard then encouraged the development of **Yale** and **Princeton**. Collectively, they became known as the **Ivy League**.

**Connection**: The Ivy League's origins are religious, not academic. They existed to serve a theocratic mission, which sets up the irony of what they became.

#### Phase 2: Social Clubs

As America grew wealthier, it became less religious. The Ivy League transformed into **social clubs**—places where rich Americans went to become friends and build **cohesion**. Students didn't study. They drank, dressed up, had wild parties, played football, took risks, and became lifelong friends who went on to lead America.

The lecturer references the concept of cohesion from a previous class: committing transgressions together makes groups more cohesive.

#### Phase 3: State Schools and Research Universities

As America industrialized, it needed to educate its population:

- **State schools (A&M schools)**: Built to train farmers, engineers, and soldiers. "Texas A&M" stands for Texas Agricultural & Mechanical. These were extremely successful—America industrialized quickly, and most people attended these schools.

- **Research universities**: America recognized it needed science and technology, so it copied the best universities in Germany (then the world center of science and technology). Early examples: **University of Chicago** and **Johns Hopkins**.

Around 1900, the system looked like this:

- Poor people → state schools → learn a trade → good job
- Aspiring professors/scientists → research universities
- Rich people → Ivy League social clubs

> The lecturer comments: "Quite honestly, it was a really good system. And America should have stayed with this system."

#### Phase 4: The Ivy League Fights Back

The Ivy League was becoming irrelevant. Smart people went to Chicago and Johns Hopkins. Over time, those institutions would overtake the Ivy League because "just because you're rich doesn't mean you're smart."

Harvard's response: **scholarship programs** to recruit the best and brightest students from across America. This created the **SAT**—originally a scholarship tool to identify top students nationwide and bring them to Harvard.

**Connection**: The SAT was not created as a universal standard. It was created as a competitive weapon for Harvard to reclaim intellectual relevance.

#### Phase 5: The Backlash from Legacy Families

Once the SAT brought smart students to Harvard, the **legacy students** (rich kids, alumni children) were unhappy. Their kids now had to take a test to get in and were in class with smarter people.

Harvard's problem: it wanted the best students but also needed to maintain good relations with wealthy alumni.

#### Phase 6: The "Holistic" System

Harvard's solution was a new admissions system where grades and test scores were no longer the only factors. The new keyword: **holistic**—specifically, **character**.

> "Just being smart is not good enough. You have to be a good person. You have to be a strong and brave and virtuous person."

The lecturer states bluntly that the concept of "character" was created to keep Jewish applicants out. Jews were academically strong but stereotyped as bookish and not athletic—not "manly" enough. The holistic system (essays, recommendations, extracurriculars) was designed to identify and exclude them.

Today, the lecturer argues, the same system is used to identify and limit Asian applicants. All the additional information collected—profiles, household details—serves to identify ethnicity.

A student asks whether admissions officers would even ask about household details. The lecturer confirms: all the information collected today serves to identify ethnicity and manage the demographic composition of the class.

### Harvard's Real Goal: Power, Not Academics

#### Main Idea

The purpose of all these admissions changes is to ensure Harvard remains the **institution of power** in America. Harvard is not interested in academics—it's interested in power. Therefore, it must ensure the people who attend are the ones most likely to succeed (in worldly terms, not academic terms).

Two key principles govern the system:

- **Secrecy**: Harvard never reveals why it admitted or rejected anyone. It doesn't have to.
- **Discretion**: Harvard can choose to admit or reject for any reason. Unlike the Gaokao system, where a high score guarantees admission, the best student in the world can be rejected if Harvard doesn't want them.

> "From Harvard's perspective, 'best' just means the most likely to succeed. Not the smartest student."

#### Supporting Material: The Four-Student Thought Experiment

The lecturer presents a scenario: you are a Harvard admissions officer. You can admit only one of four students:

1. The best math genius from China
2. The best basketball player in America
3. The best student in the world
4. Someone whose father, grandfather, and great-grandfather all went to Harvard (three generations of legacy)

The class immediately identifies #4 as the correct choice. The legacy student is the most likely to succeed in the world—not because of talent but because of inherited advantage. Harvard isn't interested in educating smart people; it wants to graduate rich people.

If #4 doesn't exist, the next choice is #2 (the athlete). Definitely not #1 (the math genius from China), because that person will likely become a math professor—Harvard doesn't want professors, lawyers, or doctors. It wants future company heads, famous figures, presidents.

The lecturer adds a cynical note: Harvard still wants students #1 and #3 to **apply** because rejecting them improves Harvard's selectivity metrics.

#### A Student Question: Do All Admissions Officers Think This Way?

The lecturer clarifies: this mentality applies to **elite schools** where demand far exceeds supply. Most schools are simply trying to recruit students and collect tuition.

### Harvard as a Venture Capital Firm

#### Main Idea

The best analogy for Harvard is a **venture capital firm**. The lecturer illustrates with two investment options:

- **Option 1**: A restaurant backed by government connections. No risk. Guaranteed $500,000/year returns. Solid plan.
- **Option 2**: An AI/Bitcoin website. The founder can't code, has no experience, and the idea is vague. But if it works: $1 billion.

A venture capitalist always takes Option 2. Harvard operates the same way.

> "They want crazy people who will change the world because that makes Harvard look good."

Harvard would rather have a class where 10 people succeed spectacularly and 990 fail than a class where 1,000 people succeed modestly. Why? Because we only remember the 10 who succeed. Everyone else is forgotten.

**Connection**: This VC mentality explains why Harvard optimizes for high-variance outcomes rather than reliable ones. Brand recognition comes from outliers, not averages.

### Dissociative Personality Disorder and Admissions

#### Main Idea

The lecturer connects to a previous class on **dissociative personality disorder**, arguing that people most likely to succeed in a competitive game have this characteristic. Harvard's admissions process is designed to identify it.

#### Supporting Material: The Lecturer's Yale Application

The lecturer uses his own Yale application as a case study, examining three layers: what the application said, what background information admissions could derive, and how they judged his potential.

**The application (surface level):**

- Public high school in Canada (not private, not prestigious)
- Top 10 out of 200 students (good, not exceptional—he was #10)
- SAT: 1400/1600 (decent, not outstanding—people target 1500+)
- Extracurriculars: soccer team (joined because they needed players), editor of school newspaper, captain of a quiz team called "Reach for the Top"
- Essay: about physicist Richard Feynman; described as boring, "ChatGPT could have written it"
- Teacher recommendations: described him as "ambitious"—a dirty word in Canada meaning too aggressive, too pushy, doesn't play by the rules

**The background (derived information):**

- **Poor**: couldn't afford the application fee, needed a fee waiver
- **Immigrant**: born in China in 1976, family moved to Canada in 1983 at age six
- **Transferred high schools**: moved from a poor high school to a rich high school in a different neighborhood, commuting 30 minutes by subway
- **Punished for ambition**: his principal threatened to write a discipline letter (a serious mark on his record) for transferring schools
- **Socially isolated**: had no friends at the new school because outsiders weren't welcome; was seen as a grade-grubber

**The judgment (what Yale saw):**

Yale identified signs of dissociative personality disorder:

1. **Desperation**: If he didn't get into Yale, he felt he would "drown." It was life or death. Yale wants that desperation.

2. **Insecurity**: An insecure person has a void that can only be filled through achievement. One million isn't enough—they need two million, then ten million. An insecure person sees the world as a competition and never stops achieving.

3. **Transgressive/immoral**: Ready to break rules to succeed. Leaving his poor high school for a rich one broke Canadian social norms. The principal said no; he said "screw off." Other students said he wasn't welcome; he said he didn't care.

> "They saw the desperation, they saw the insecurity, they saw the hunger and the immorality. And this is all part of dissociative personality disorder. And that's why they let me in."

**Connection**: Yale operates as a VC firm. The lecturer was a risky investment—he might go crazy, become depressed, but he might also change the world. Yale bets on high-variance outcomes.

#### A Student Question: Why Are Harvard/Yale Graduates So Famous If the System Is Unfair?

The lecturer explains portfolio diversification. The majority of students are **safe investments**—legacies and athletes who will succeed regardless because their families are rich. Then there's roughly one percent of positions for high-risk bets—people like the lecturer, admitted on intuition.

> "It's all intuition. They don't have a formula for this. They just think, this guy is interesting. Let's just let him in."

### How the Meritocracy Creates Trauma

#### Main Idea

The meritocracy doesn't just find people with trauma—it **produces** trauma. The lecturer examines three levels: university, high school, and parenting.

#### Level 1: Yale as the Hunger Games

Getting into Yale is not the end—it's the beginning of relentless competition. Students compete against the most competitive people in the world in a zero-sum environment:

- **In the classroom**: seminar-style classes with 20 students competing to impress the professor
- **Outside the classroom**: competing for clubs, secret societies, graduate school spots, law school, medical school, scholarships (like the Rhodes Scholarship)

> "From the first day, it's an endless pursuit of achievement. It's just competition after competition after competition. Wherever you go, you're being judged by someone."

This creates deep, lasting insecurity—even among students from wealthy families. Everyone becomes convinced that life is a competition, that everyone is an enemy, and that they must achieve constantly or be destroyed.

**Connection**: This is exactly what Harvard and Yale want. These insecure, driven people will never be satisfied with one million—they'll pursue a billion, then ten billion. That's what produces the outlier successes Harvard trades on.

#### Level 2: High School as Training Ground

High school becomes a Hunger Games preparation system. Everything is organized around getting into elite universities—an extension of the same competitive pressure downward.

#### Level 3: Parenting

The lecturer contrasts two parenting models:

- **Healthy system**: "I love you unconditionally. No matter what you do, I will support you." Result: the child is happy, fulfilled, and secure—but will probably end up as a teacher.

- **Meritocratic system**: Neglect combined with conditional approval. "I don't have time for you, but if you win the swimming competition or place first on your math test, I'll take you for ice cream." This is trauma. Most kids are damaged by it. But some thrive—the trauma drives them to achieve relentlessly, get into Yale, compete in the Hunger Games, and keep competing for life.

#### A Student Question: Would You Go to Yale Again?

The lecturer says probably yes—because the system doesn't give poor people a real choice. Before the meritocracy, you didn't need elite credentials. George Washington never went to college. Abraham Lincoln didn't go to the Ivy League—and they're considered the two greatest presidents. Today, all top leaders went to elite schools. The system is set up so that if you want to succeed and you're poor, the Ivy League feels like the only path.

However: the lecturer says he would **not** send his own children to the Ivy League.

### The Data: How the Meritocracy Destroyed America

The lecturer shifts to a slide presentation with data.

#### Global University Dominance

In 1875, the best universities were in Germany (most Nobel Prize winners), followed by France and the UK. America was nonexistent. Through investments in research universities and importing German scientists after World War II, America now dominates.

#### More Education, More Inequality

- In 1940, about 5% of males went to university. Today, roughly 35%.
- Despite this massive increase in education, America has become **more unequal**, not less.
- The **Gini coefficient** (measuring inequality) has risen sharply in the past 20 years. America is now one of the most unequal societies in the world.

#### Declining Social Mobility

A graph shows the percentage of people doing better than their parents:

- In 1940: most people were doing better than their parents
- Today: most people are **not** doing better than their parents

> "Your generation, you will not do better than your parents, most of you."

#### Income Inequality

The top 1% controls most of the wealth. Even as America has become wealthier overall, the gains go to the top 1%.

#### Student Debt Crisis

- University tuition has skyrocketed while wages have stagnated
- Student loan debt has exploded
- Student debt is the only type of debt in America that cannot be discharged through bankruptcy
- When you die, it passes to your children

#### Rising Depression

Depression among teenagers has increased sharply. Middle-class and wealthy students are more likely to identify as depressed than poor students.

### The Architects of the Meritocracy

#### Main Idea

Two individuals created the modern meritocracy:

- **James B. Conant**: President of Harvard for decades. Brought in the SAT to identify scholarship students. Made Harvard into the best research university in America and determined to make it the most powerful institution in the country—deciding who wins and who loses.

- **Henry Chauncey**: Dean of Harvard, in charge of the scholarship program. Later led a new organization called **ETS** (Educational Testing Service), responsible for the SAT, TOEFL, AP, and GRE.

Both were from Harvard. The entire system was designed to benefit Harvard.

**Connection**: The meritocracy isn't a natural development—it was engineered by two Harvard men to serve Harvard's interests.

#### The Results

- In 1940, 90% of Harvard applicants were accepted. Today, it's about 5%. Stanford is even lower.
- Harvard's **endowment** is approximately $40 billion—more money than most countries.
- Harvard produces more billionaires than any other institution: 127 billionaires as of 2024. Stanford is second.
- 7% of all Americans with over $100 million in net worth graduated from Harvard.
- The next schools on the list (MIT, Stanford, Penn, Columbia, Yale, Cornell, Princeton) are all Ivy League plus MIT.

#### Elite Domination

A study published in **Nature** examined America's elite and found they almost all attended a small number of elite universities. Harvard graduates (shown in red) dominate across every category:

- Generals and admirals
- Philosophers and professors
- Pulitzer Prize winners
- Billionaires
- New York Times bestselling authors
- Fortune 500 CEOs
- Federal judges
- Senators

The contrast is stark: Harvard's share of the general population is tiny, but its share of the elite is enormous.

#### The Elite Don't Know They're Elite

A survey asked people in elite organizations to estimate how many of their colleagues went to Harvard. Their estimates (blue) were far below the actual numbers (red). Harvard graduates genuinely don't realize they dominate America.

> "You ask them and they say, 'No, it's a conspiracy theory. We're just average people.'"

#### Main Idea

Getting into Harvard or Yale isn't enough—you must join the elite social clubs:

- **Harvard**: The Porcelain Club
- **Princeton**: The Ivy Club
- **Yale**: **Skull and Bones** (the most famous college secret society)

#### Supporting Material: The 2004 Election

In 2004, both presidential candidates—George W. Bush (Republican) and John Kerry (Democrat)—were Yale Skull and Bones members. The lecturer presents this as evidence that the game is rigged.

### The Political Consequences

#### Barack Obama

Columbia undergrad, Harvard Law School. In 2008, he promised hope and change—told Americans the game was rigged against them and that he would be their champion. He won because the US economy had collapsed, caused by an Ivy League–controlled banking and political system that became corrupt.

**Who did Obama appoint to fix the crisis?**

- **Larry Summers**: Harvard graduate, former Harvard president, former Treasury Secretary under Bill Clinton—"the guy who created the mess in the first place"
- **Tim Geithner**: Dartmouth graduate, Treasury Secretary—"friends of Wall Street who came in to save Wall Street"

The concept of **moral hazard**: if there are no consequences for mistakes, people will repeat them. Critics argued the banks should face consequences. Larry Summers said: no, we must save the banks to save the economy.

When critics asked about helping American homeowners who lost their homes, Summers invoked moral hazard in the opposite direction—helping ordinary Americans would mean they "won't learn anything."

> The banks got saved. The people got screwed over.

**Connection**: This double standard—moral hazard applies to ordinary people but not to Wall Street—is presented as a direct product of the Ivy League elite protecting its own.

#### Donald Trump

The lecturer argues Trump's rise is a direct consequence of Obama's failures. Millions who voted for Obama's hope and change, then felt betrayed, voted for Trump because he promised to destroy the system.

#### Supporting Material: The 2011 White House Correspondents' Dinner

Obama publicly mocked Trump, showing a slide of what the White House would look like if Trump were president—a casino. Trump was in the audience. Everyone laughed at him. The lecturer argues this humiliation motivated Trump to run for president.

> "Thank Obama for Donald Trump. Without Obama, there would be no Donald Trump."

#### J.D. Vance

Vice President under Trump. Like Obama, he wrote a bestselling book (**Hillbilly Elegy**) that launched his political career. He initially opposed Trump, calling him a potential fascist and dictator, then reversed completely—declaring Trump "the greatest guy in the world."

The lecturer describes both Obama and Vance as "soulless robots"—puppets who tell people what they want to hear. They have no ideas of their own. All they know is how to achieve.

#### Johnny Kim

A likely future political figure. Navy SEAL (top military achievement), Harvard Medical School (doctor), NASA astronaut. The "ideal Ivy League graduate."

Why was he able to do all this? His Wikipedia entry reveals: in 2002, police entered his home and killed his father in front of him. His father was a domestic abuser. Kim was traumatized for life.

**Connection**: Kim has dissociative personality disorder. He turned trauma into relentless achievement. But the lecturer argues he, like Obama and Vance, has no ideas of his own—he's "just a puppet."

### The Alternative: Real Learning

#### Main Idea

Before the meritocracy, success didn't require college. It required certain values and attitudes:

1. **Open-mindedness**: recognize you have a lot to learn; ask questions; learn from others
2. **Embrace failure**: failure is the best teacher because it forces reflection and builds resilience
3. **Reflection**: time and space to think for yourself
4. **Resilience**: built through surviving failure and reflecting on it

The natural cycle: open mind → make mistakes → learn from them → reflect → build resilience → grow → achieve true potential.

#### How the Meritocracy Destroyed This Cycle

- **Failure is now catastrophic**: a failed class teaches you a lot, but it destroys your GPA. You can't afford failure.
- **No time for reflection**: the school day is long, homework is heavy, activities fill every minute. Students have no time to think for themselves. The lecturer says this is intentional.
- **Without failure and reflection, there's no resilience, and no real learning.**

#### What the Ivy League Does to Normal People

For someone from a non-wealthy background, the Ivy League produces:

1. **Arrogance**: "I went to Yale, therefore I know more than anyone. I don't have to ask questions or learn anymore."
2. **Utilitarianism**: only caring about things that help you succeed, no interest in real learning
3. **Narrow-mindedness**: only focused on success, not creative exploration

> "If you're rich, go to Harvard and Yale and Princeton because you'll become more rich. But if you're a poor person, going to these schools may be a mistake."

#### Supporting Material: The Lecturer's Post-Yale Struggles

After graduating from Yale, the lecturer "stumbled through life"—failure after failure because he wanted instant success and refused to invest time in learning a real skill or developing a real passion. In his late twenties and early thirties, he became severely depressed, considered suicide, and spent every day playing video games in his parents' basement.

> "I was traumatized by Yale. And it took me a long time to get over the trauma and recognize this is a false system."

He had to relearn: open-mindedness, embracing failure, resilience, and learning. That's why he became a teacher.

### Two Modes of Being

#### Main Idea

A student asks whether it's possible to be both open-minded and successful with good grades. The lecturer introduces a psychological framework:

- **Altruistic mode**: connected to family, relationships, creativity, passion, love
- **Utilitarian mode**: fight-or-flight, focused on rewards (like grades)

These two modes are **mutually exclusive**—you can't operate in both simultaneously. Creativity comes from the altruistic mindset. Achievement-chasing comes from the utilitarian mindset.

Harvard demands both simultaneously: applicants must demonstrate passionate commitment to a cause AND desire to be a billionaire AND loyalty to Harvard. The only people who can perform this contradiction are those with dissociative personality disorder—they can act as multiple people.

> "Harvard wants the best actors in the world. They want people pretending they're something they're not."

The lecturer returns to Obama as the archetype: promised hope and change in 2008, abandoned it immediately in office, revived it for reelection in 2012, then went water skiing.

> "The man is actually soulless. Stands for nothing."

### Key Takeaways

- The meritocracy was engineered by Harvard to serve Harvard's power, not to create a fair society
- Elite admissions optimize for high-variance outcomes (venture capital logic), not academic excellence
- The system identifies and selects for trauma, desperation, and insecurity—then produces more of it
- Real learning requires open-mindedness, failure, reflection, and resilience—all of which the meritocracy suppresses
- The concentration of Ivy League graduates across all sectors of American power is empirically documented, yet invisible to the elite themselves
- For non-wealthy individuals, the Ivy League can be psychologically destructive rather than liberating

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Meritocracy**: the system where people succeed based on talent, ability, and hard work—presented here as a destructive ideology
- **Holistic admissions**: the system of evaluating character, extracurriculars, and essays alongside grades—originally created to exclude Jewish applicants
- **Secrecy and Discretion**: the twin principles allowing elite schools to admit or reject anyone without explanation
- **Moral hazard**: the idea that removing consequences for mistakes encourages repeating them—applied selectively by elites
- **Dissociative personality disorder**: used here to describe the psychological profile (desperate, insecure, transgressive) that elite admissions selects for
- **Gini coefficient**: a measure of economic inequality
- **Endowment**: the accumulated investment wealth of a university
- **A&M (Agricultural & Mechanical)**: the original state school model for training tradespeople
- **ETS (Educational Testing Service)**: organization responsible for SAT, TOEFL, AP, GRE—founded by a Harvard dean
