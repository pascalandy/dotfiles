## The Iliad, Homer, and the Nature of Great Books

### Overview

This is a classroom lecture covering the first half of the Iliad. The instructor uses a student exercise about Achilles to build toward a larger argument: great books create characters so real they expand our consciousness. The lecture then explores how Homer achieved this, proposing a speculative theory rooted in universal consciousness and Jungian archetypes.

---

### The Achilles Exercise: Great Books as Mirrors

#### Main Idea

The instructor assigned three imaginative questions about Achilles to demonstrate how a great book excites the reader's imagination and reveals truths about the human heart. The exercise shows that fictional characters can become so real that readers use them to understand themselves.

#### Supporting Material: The Three Questions

**Question 1 — Imagine yourself as Achilles. How are you similar?**

A student described Achilles as "almost a mirror." The student loves soccer but one day felt humiliated, stopped playing, and it tore at their heart. The student's own phrase was "pride and vulnerability."

**Connection**: This reveals a key insight the Iliad offers — that arrogance and insecurity are two sides of the same coin. They are bundled together. Reading the Iliad gives tremendous insight into oneself and the nature of the human heart.

**Question 2 — Imagine Achilles' childhood.**

The student said Achilles was probably rebellious and very imaginative as a child — always testing boundaries, possessing a vivid imagination.

**Question 3 — Imagine Achilles today. What would he be doing?**

The student's answer: Achilles would probably try to be an Olympian athlete. The obvious answer would be soldier, since he loves war. But the student recognized that what Achilles really wants is to stand out, be admired, and be famous — which maps better onto an Olympic athlete than a modern soldier.

The student went further: being an Olympian athlete requires coaches, sponsors, regimented structure, and conformity. Achilles is too independent-minded. He would rebel against all that authority and structure, and so he would probably fail in today's society.

**Connection**: The depth of this answer shows that the student can imagine Achilles as a whole, living person — projecting him into a completely different world and reasoning about how his personality would interact with modern constraints.

#### The Point of the Exercise

> "What a great book does is excite your imagination. It helps your imagination peer deeply into your own human heart and see how complicated, how complex, how dark it is."

A great book is composed of characters that are real to you and make the world more real to you. The reader can:

- Imagine a character's past and childhood
- Experience what the character experienced
- Project the character into the present world as a living, breathing person

This leads to greater imagination, greater empathy, and greater curiosity.

### Harold Bloom: Great Books Help Us Become Human

#### Main Idea

The instructor introduces **Harold Bloom**, the most famous American literary critic, who taught at Yale. The instructor studied English literature at Yale and sat in on one of Bloom's classes. Bloom's definition of a great book: a great book helps us become human.

#### Supporting Material: "Hear Yourself Speak"

Bloom's key concept is that characters in great books are different because they are able to **hear themselves speak**. This means that as a person speaks, a part of them steps back and analyzes what they say — it has to make sense to themselves and to others.

**Connection**: This capacity is what we call **consciousness**. To be human means to be conscious, and consciousness is ultimately an act of imagination.

#### The Mechanism: Disassociation

The instructor introduces the concept of **disassociation** — as you speak and exist in the world, a part of you, "almost like a ghost," steps away and observes the entire scene. You are not only imagining yourself speaking, but also imagining what is happening inside the mind and heart of the person you're speaking to — your effect on them.

### The Agamemnon-Achilles Argument: Consciousness in Action

#### Main Idea

The argument between Agamemnon and Achilles in Book 1 of the Iliad demonstrates extraordinary levels of consciousness. Both characters operate on multiple levels simultaneously, and this multi-layered awareness is what makes them feel real.

#### Supporting Material: The War Council Scene

**The setup**: Agamemnon kidnapped a girl. Her father, a priest of Apollo, demanded ransom. Agamemnon refused. Apollo sent a plague. Greek soldiers are dying. Achilles tells Agamemnon he must return the girl. Agamemnon agrees but demands Achilles' girl in return. This starts the main conflict of the Iliad.

#### Agamemnon's Three-Layered Consciousness

When Agamemnon speaks in the war council, he is doing three things simultaneously:

1. **Responding to Achilles** — Achilles made him lose face, so he wants to make Achilles lose face in return
2. **Managing perception among the other generals** — If he simply concedes to Achilles, he loses respect. In this world, losing respect can get you killed. These people are gangsters. He must demand something from Achilles to demonstrate superiority
3. **Ensuring internal coherence** — He constructs a rationale for himself: "I love her. You're stealing from me what I love. I love her more than my own wife, Clytemnestra. Therefore, I must enact vengeance."

**Connection**: This triple-layered awareness — responding to the other, managing an audience, and maintaining internal logic — is what makes Agamemnon a real character.

#### Achilles' Matching Consciousness

Achilles responds with his own multi-layered speech:

- **Response to Agamemnon**: "I'm risking my life for you. You gain most of the treasure and profit from this war. I take a little bit, and now you're stealing that from me."
- **Appeal to the audience**: He's trying to win sympathy from the other generals
- **Identity assertion**: He is conscious of who he is and what he stands for

#### The Battle of Worldviews

> "Through their speeches, they're trying to control reality. Through their speeches, they're trying to impose their reality on others."

This is what makes the scene so emotionally violent. It is a **battle of worldviews** — a struggle for control over narrative:

- **Achilles' narrative**: He is doing everything he can to save the Greeks. He is the hero.
- **Agamemnon's narrative**: Achilles is a selfish narcissist who came to Troy only to win personal glory. He's using Agamemnon as a pretext.

Agamemnon escalates by drawing on his own memory and experience to attack Achilles directly:

> "Achilles, you say that you came here to fight for me, but we all know that you are vain. You're a narcissist. You're an asshole. You came here to win glory for yourself."

Achilles responds: "Fine. If you do that, then I won't fight for you anymore."

#### Supporting Material: Nestor and the Living World

The instructor emphasizes that it's not just the two main characters who are alive — **Nestor**, one of the older generals, enters the scene and tries to reconcile Agamemnon and Achilles. Every character in the Iliad — Odysseus, Hector, Priam, Nestor — is alive with real emotions, real feelings, and real experiences.

**Connection**: The test of whether characters are truly alive is coherence across time — we can look back at how the conflict came into being (the past), and we can make predictions about the future, and it all holds together.

#### Supporting Material: Suicidal Commitment to Narrative

The conflict escalates to a point that is almost suicidal. Hector and the Trojans are coming to burn down the Greek ships. If the ships burn, every Greek will be killed. Even at this existential moment, neither Agamemnon nor Achilles gives up their narrative.

When Odysseus comes to Achilles and begs him to return — offering all the money in the world, Agamemnon's daughter, and all of Troy after conquest — Achilles refuses:

> "No. I want Agamemnon to come and apologize. If he's not willing to come and apologize to me, then screw all of you."

**Connection**: This reveals how human and how real the characters are. Their commitment to their narrative overrides even self-preservation. The reader, seeing this, gains greater imagination, empathy, and curiosity — and the ability to step back and observe themselves and others.

#### The Civilizational Claim

> "That's the power of a great book. And this is how the Iliad created the greatest civilization on Earth in history, the Greek civilization."

### How Did Homer Do This? The Mystery of Creation

#### Main Idea

Homer is one of the greatest literary minds in history, but almost nothing is known about him. The Greeks called him "the teacher" and considered him the father of their civilization. Scholars have debated for centuries how one human mind could construct an entire universe of real characters. Some have speculated Homer was multiple people.

The instructor now speculates on the mechanism behind Homer's achievement.

---

### Identity, Memory, and Emotion: The Standard Model

#### Main Idea

The instructor outlines the conventional understanding of how personality and identity develop, then identifies three major problems with this model.

#### The Standard Process

1. We have **experiences**
2. Experiences become **short-term memory**
3. The brain filters memories into **emotions** — indexing experiences according to emotional strength or value (happy, sad, angry, etc.)
4. Memories that create emotions become part of your **identity**. Memories with no emotional charge are discarded
5. Different combinations of memories create **different identities** — your identity at school differs from your identity at home or in another country, because different identities are needed to navigate different landscapes
6. Together, these identities form your **worldview** — who you are, how you perceive the world, how you make decisions, navigate relationships, and behave

> "Your memories are your emotions."

#### Three Problems with This Model

**Problem 1 — The Sorting Mechanism**

Why do different people respond to the same experience with different emotions? One person gets 50% on a test and is devastated; another is delighted. Some people are glass-half-empty; others are glass-half-full. Where does this baseline personality come from?

The genetic answer ("your parents are optimistic, so you're optimistic") doesn't hold up. The instructor has three children, and all three are different from both parents. Personality doesn't simply come from parents.

> Where does personality come from? We don't know.

**Problem 2 — Memory Storage**

We know where breathing happens in the brain. We know where language acquisition happens. But we don't know where memories are formed, stored, and accessed in the brain.

> "That's really weird, guys."

**Problem 3 — Empathy / Theory of Mind**

The standard model doesn't adequately explain how we perceive the emotions of others. Saying "we have different identities and we reference our own" doesn't truly explain the mechanism of empathy.

### The Speculative Theory: Universal Consciousness and Archetypes

#### Main Idea

The instructor proposes a theory that the brain is not a storage facility but an **antenna for the vibrations of the universe**. Consciousness comes from our interactions with the universe, not solely from the brain. Memories are stored in the universal consciousness across infinite dimensions.

#### The Analogy: Computer and the Cloud

> "Think about the Internet. You have a laptop, and some memory is stored on the laptop, but most of it is actually stored in the cloud. By interacting with the Internet, your computer becomes more conscious."

Similarly: if you assume everything comes from the human brain alone, nothing makes sense. But if you assume the human brain is merely an antenna to a universal consciousness, it makes more sense.

#### Archetypes (Carl Jung)

Through imagination and consciousness, we implant ourselves into the universe. Different emotions implant themselves in different wavelengths and dimensions. The combination of your emotions creates a unique imprint in the universe.

**Archetypes** — a concept from **Carl Jung**, Swiss psychologist — are different personalities that access the same parts of the universe and therefore behave the same way. That's why certain types of people even look alike:

- Evil people have a certain look
- Good people have a certain look
- Clever people have a certain look

#### How Homer Did It

Homer opened his mind to the universe and was therefore able to access all archetypes. Everyone does this to some degree through empathy, but Homer was able to do it at a far greater level than anyone else. He transplanted these archetypes into the world of the Iliad, creating an entire universe of real people.

#### Supporting Material: The Iliad's Timelessness

The Iliad was written 2,500 years ago by a man living in the Mediterranean/Aegean, in a completely different culture and time. Yet it speaks directly to a Chinese student living in China in the 21st century. The student can imagine Achilles as themselves.

**Connection**: If the universe is conscious, infinite, and eternal — and archetypes exist within it that Homer could draw on — then the Iliad's timelessness makes sense. This consciousness is both spaceless and timeless.

> "How was he able to construct characters like Achilles, Odysseus, Agamemnon that are so real that they feel alive to us? That they're so real that we can hear them speak to themselves."

### Prophet, Poet, Teacher: The Same Function

#### Main Idea

In ancient Greek culture, the roles of prophet, poet, and teacher are the same function. All three access the truth of the universe and construct it in language that allows others to access this truth eternally.

#### What "Prophet" Really Means

A student asks about the word "prophet" — if the universe is timeless, prophets aren't really predicting the future. The instructor clarifies:

> "The word prophet doesn't actually mean someone who predicts the future. The word prophet actually means someone who speaks the truth."

Truth is the universe, which is God. The universe is past, present, and future altogether — beyond space and beyond time. When you speak truth, you speak what has happened, what is happening, and what will happen. The test of a prophet's words is whether they come to pass.

#### Supporting Material: Achilles and Moral Truth

Much of prophetic truth is **moral truth**: if you do evil onto others, evil will come onto you.

In the Iliad, Achilles is stuck — he wants Agamemnon to apologize so he can fight the Trojans. Agamemnon refuses. So Achilles effectively sacrifices **Patroclus** — it is the death of Patroclus that enables Achilles to rejoin the war. Because he has done evil unto Patroclus, evil will now come unto Achilles.

> "Truth is eternal. If you do evil onto others, then evil will have to come onto you. So it's both a prediction as well as a moral judgment."

#### Why Poetry

In an illiterate culture with no writing, Homer traveled to different places and told the story of the Iliad aloud. It was like music — beautiful, poetic. But it was beautiful **because** it was truthful. The listeners felt that Achilles was a real person with real emotions, and through Achilles they better understood themselves.

#### The Teacher Function

Understanding yourself through understanding the Iliad — the psychology, motivations, and emotions of its characters — gives birth to consciousness. Once you can hear yourself speak, once you can imagine with other people, that gives birth to civilization.

#### Supporting Material: Greek Civilization as Derivative of Homer

Every great thinker of Greek civilization — **Plato**, **Thucydides**, **Aeschylus** — is derivative of Homer. They all operate within Homer's universe, working off it but applying it differently.

**Thucydides**, for example, writes in a Homeric way — his characters are always giving speeches. The difference is that Thucydides writes about real people and real events, whereas Homer sings about characters of his imagination drawn from the Greek cultural landscape.

> "In literature, prediction and truth are the same thing. If you're gonna speak truth, you can predict the future. Because that's what truth is. Truth is eternal. Past, present, and future converge on truth."

### Key Takeaways

- A great book creates characters so real that readers can imagine their past, experience their present, and project them into different worlds
- Arrogance and insecurity are two sides of the same coin — a core insight the Iliad reveals about the human heart
- Consciousness is the ability to "hear yourself speak" — to step back and observe yourself, your words, and their effect on others
- The Agamemnon-Achilles conflict is fundamentally a battle over narrative control, not over a girl
- Both characters maintain their narratives even at the cost of self-destruction
- The conventional model of identity formation (experience → memory → emotion → identity) has three unresolved problems: the origin of personality, the location of memory storage in the brain, and the mechanism of empathy
- The instructor's speculative theory: the brain is an antenna to universal consciousness; Homer accessed archetypes within this consciousness to create his characters
- Prophet, poet, and teacher are the same function — accessing truth and constructing it in language that enables civilization
- The word "prophet" means one who speaks truth, not one who predicts the future
- Moral truth is both prediction and judgment: evil done to others returns to the doer

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Consciousness**: The act of stepping back from yourself to observe and analyze your own speech, actions, and their effects — ultimately an act of imagination
- **Disassociation**: The capacity to separate from your immediate experience so that a part of you observes the entire scene like a ghost
- **Archetypes** (Carl Jung): Universal personality patterns that exist within the collective or universal consciousness; different people who access the same parts of the universe behave and even look alike
- **Worldview**: The sum of your identities — who you are, how you perceive the world, and how you make decisions and navigate relationships
- **Theory of Mind**: The ability to perceive and understand the emotions and mental states of others _[acknowledged as insufficient by the instructor]_
