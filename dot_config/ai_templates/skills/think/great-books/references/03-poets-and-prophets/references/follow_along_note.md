## The Iliad, Poetry, and the Birth of Greek Civilization

### Overview

This is a lecture exploring how a single epic poem—the Iliad—gave birth to Greek civilization. The lecturer moves through Greek concepts of virtue and flourishing, the mechanics of speech-making as a form of warfare, Kant's epistemology applied to language, and Percy Bysshe Shelley's "A Defence of Poetry" to argue that poets are prophetic channels who create shared reality through language.

---

### The Central Question

The lecturer frames the entire session around one driving question: **How is it possible that one epic poem can give birth to a civilization?**

Greek civilization is presented as the greatest and most creative civilization in human history—the foundation of Western civilization. It produced Plato, Thucydides, Herodotus, Aeschylus, Euripides, and Sophocles. All of this, the lecturer argues, traces back to Homer's Iliad.

---

### Aretē and Eudaimonia: The Two Core Concepts of Greek Civilization

#### Aretē

**Aretē** means virtue, excellence, or character. It refers to what makes you special—what you excel at. The lecturer identifies two traditional types of aretē in Greek culture:

- **War fighting** — the paragon is **Achilles**
- **Speech making** — the paragon is **Odysseus**

These two figures represent the twin pillars of Greek excellence. The Iliad centers on Achilles; the Odyssey (which the class will read next) centers on Odysseus.

#### Eudaimonia

**Eudaimonia** means flourishing. The core idea: you can only be happy, only be truly yourself, when you are achieving and expressing your aretē.

#### Supporting Material: Achilles' Prophecy

Before coming to Troy, Achilles was given a prophecy—he could either die old at home or die young as a hero on the shores of Troy. His response was immediate and obvious to him: of course he would die young in Troy, because only through fighting and winning glory could he achieve eudaimonia.

**Connection**: This explains why Achilles is so miserable when he withdraws from battle after his quarrel with Agamemnon. Without fighting, he cannot be Achilles. He is cut off from his own identity.

> "I can only be happy when I am being my creative best. When I'm achieving my true potential."

---

### Speech Making as War Fighting

#### Main Idea

The lecturer argues that war fighting and speech making are fundamentally the same activity pursued through different means. Both attempt to **impose your reality onto the world and make others believe what you believe**.

- **War fighting** achieves this through force — by strength, you demonstrate superiority and compel obedience
- **Speech making** achieves this through words — through beauty and truth, you create a new reality that others submit to

#### Supporting Material: The Embassy to Achilles (Book 9)

The context: Achilles has withdrawn from the war. The Trojans, led by Hector, are destroying the Greeks. Agamemnon, Odysseus, Nestor, and others hold a war council and agree to beg Achilles to return. Odysseus delivers a long speech to Achilles.

The lecturer poses a key question: **Why are the speeches in the Iliad so long?**

Odysseus could simply say: "We'll give you a million dollars. Will you fight?" Or: "Stop being an asshole. Come fight." Achilles could simply respond: "No." But both deliver extensive speeches. The reason:

> "They're not trying to respond to each other. They're trying to create their own reality."

Speech making is described as **projecting a movie onto the world**—creating a new reality that others must inhabit.

#### Odysseus' Strategy: Expanding Achilles' Imagination

Odysseus knows that Achilles' refusal is really about **face**—he's lost face in front of others and won't back down until Agamemnon apologizes. But Odysseus also knows Agamemnon won't apologize. So Odysseus, as the great orator (his aretē), attempts to create a new emotional reality for Achilles by **expanding his imagination**. That's why the speech is so long.

Odysseus' technique moves through time:

1. **The present contrast** — Before them is a great feast. Odysseus asks Achilles to imagine the opposite: a desert where everyone is dying and starving. That's the war right now. That's why they need him.
2. **The present threat** — He paints Hector as a giant, a god, running around killing Greeks
3. **The past** — He invokes Achilles' father, Peleus, reminding Achilles he promised to win glory for his father and for the Greeks
4. **The future** — He paints the victory: all the riches of Troy, Agamemnon's daughter as a bride, and eternal glory

**Connection**: By moving through past, present, and future, Odysseus is trying to pull Achilles out of his narrow, self-absorbed reality and into a larger world where returning to battle makes emotional sense.

#### Achilles' Counter-Speech: Contracting Inward

Achilles refuses to be beaten. His speech counters Odysseus by contracting inward rather than expanding outward. Key differences:

- Achilles uses **"I, I, I"** and **"me, me, me"** — Odysseus uses **"we"**
- Achilles dismisses each of Odysseus' points personally: Hector ran from *me*; I should go home to see *my* father; I spit on Agamemnon's daughter

**Connection**: This is a **war of realities, a war of narratives**. Each speaker creates their own world through speech and tries to make the other inhabit it.

### The Techniques of Memorable Speech

#### Main Idea

The goal of speech making is not just to paint a reality but to make that reality **internalized** by others. To do that, it must be **memorable**. The lecturer connects this to the students' own experience—they were asked to memorize a speech and found they retained much of it without forced memorization. That demonstrates the power of speech.

What makes speech powerful? **Poetry.** The elements of poetry include:

- **Imagery** — Drawing pictures for the audience to see (Odysseus' specialty)
- **Metaphors/Connections** — Clarifying reality by showing things you couldn't see before. The lecturer offers an example: "The sky is like the sea" doesn't surprise you. "The sky is like a snail" surprises you, and because it surprises you, you remember it. When you remember it, it **reorders how you see reality**.
- **Diction** — Choice of words
- **Syntax** — Arrangement of language

#### The Greek Education System

The Greeks' education system was remarkably simple: **memorize the Iliad**. By memorizing the Iliad, you learned how to make a great speech—how to have impact, how to imprint yourself on others.

**Connection**: This system of speech making became the basis of Greek civilization. Everything got done by going before people and making a speech, creating a reality for people to accept. The lecturer states directly:

> "This is what leads to democracy."

### Kant and the Nature of Reality

The lecturer shifts to philosophy to explain the mechanism by which Homer creates civilization.

#### Immanuel Kant's Framework

Kant, in *The Critique of Pure Reason*, argues that we are not passive observers of reality—we are **active participants** who shape and form reality.

Kant divides the world into two:

- **Noumena** (things in themselves) — Objective reality, which is just vibrations, frequencies, pure energy. We cannot directly perceive these.
- **Phenomena** (things to us / things that seem to us) — The world as we filter and understand it

The filter we use is **time and space**, which do not exist outside of us but inside of us:

- **Time** = sequence (one, two, three, four, five—putting things in order)
- **Space** = sensation (the five senses)

The critical insight: **If we can control time and space, we can control reality itself.**

#### Language as the Controller of Time and Space

What controls time and space? **Language.**

Before language, each person perceived their own individual time and space. With language, people come to a **collective understanding of reality**.

Who creates language? **Poets create language.** Therefore, poets create reality itself through their poetry.

**Connection**: This is exactly what Odysseus is doing—using poetry to create a new shared reality in which people get along, in which Achilles can forget his hatred and fight for the common good.

> "Even though each of us has our own understanding of reality, a poet is able to create a language that is so beautiful that it comes into us and we internalize language, we absorb the language, and we together create a new reality with the language as the building blocks."

### Homer as Creator of Civilization

#### Poets as Prophets

The lecturer claims poets are really **prophets**. All great religious figures—Jesus, Zoroaster—were really poets. They work through a **divine connection to the universe**.

#### The Universe and Its Names

Different thinkers have named this universal force:

- **Hegel**: **Geist** — The lecturer breaks this German word into three English cognates:
  - **Ghost** — the underlying, invisible presence
  - **Gist** — the essence of things
  - **Geyser** — always erupting, always changing, always becoming
- **Carl Jung**: **Collective unconscious**
- **Plato**: **Realm of the Forms and Ideals**
- **Christianity**: **Heaven**

The universe is described as a **"divine psychic Internet"** where every memory is stored. Poets access this universe and summon its memories to create epic poetry.

#### The Iliad as Living Memory

Each character in the Iliad is presented as a real person with a living past, present, and future. Homer is able to create such vivid characters because he summons them from the universe—their consciousness is still alive there.

Because these people are living, **the Iliad itself is a living memory**. Homer, as a bard in a pre-literate culture, would go from town to town and recite his poetry to crowds, painting a movie for everyone to observe together—just as Odysseus does with his speeches.

The lecturer draws an analogy to a movie theater: the same movie plays, but everyone's experience is different. Each person implants their own consciousness into the Iliad, making it a **shared creation**.

#### The Iliad as Portal

The critical claim: the Iliad was created through inspiration from the universe, but it is also **a portal back into the universe**. By interpreting the Iliad through your own unique connection to the universe, you create a new universe unto itself. This process is what the lecturer calls **civilization**.

> "This is why one poem, the Iliad, was able to give birth to an entire civilization. Because it was so alive, so powerful, so connected to the universe itself, to the monad, that when you observed it, you created within yourself a portal into the monad."

The evidence this is true: even today, the Iliad speaks to us. We identify with Odysseus, understand Achilles, relate them to ourselves, and predict how they will behave. The Iliad is **eternal and immortal** because it connects us to the monad.

The more you read the Iliad, the more it opens your mind. The lecturer describes the mind as an **antenna to the universe**—reading the Iliad increases your "download speed," strengthening your connection and allowing you to absorb more and create new realities.

> "That's why we read the great books. Because it literally allows us to connect and talk to God itself."

### Percy Bysshe Shelley's "A Defence of Poetry"

The lecturer shifts to reading and interpreting passages from Shelley's essay to show how a Romantic poet articulated the same ideas.

#### Theater as the Expression of Poetry's Power

Shelley discusses how Homer's influence extended to the Athenian playwrights—**Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides**—and how theater became the essence of Athenian life. Theater was not mere entertainment but a vehicle for **education, enlightenment, and elevation**.

> "The tragedies of Athenian poets are as mirrors in which a spectator beholds himself under a thin disguise of circumstance."

The idea: poetry and drama serve as **mirrors**. You see yourself in Achilles, in Oedipus. By observing them objectively, you better understand yourself.

#### Epiphany and Catharsis

The lecturer identifies a common pattern in Greek tragedy:

1. A tragic character is undone by **hubris** (arrogance, the great tragic flaw)
2. Achilles has hubris. Agamemnon has hubris. Hector will die because of hubris.

> "No matter how great you are, you will suffer from hubris. The greater you are, the more you'll suffer from hubris, which can only lead to tragedy."

Two effects on the audience:

- **Epiphany** — You recognize that hubris leads to tragedy, which makes you a more humble person
- **Catharsis** — Regardless of epiphany, tragedy still falls. Hector still dies. Patroclus, however innocent, still dies. You feel connection, you cry, and this **purges** your feelings—your hubris, your hatred. This makes you a whole person.

Through catharsis, you connect with the character. The character lives in you, and you live in the character.

#### Supporting Material: Oedipus

The classic case is **Oedipus**, who killed his father and married his mother. When he discovers the truth, he blinds himself and goes into exile. The lecturer notes that if you read Sophocles' version carefully, Oedipus did nothing wrong—it was fate, accident. But that's what life is about.

**Connection**: Greatness means acknowledging your limitations and fate but struggling regardless. That is aretē. That is eudaimonia—recognizing the universe may have a grudge against you, but you struggle on.

> "That story, when you watch it yourself, it brings sorrow to you, it brings pity, but it makes you also much more wise and reflective about the world that we live in. And that creates wisdom, that creates empathy, that creates morality."

### Poetry as Gateway to the Divine

#### Poets Combine the Material and the Spiritual

Shelley argues that poets absorb both the divine (the monad, the Geist) and the material world (human nature, life itself). They combine these two together—**the present with the eternal, the here with the forever**—and turn them into words that express both dimensions.

#### Poetry Reawakens Memory

The lecturer introduces a concept of **reincarnation**: we incarnate into this world because there are experiences available here (sex, pain, love) that are impossible in the spiritual realm, where we are formless. But upon incarnation, our memories of former selves and the spiritual are lost. Poetry, because it connects both the divine and the present, can **spark your soul**—the forever memory of all your lives and your connection to the spiritual.

> "Poetry is a gateway into your soul."

Shelley's words:

> "Re-enaminate, in those who have ever experienced these emotions, the sleeping, the cold, the buried image of the past."

Poetry reignites past lives within you. It summons them up and they say: "We remember."

#### Poetry Defeats the Prison of Perception

Shelley writes that poetry "defeats the curse which binds us to be subjected to the accident of surrounding impressions." The lecturer interprets: before poetry, we are in a **prison of our own making**, seeing only what we can see through time and space. Poetry gives us **higher sight**—allowing us to see the eternal, the past, the future, beyond mere perception.

> "Wherever it spreads its own figured curtain or withdraws life's dark veil from before the scene of things, it equally creates for us a being within our being."

Each time we use our imagination, we create a new reality for ourselves.

#### The Poet as Creator

Shelley quotes Tasso:

> "None but God and the poet deserve the name of creator."

The poet sees beyond time and space, beyond this reality, and distills those visions into words that help others connect to the divine. Poetry is a **portal to the divine**.

---

### Poets as Prophets and Legislators

#### Main Idea

Shelley's conclusion, which the lecturer endorses fully:

> "Poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world."

The lecturer's interpretation: everything we know, everything we do, exists because of the language poets create. Poets are prophets whose words create our reality.

Key characteristics of poets as Shelley describes them:

- They don't know they are prophets. They create because they have no choice—there is a fire burning in them that must be released
- They are not fully human in the conventional sense—they are **messengers of the divine flame**
- When Homer speaks, he doesn't understand what he's speaking. He's channeling God—a portal for God to speak to us
- They are "mirrors of the gigantic shadows which futurity casts upon the present"—prophets because the future speaks through them today

> "Poets are the hierophants of an unapprehended inspiration."

**Connection to the central question**: Homer created civilization because God willed that Homer speak truth, and willed that this truth spread across the world through his poetry. It is all by design.

### Key Takeaways

- Greek civilization rests on two concepts: **aretē** (excellence/virtue) and **eudaimonia** (flourishing through expressing your excellence)
- Speech making and war fighting are the same activity through different means—both impose a new reality on others
- Long speeches in the Iliad are long because they are **wars of narrative**, each speaker constructing an entire world for the listener to inhabit
- Poetry is memorable because of imagery, metaphor, diction, and syntax—and the Greeks educated themselves by memorizing the Iliad
- Kant's epistemology suggests language controls time and space, and therefore poets who create language create reality
- The Iliad functions as a **living memory** and a **portal** into the universal consciousness, which is why one poem could birth an entire civilization
- Greek tragedy works through **epiphany** (recognizing hubris leads to downfall) and **catharsis** (emotional purging through shared suffering)
- Poetry connects the material and the spiritual, reawakens lost memory, and defeats the prison of limited perception
- Read the great books—they literally expand your capacity to perceive and create

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Aretē**: Virtue, excellence, or character—what makes you special, what you excel at
- **Eudaimonia**: Flourishing; happiness achieved only through expressing your aretē
- **Hubris**: Arrogance; the great tragic flaw that undoes even the greatest characters
- **Epiphany**: The recognition, through observing tragedy, of a fundamental truth (e.g., hubris leads to downfall)
- **Catharsis**: Emotional purging through pity and sorrow experienced during tragedy
- **Noumena**: Kant's term for things in themselves—objective reality as pure vibrations/energy
- **Phenomena**: Things as they appear to us, filtered through time and space
- **Geist**: Hegel's term for the universal spirit—ghost, gist, and geyser combined
- **Monad**: The universal consciousness; the divine psychic Internet where all memory is stored
