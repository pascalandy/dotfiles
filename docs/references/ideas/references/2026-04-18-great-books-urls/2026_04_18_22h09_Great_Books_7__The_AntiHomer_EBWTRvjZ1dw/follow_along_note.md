## The Aeneid as the Anti-Homer

### Overview

This is a university lecture examining Virgil's Aeneid as a deliberate political instrument designed by Augustus Caesar to corrupt and invert the moral teachings of Homer's Iliad and Odyssey. The lecturer argues that the Aeneid replaced Homeric values (love, forgiveness, generosity) with Roman values (piety, obedience, duty) and that this inversion shaped Western civilization through the Dark Ages until Dante's Divine Comedy offered a corrective.

---

### Homer as the Infrastructure of Greek Civilization

#### Main Idea

Homer's Iliad and Odyssey formed the mental infrastructure of Greek civilization. All educated Greeks memorized these works. At this point in history, Greek culture was oral—people spoke and listened before audiences rather than reading and writing. Homer became the foundation of their worldview, and this foundation produced what the lecturer calls "the greatest civilization in human history."

#### Key Greek Values

Two Greek concepts are introduced as central to understanding what Homer transmitted:

- **Arete**: excellence, being the best at what you can be
- **Eudaimonia**: flourishing, human happiness achieved only through fulfilling your arete

**Connection**: Odysseus exemplifies this framework. His arete is speech—he is a great speaker—and he uses that gift to fulfill his destiny and save his family, thereby achieving eudaimonia.

---

### Romans vs. Greeks: A Clash of Values

#### Main Idea

The Romans conquered the Greeks militarily but recognized that Greek culture was vastly superior. This created a fundamental tension: well-educated Romans began drifting toward Greek culture, which Roman leadership perceived as a corruption of the Roman soul.

#### Greek Values vs. Roman Values

| Greeks | Romans |
|--------|--------|
| Open, curious | Conservative, tradition-bound |
| Arete and eudaimonia | **Piety**: obedience to fathers, history, tradition |
| Theater, philosophy, rhetoric | Military prowess, war-making |
| Tragedy as communal catharsis | Gladiatorial combat, bloodlust |

#### Augustus Caesar's Problem

Augustus Caesar, the first emperor of Rome, recognized a long-term threat: even though Rome conquered Greece physically, Greece would conquer Rome spiritually. The main vehicle of this spiritual conquest was Homer.

> "Even though the Romans have conquered the Greeks physically, spiritually, the Greeks will conquer the Romans. And the main issue is Homer."

#### The Solution: Corrupt Homer

You can't burn books—there aren't many books, and people have memorized Homer. So the solution is not destruction but corruption. Augustus Caesar devised the Aeneid as the **anti-Homer**, an inversion of Homer's ideas. It became the bible of the Roman Empire. Every schoolboy had to memorize it as the primary method of learning Latin.

### The Aeneid's Grand Structure and Purpose

#### Main Idea

The Aeneid replicates both Homeric epics in a single work. It contains twenty-four books: the first twelve model the Odyssey, the last twelve model the Iliad. The lecturer frames the Aeneid as propaganda—deliberate, blatant in its purpose.

#### Authorship

**Virgil** is credited as the poet, but the lecturer argues Augustus Caesar was the true architect. Augustus provided the framework; Virgil composed it into Latin poetry. At the end of his life, Virgil wanted to burn the Aeneid—he feared that using his poetic gift in service of the emperor rather than the gods would bring divine punishment. Augustus refused to let him destroy it.

#### Historical Arc

The lecturer traces a sweeping trajectory:

- Homer → Greek civilization, birth of Western civilization
- The Aeneid → the Dark Ages, roughly a thousand years when Western creativity ends
- Dante's Divine Comedy → liberation of the human soul from the Aeneid's poison

### The Fall of Troy: Greek Culture as Trojan Horse

#### Main Idea

Aeneas, a Trojan survivor, tells Queen Dido of Carthage the story of Troy's fall. His purpose is to show how deceptive and manipulative the Greeks are, and to argue that the values of Homer—especially love—are evil and lead to tragedy.

#### Supporting Material: The Greek Prisoner and the Wooden Horse

The Greeks pretend to leave the beaches of Troy, leaving behind a wooden horse. The Trojans debate what to do and decide to destroy it. Then a Greek soldier appears—captured by shepherds—and tells a story about how the horse is a gift from the Greeks to the gods. Since it's a gift to the gods, destroying it would anger them.

**Connection**: The lecturer draws a direct allegorical line—the real Trojan horse is Greek culture itself. The Greek soldier is trained in theater, philosophy, and rhetoric, using all his craft to deceive the naive Trojans. The message to Romans: do not let Greek culture inside your walls, or it will corrupt and destroy you from within.

#### The Text: Theater as Deception

The passage read aloud describes the Greek prisoner performing grief and helplessness before the Trojans:

> "Now hear the treachery of the Greeks and learn from a single crime the nature of the beast."

> "Where can I find some refuge? Where on land? On sea? What's left for me now? A man of so much misery."

The lecturer explains that Greek theater was enormously popular in Rome around 30 BCE. The Aeneid reframes theater not as the paragon of Greek civilization but as a tool of deception.

> "His groans convince us, cutting all our show of violence short."

### The Death of Priam: Negating the Iliad's Moral Lesson

#### Main Idea

The Aeneid rewrites the ending of the Iliad—the scene where Priam and Achilles forgive each other—and inverts its moral. In the Iliad, Priam's generosity and capacity for forgiveness lead to one of literature's greatest moments of reconciliation. In the Aeneid, that same generosity leads directly to Priam's destruction.

#### Supporting Material: Priam's Death at Pyrrhus' Hands

Pyrrhus (also called Neoptolemus), son of Achilles, kills Priam's son Polites in front of his parents—a reimagining of Achilles killing Hector before Priam and Hecuba on the walls of Troy.

Priam confronts Pyrrhus with a direct appeal to the Iliad's moral legacy:

> "You say you're Achilles' son? You lie. Achilles never treated his enemy Priam so. No. He honored a suppliant's right. He blushed to betray my trust. He restored my Hector's bloodless corpse for burial, sent me safely home to the land I rule."

Pyrrhus responds by murdering Priam at the altar, dragging him through his son's blood.

> "A powerful trunk is lying on the shore. The head wrenched from the shoulders. A corpse without a name."

**Connection**: The Aeneid wants Roman readers to reconsider the Iliad's lesson. Instead of seeing Priam as heroic for forgiving Achilles, they should see him as a foolish old man who deserved to die for believing his enemy had goodness in him. The moral lesson of forgiveness is deliberately negated.

#### Romans and Violence

The lecturer contrasts Greek and Roman entertainment cultures:

- Greeks went to theater, watched tragedy, and cried together to rejuvenate their humanity
- Romans went to the Colosseum to watch gladiator fights—they liked blood and violence

The Aeneid is described as "almost pornographic in the violence that it depicts," and the Romans loved it precisely because they were, in the lecturer's words, "a bloodthirsty people."

### Helen and the Inversion of Love

#### Main Idea

The Aeneid's central philosophical inversion: where the Odyssey teaches that love is the redemptive force of the world, the Aeneid teaches that love is the source of evil.

#### Supporting Material: Aeneas Sees Helen

As Troy burns, Aeneas is paralyzed by rage and grief. Then he sees Helen hiding at an altar and blames her—and by extension, love itself—for the destruction of civilization.

> "Out it flared, the fire inside my soul, my rage ablaze to avenge our fallen country. Pay Helen back, crime for crime."

**Connection**: The Odyssey's main lesson is that love is the redemptive force—if you are shattered, love will lead you home and heal you. The Aeneid inverts this: love is what destroys civilization, corrupts you, and leads you to hell.

---

### The Death of Creusa: Wifely Duty as Self-Sacrifice

#### Main Idea

Aeneas escapes Troy carrying his father on his back and holding his young son Iulus. His wife Creusa follows behind. When he reaches the ship, she is missing. He goes back to find her dead—she killed herself.

#### Roman Family Hierarchy

The order in which Aeneas carries his family reveals Roman priorities:

1. **The patriarch (father)** — most important, carried on his back
2. **The son (heir)** — held by the hand
3. **The wife** — follows behind, her role is to serve

**Connection**: This is explicitly contrasted with the Odyssey, where Penelope and Odysseus are equals in their relationship.

#### Supporting Material: Creusa's Ghost

Creusa's ghost appears and explains her suicide. She killed herself because:

- It is her duty not to burden her husband
- He is destined for great things—founding an empire—and must marry into a new lineage
- If captured by the Greeks, her slavery would embarrass him

> "A long exile is your fate. The vast plains of the sea are yours to plow... great joy and the kingdom are yours to claim and the queen to make your wife. Dispel your tears for Creusa whom you loved."

The lecturer states bluntly: "That is the Roman perception of a wifely duty. If you're not useful to your husband, just kill yourself."

### Heaven as Piety, Not Love

#### Main Idea

If love is hell in the Aeneid, then heaven is **piety**—obedience to the prophecy of the gods. The will of the gods demands that Troy be destroyed so Rome can be created. The will of the gods demands that Aeneas lead the Trojans to found Rome. What is good, what is divine, is obedience to this prophecy.

> "A long exile is your fate. The king of lofty Olympus won't allow it."

**Connection**: In the Odyssey, the gods will that Penelope and Odysseus reunite—love is the divine force. In the Aeneid, the gods will that Creusa and Aeneas separate—duty and obedience are the divine forces.

---

### The Long Shadow of the Aeneid

#### Main Idea

The lecturer acknowledges the Aeneid may not sound influential from a modern perspective, but emphasizes that it became the basis of Roman civilization. Civilization is defined here as "a set of values, a set of ideas that guide you in your life." The Aeneid inverted Homer, and this inversion shaped Western thought through the entire medieval period.

The course will continue reading the Aeneid for two weeks, then move to Dante's Divine Comedy, which the lecturer frames as "the liberation of the human soul from the poison that is the Aeneid."

---

### Key Takeaways

- The Aeneid was a deliberate political project by Augustus Caesar to replace Greek values with Roman ones
- Its strategy was not to destroy Homer but to corrupt Homer by inverting his moral lessons
- Love, generosity, and forgiveness—celebrated in the Iliad and Odyssey—are recast as weaknesses that lead to destruction
- Piety (obedience to fathers, tradition, and divine prophecy) replaces love as the highest virtue
- The family hierarchy shifts from the Odyssey's model of partnership to a rigid patriarchal order
- Greek culture itself (theater, rhetoric, philosophy) is allegorized as a Trojan horse that corrupts from within
- Dante's Divine Comedy will later serve as the antidote to the Aeneid's inversion

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Arete**: excellence; being the best at what you can be
- **Eudaimonia**: flourishing; human happiness achieved through fulfilling one's arete
- **Piety** (Roman): obedience to one's fathers, to history, and to tradition
- **Anti-Homer**: the lecturer's framework for understanding the Aeneid as a systematic inversion of Homeric values
