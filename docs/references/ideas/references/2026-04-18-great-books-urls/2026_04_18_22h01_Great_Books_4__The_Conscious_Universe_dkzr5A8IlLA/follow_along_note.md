## The Iliad — Conclusion: Consciousness, Love, and the Universe

### Overview

This is the concluding lecture on Homer's Iliad, covering the psychological manipulation between Achilles and Patroclus, a model of multi-layered consciousness drawn from Kant and Hegel, the symbolic meaning of the Shield of Achilles, and the final confrontation between Priam and Achilles — argued here as the greatest battle in the poem. The lecture frames the Iliad as a tool for developing empathy and wisdom through forced perspective-switching.

---

### The Battle of Wills: Patroclus vs. Achilles

#### Main Idea

The lecture picks up from the core conflict structure of the Iliad — a war of wills. The original clash between Agamemnon and Achilles has led to catastrophe for the Greeks. Achilles refuses to fight unless Agamemnon apologizes. Agamemnon won't. The Greeks are stuck. So Achilles sends Patroclus to get the Greeks to beg more — but Nestor, seeing through the ploy, redirects: he tells Patroclus that maybe _he_ can fight in Achilles' place.

This creates a new battle of wills — between Patroclus and Achilles — because Patroclus has spent his whole life in Achilles' shadow and desperately wants to prove himself.

#### The Three Levels of Psychological Operation

The lecturer introduces a framework for reading the dialogue between Patroclus and Achilles. Each character operates simultaneously on three levels:

1. **The Conscious/Emotional Level — "The Actor"**: The surface performance. Patroclus appears before Achilles crying. Achilles responds with visible annoyance: "Why are you crying? This is not our war."

2. **The Calculating/Manipulative Level — "The Director"**: The tactical maneuvering. Patroclus wants Achilles to agree to let him join the battlefield. Achilles considers how he might trick Patroclus into serving his own interests.

3. **The Strategic/Planning Level — "The Producer" or "The Investor"**: The long-term play. Patroclus wants to win honor and prove himself Achilles' equal. Achilles wants to win all glory for himself.

**Connection**: The lecturer argues this three-level operation maps onto Freud's model — **id** (basic desire: glory, sex, money), **ego**, and **superego** — and that all humans operate this way simultaneously and subconsciously. We don't fully know why we do what we do.

### Achilles' Hidden Sabotage of Patroclus

#### Main Idea

Achilles agrees to let Patroclus go fight — but he adds something to his instructions that dooms Patroclus. On the surface, Achilles tells Patroclus to drive the Trojans from the ships and then come back. But embedded in the speech is a psychological trap.

#### Supporting Material: The Speech (Book 16)

> "Once you have whipped the enemy from the fleet, you must come back, Patroclus. Even if Zeus, the thundering lord of Hera, lets you seize your glory, you must not burn for war against these Trojans."

> "You will only make my glory that much less."

> "Oh, to God, father Zeus, Athena, and Lord Apollo. Not one of all these Trojans could flee his death. Not one. No Argive either. But we could stride from slaughter so we could bring Troy's hallowed crown of towers toppling down around us. You and I alone."

**Connection**: The lecturer's reading is precise and striking. Achilles is _not_ warning Patroclus about Hector — not once. Instead, he's implanting the idea that Zeus himself might anoint Patroclus the greatest warrior, that Patroclus could outshine Achilles, that Patroclus could win eternal glory. This inflates Patroclus with **hubris** — the very thing that will get him killed when he challenges Hector instead of retreating.

The lecturer argues Achilles understands — at a subconscious, strategic level — that a young, impetuous, jealous Patroclus, given this kind of encouragement, will overreach, get himself killed, and thereby give Achilles the perfect excuse to re-enter the battlefield and claim all the glory.

> "If he gets himself killed, good for me, Achilles. Because now I have the perfect excuse to join the battlefield and win all the glory for myself."

The lecturer emphasizes: no one sees this manipulation — not the Greeks, not the gods, not Patroclus, and not even Achilles himself. It operates below conscious awareness.

### How Do We Function at Multiple Levels? — Models of Consciousness

#### The Standard Model of Psychology

The lecturer outlines the conventional understanding:

1. We have **experiences**
2. Experiences become **memories** (which are essentially emotions)
3. Memories organize into **identities**
4. Multiple identities create a **worldview** (personality)
5. Personality determines preferences, decisions, and perception of the future

Three problems with this model:

- **Filtering**: People can share the same experience but perceive it differently. What is the filtering mechanism?
- **Malleability of memory**: Memories change over time. The same event can produce different emotional reactions at different life stages. (Example: a father accidentally hitting his child — perceived as lack of love when young, understood with sympathy when you become a parent yourself.)
- **Imagination**: Memories are finite, but imagination is infinite. How do we generate entire new worlds from limited material?

#### The Kantian Model

**Kant** proposes we are not passive observers absorbing experiences — we are **active participants creating reality**. Through space and time (influenced by language and media), we transform the **noumena** (things-in-themselves — vibrations, energy) into **phenomena** (things-as-they-appear-to-us).

Problems with this model:

- Where does our sense of space and time come from?
- What _is_ the noumena?
- If reality is subjective, how do we share the same reality?

#### Hegel's Solution: The Geist

**Hegel** introduces the **Geist** (spirit) — the force that gives us space and time. As we interact with the noumena, the noumena responds to us. This is why we can all perceive the same reality: we're all acting on the same force.

The lecturer uses a metaphor: think of the Internet. Whatever you produce is stored online, but you also have local memory on your hardware. Your memories become part of the Geist, and the Geist evolves as you evolve — dynamic, not static.

### The Greek Metaphor: Layers of Consciousness

#### Main Idea

The Greeks visualize consciousness as extending infinitely across dimensions. On one level, you talk to yourself. On another, you talk to one person. On another, you're connected to many. This goes on infinitely.

Memories get stored in the Geist — some permanently (the very powerful, very visual ones). These permanent memories give rise to new forms of consciousness:

| Layer | Examples | Characteristics |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **New Gods** | Zeus, Apollo, Aphrodite | Interfere directly in human events |
| **Old Gods** | Honor, Justice, Fate, Destiny | Older, stronger than new gods |
| **God itself** | Immutable laws of the universe | Like gravity — good, truth, beauty |

**Connection**: This explains how Patroclus and Achilles can operate at multiple levels simultaneously. If consciousness is the universe, and the universe contains different spirits and dimensions, then we literally contain multitudes. We are a **hologram of the universe** — a local replica of the whole, constantly connected to it.

> "Whatever we do here impacts the entire universe. One good act by yourself can impact the entire universe itself because the universe is conscious."

### The Shield of Achilles: The Soul as Universe

#### Main Idea

After Patroclus dies wearing Achilles' armor, Thetis commissions Hephaestus to forge a new shield. The shield contains living images — not pictures but movies, constantly in motion. The lecturer argues the shield represents **Achilles' soul** — his consciousness — which is a universe unto itself.

#### Supporting Material: The Shield's Imagery

> "And you forged a fallow field, broad rich plowland tilted for the third time. And across, crews of plowmen wheeled their teams, driving them up and back..."

> "And he forged a king's estate where harvesters labored, reaping the ripe grain, swinging their whetted scythes..."

The lecturer draws attention to the density of **verbs** — wheeled, driving, churning, reaping, swinging — emphasizing that everything is in motion. This is what memories are: images in motion, changing, reacting, imagining.

**Connection**: The memories depicted on the shield don't come solely from Achilles' personal experiences — they come from the universe itself. Our experiences are the interface that allows us to access the universal consciousness and absorb memories from elsewhere.

> "We are constantly living and dying. When we die, all we're doing is shedding our own bodies and assuming new bodies. But our memories stay with us. The poetry in us stays with us and is constantly being rewritten."

### Achilles After Killing Hector: Guilt and Madness

#### Main Idea

Achilles kills Hector and should be satisfied — he's proven himself the greatest warrior, secured eternal fame. Instead, he mutilates Hector's body, tying it to his chariot and dragging it around Troy. This horrifies everyone: Priam and Hecuba go insane with grief; Odysseus and Agamemnon are disgusted because Hector was a respected warrior who fought honorably and never cheated.

The lecturer notes Hector's final stand: when Achilles returned to battle, everyone else fled into Troy, but Hector stayed outside the gates to face consequences. The lecturer calls this **the greatest act of bravery in the Iliad** — Hector knew he would die and stood anyway.

After the mutilation, Achilles falls into depression. He can't sleep, can't eat, can't cry. The lecturer connects this to the consciousness model:

> "When you do evil, and he knows — he in his heart knows he's the one who got Patroclus killed, he manipulated Patroclus into his death — then you know because the universe knows, and this haunts you for the rest of your life. You cannot escape it. When you're evil, God doesn't have to punish you because you punish yourself with the memory of it. Your soul burns with regret and despair and guilt and shame."

### The Meeting of Priam and Achilles: The Universe Intervenes

#### Main Idea

The gods hold a council and decide to broker peace between Priam and Achilles. Hermes escorts Priam into the Greek camp. The lecturer argues this is **literal, not metaphorical** — it's how consciousness works.

Within the shared consciousness of the universe, Achilles' and Priam's thoughts eventually converge. Other consciousnesses (the gods — living memories stored in the Geist) see the conflict and engage in dialogue to resolve it. Subconsciously, both men know they need to meet.

The universe then creates reality around this agreement: guards look away as Priam passes, Greeks pretend not to see him. The lecturer connects this to the Chinese concept of the **Mandate of Heaven** — the will of the universe that must be obeyed for events to continue.

> "The universe has instructed them: Priam and Achilles must meet. Therefore, you must make it possible for them to meet."

### The Greatest Battle: Priam Before Achilles

#### Main Idea

The lecturer frames this as the greatest battle in the Iliad — not a battle of swords but of souls. Priam, whose sons were slaughtered by Achilles, kneels before his enemy and kisses the very hands that killed them.

#### Supporting Material: The Text (Book 24)

> "The majestic king of Troy slipped past the rest and kneeling down beside Achilles, clasped his knees and kissed his hands. Those terrible, man-killing hands that had slaughtered Priam's many sons in battle."

> "Awesome as when the grip of madness seizes one who murders a man in his own fatherland and flees abroad to foreign shores to a wealthy noble host, and a sense of marvel runs through all who see him. So Achilles marveled, beholding majestic Priam."

The effect: Achilles is **defeated** by this act. The greatest warrior who bows to no one is stunned, awed.

> "Greatness does not come from defeating your enemies. It comes from forgiving your enemies."

The lecturer argues this single act changes the universe forever — it reverberates across all consciousness.

#### Priam's Speech: The Power of Empathy Through Love

> "Remember your own father, great godlike Achilles, as old as I am, past the threshold of deadly old age..."

> "Fifty sons I had when the sons of Achaea came... but one, one was left me to guard my walls, my people, the one you killed the other day defending his fatherland, my Hector."

> "I have endured what no one on earth has ever done before. I put to my lips the hands of the man who killed my sons."

**Connection**: Priam is able to reach Achilles because **love activates imagination**. Priam's love for Hector allows him to imagine Achilles' love for his own father, Peleus. He finds the one point of connection in the universe between them.

The lecturer identifies two fundamental forces:

- **Love**: The unifying force of the universe — it brings everything together
- **Imagination**: The animating force of the universe — it makes the universe alive

> "Our purpose in this world is to love each other and then to activate our imagination to create the reality that we live in."

#### The Resolution: Achilles' Liberation

> "Taking the old man's hand, he gently moved him back. And overpowered by memory, both men gave way to grief. Priam wept freely for man-killing Hector, throbbing, crouching before Achilles' feet as Achilles wept himself. Now for his father, now for Patroclus once again, and their sobbing rose and fell throughout the house."

The lecturer explains: Achilles' core problem was that he couldn't cry — he couldn't access his guilt, couldn't understand what he'd done wrong. Priam's forgiveness liberates Achilles' soul from its imprisonment in evil, allowing it to reconnect with the universe and return to its "poetic self."

> "Poor man, how much you've borne. Pain to break the spirit. What daring brought you down to the ships all alone to face the glance of the man who killed your sons, so many fine brave boys. You have a heart of iron. Come, please sit down on this chair here."

Achilles becomes wiser, gentler, more generous. The lecturer calls this **the epiphany of Achilles**.

> "This is what life is. This is what it means to be human. To do battle with our own heart."

### The Ending: Andromache's Lament and the Power of Perspective

#### Main Idea

The Iliad ends not with Greek triumph but with the Trojan perspective. Priam brings Hector's body back to Troy. The city mourns. Andromache, Hector's wife, leads the lament.

> "Oh, my husband, cut off from life so young. You leave me a widow lost in the royal halls and the boy only a baby... I cannot think that he will ever come to manhood. Long before that, the city will be sacked, plundered top to bottom."

The lecturer emphasizes: Homer is a Greek, writing for Greeks who celebrated the victory over Troy. Yet he ends the poem from the enemy's perspective — a Trojan woman foreseeing the destruction of her city, the murder of her child, her own enslavement.

**Connection**: The lecturer calls this **the big bang of civilization**. Being forced to switch perspectives — to imagine yourself as the enemy — is described as a violent assault on your own consciousness, destroying your prejudices, beliefs, and values, and opening access to the entirety of the universe.

> "Only through trauma, only through pain, only through suffering can you access empathy and wisdom. And that's a great truth of the Iliad."

The lecturer draws a contemporary parallel to Gaza/Palestine, asking the audience to imagine the emotional impact of dreaming yourself into the perspective of those being attacked.

### Reincarnation, Empathy, and the Purpose of Great Books

#### Main Idea

The lecturer connects the Iliad's themes to Buddhist and Hindu concepts of **reincarnation** — the idea that we constantly incarnate into different roles (murderer, victim, human, plant, animal) until we achieve wisdom. The purpose of each life is to develop **empathy**, which leads to wisdom and enlightenment.

A great book functions as a shortcut through this process: it's a universe unto itself, allowing you to assume multiple lives simultaneously. In the Iliad, you are Agamemnon, then Achilles, then Hector, then Priam, then Andromache.

> "If you were to spend your entire life with the Iliad, I guarantee you, you'll come out a much more wise person who now has a universe in your soul, and that will make you invincible and eternal."

### Key Takeaways

- We operate on multiple psychological levels simultaneously — conscious, calculating, and strategic — and much of what drives us is invisible even to ourselves
- Achilles subconsciously manipulates Patroclus into overreaching by implanting hubris, not by warning him of real danger
- The universe, in this framework, is conscious and composed of living memories — our actions ripple through it permanently
- Love is the unifying force; imagination is the animating force — together they define human purpose
- Forgiveness is portrayed as greater than victory — Priam's act of kissing Achilles' hands is the poem's true climax
- The Iliad forces perspective-switching as a mechanism for developing empathy, which the lecturer considers the foundation of wisdom
- Great literature is a tool for accelerating the development of empathy by letting you live multiple lives at once

### Key Terms Introduced

- **Hubris**: Excessive pride or self-confidence, here implanted in Patroclus by Achilles' speech, leading to Patroclus' death
- **Geist**: Hegel's concept of a universal spirit that gives us space and time, evolves as we evolve, and stores collective memory
- **Noumena**: Kant's things-in-themselves — raw reality (vibrations, energy) before our minds process it into experience
- **Phenomena**: Things-as-they-appear-to-us — reality as shaped by our consciousness
- **Mandate of Heaven**: Chinese concept invoked to describe the universe's will directing events toward necessary outcomes
- **As above, so below**: Principle that individual actions mirror and affect the universal order
